PART ONE — THE FORECAST

1

Mart day started at ten to five, in the dark, with the weaners.

Griff had them penned from the night before, forty-one of them, eight-week-old blues off four sows, and the trick of loading weaners was the trick of everything with pigs, which was that you couldn't make a pig do anything but you could make a pig think of it first. You set the trailer ramp at the gate of the race, and you stood where a pig that didn't want to go up a ramp would want to go instead, and you filled that spot with your legs and your board, and you waited, and the boldest one — there was always a boldest one — put a foot on the tin to hear what it sounded like, and then it was done, the whole forty-one went up in a river of backs, and any man watching would have said it was easy, and it was, the way most things were easy that had thirty years in them.

Sal had the tea in the flask and the paperwork in the cab, movement licences done on the laptop the night before, and she stood in the yard light with her coat over her nightie to see him off, which she did not have to do and had done every mart morning of their married lives.

"Don't take less than the trade," she said, which was the joke; it was what her mother used to shout after her father.

"I'll give them away and buy you summat nice with it."

"You know Bob Walcott'll talk you into stopping for the store cattle."

"I'm not stopping for the store cattle."

"You'll stop for the café, then."

"I'm stopping for the café," Griff said. "I'm not made of wood." And the yard light went off behind him as he pulled out of the gate, which was Sal saving the electric, and the lane unrolled in the headlights with the drains lying flat and pale either side like something poured level, and it was eleven minutes to the mart if you caught nothing behind a beet lorry, and he caught nothing, and the weaners rode quiet, which weaners did once the trailer was moving; it was the stopping they had opinions about.

The mart at half five was lit up like a liner in the dark of the fen edge, and it smelled the way it had smelled his whole life, which was of diesel and disinfectant and straw and animals and bacon out of Rene's café, all of it knitted into one smell that was not, whatever anybody from off said, a bad smell; it was the smell of the trade, and the trade was the oldest thing in the town by eight hundred years, give or take the church. Griff backed onto the docks between Harold Fen's trailer and a wagon from Kirton, and Charlie on the docks read his licence and chalked his pens, and the weaners went down the alleys with a mart man ahead of them being somewhere a pig would rather go, and that was them, pens forty-six and -seven, out of his hands till the ring.

"Two pens of blues off Whitegate," Charlie said, writing. "They'll do. You're first after the coffee's kicked in, about half nine."

"Story of my life, Charlie."

"Rene's got them dirty sausages on."

"I know she has," Griff said. "I could smell them from home."

The café at six on a Thursday held the parliament of the trade, and it was a better parliament than the one on the telly by every measure Griff knew of, chiefly that everybody in it had done what they were talking about and had the fingers missing to prove it. He got his mug and his dirty sausages — Rene's term, official, chalked on the board, on account of the mustard being in the batter, and no man had ever been served them without being asked was he man enough — and he sat where he sat, with Harold Fen and the Kirton lot and old Stan Pickess who had sold his cows nine years back and still came every Thursday, in his mart coat, to drink tea and disapprove of prices, because the mart was where his people were and a man went where his people were.

The talk was feed, which was up; and lamb, which was down; and the weather, which was sitting out over the North Sea making its mind up; and the Marsh brothers' sheds, which had had the inspectors round again — "clean as a dairy, mind," Harold said, fair being fair, "you can say what you like about them sheds, and I do, but there's not a bird in there wants for owt on paper" — and somebody's son who'd gone viral, whatever that was when it was at home, filming himself dressed as a wolf in the beet, and the new medication records portal, which had been down since Friday, and would stay down, it was agreed, till the exact minute a man relied on it.

Nobody talked about the other thing. The other thing had been on the news for a year — the summits, the letters signed by five hundred professors, the American senator who'd cried on the television, the adverts in between it all still trying to sell you a car — and it had washed over the café the way the news always washed over the café: as weather happening to other people, in London and America and places with carpets, while the actual world, the world of feed and lamb and the medication portal, went on being run by them as ran it. Stan Pickess had watched one of the summits on his daughter's telly and delivered the café's entire settled position on artificial intelligence in one line the previous autumn — "I've heard cleverer things said in this room by men who couldn't spell" — and the matter was considered closed.

The ring at half nine was full round the rails, and Bob Walcott was up in the rostrum in the yellow waistcoat with his gavel and his gift.

Griff had known Walcott forty years and the gift had never once staled for him: the patter, the numbers rolling out of the man like water off a weir, and under the patter the actual work, which was that Bob Walcott knew every face at that rail, knew who was buying and who was pretending, knew who'd lost his missus in the spring and wanted the ring's eyes kept off him, knew to the pound what a pen would make before a gate opened, and lied about it upward with such love that the trade followed him there. Selling was the smallest part of it. What Walcott auctioned, Thursday on Thursday, was the town to itself.

"Now then, now then — pens forty-six and forty-seven, forty-one strong blues off Griff Dennett at Whitegate, and you all know what that means, they've been done right and they'll go on for anybody, look at the level of them —" the weaners pouring round the ring like one animal, the way stock did, "— and I'm not starting where you want me to start, I'm starting where they're worth, don't insult me, Maurice, it's not Christmas —" and the bids came off the rails in twitches and nose-touches that no camera would ever have caught, and the price climbed to fair, and past fair by a pound, and Walcott's gavel went down like a full stop, "— Dennett's the name, quality's the game, next!"

Sold. Forty-one animals he'd pulled into the world with his own hands on a cold night in March, gone in ninety seconds to a finisher from Sleaford, and that was right, that was the shape of it, that was Thursday. Griff stood at the rail while they ran out and did what he always did, which he had never told anybody about and never would: he counted them out. Not for the tally — Charlie had the tally. He counted them because you saw an animal out of your keeping the way you saw a guest out of your yard, with your eyes, all the way to the gate. His father had done it. He'd never asked whether his father's father had, but he'd have had money on it.

The cheque would come to less than the diesel and the licences and the feed said it should, because everything came to less than it should now; that was the arithmetic the whole rail stood inside of and nobody said out loud on a Thursday, the way you don't discuss the tide while you're swimming in it. The Spalding contract was the other half of Whitegate's sums — sixty finished pigs a month, collected, weighed, invoiced, no ring, no Walcott, no counting them out, just a wagon at seven on the second Tuesday and a remittance slip with a deduction on it called "market adjustment" that adjusted one way only. Griff hated the contract with a low, permanent, unexamined hate, the way you hate a good bridge with a toll on it, and he renewed it every year, because the mart alone wouldn't keep the farm, and the farm was not a business. The farm was what his family was instead of a surname.

He was loading the empty trailer when Walcott came across the dock in the yellow waistcoat with two teas, which meant Bob wanted something or had heard something; the tea tax, they called it.

"Store cattle Saturday week," Walcott said. "There's a dispersal on. Fen End's finishing up — the whole herd, gear and all. Third dispersal this year, Griff, and it's March." He blew on his tea. "I've sold this town's stock forty year, man and boy, and I'll tell you what I've never sold till lately: I never sold quits. Quits was what happened to other towns." He looked back at the ring shed, the noise of it, the lorries, the whole liner lit up and steaming in the March cold. "You'll keep them sows going a while yet, though, eh. Whitegate blues in my ring. Some things want to stay where they are."

"While there's a ring to run them in," Griff said, "there'll be Whitegate pigs in it."

"That's the stuff," said Walcott, and drank his tea, and went off to sell the town to itself till one o'clock; and neither man, being neither prophet nor senator nor professor, and having between them maybe eleven minutes' curiosity to spare in a working week for anything said in London — neither of them gave one thought that morning to the other thing, the weather sitting out over every sea at once, making up its mind; and there is no reason on earth they should have. The forecast said nothing. The forecast, for the whole of that spring, right up to the afternoon itself, said nothing at all.

Home by two, and the empty trailer swilled out, and the rounds. The rounds were the day's true spine and had been for thirty years: the paddocks walked, the arcs checked, the water looked at — you could read a pig herd's whole week in its water — and the feeders, and the fences, and the sows themselves, ninety of them out on the light land in their huts and wallows, coming to the quad not because it was feed time, it wasn't, but because coming across to Griff was a thing worth doing in the middle of an afternoon if you were a pig with time on your hands. He went among them the way he went among them. He knew every one. That was not sentiment; it was the trade; a stockman who didn't know his sows one from another at two hundred yards in poor light was not a stockman, he was a man with pigs. He knew their gaits and their grudges and their friendships — sows had friendships, thirty years had left him no room for doubt on it — and he knew Doris before she was out of her wallow and coming over: his best breeder, fifth parity, a long deep blue-and-white sow with one ragged ear and a way of leaning her whole three hundred and forty pounds against a man's legs, very gently, like a ship coming alongside, until the man scratched where she was owed.

"Get off, you great article," Griff said, scratching. "You'll have me in the muck."

Doris leaned. The March light lay flat on the fen the way it did, pewter-coloured, and the wind came off the sea over thirty miles of nothing and found him there among his pigs, one man in a brown coat in a brown field under a sky the size of the county, scratching a sow's back while she groaned like a door; and it wants recording, because of everything that came after, that Griff Dennett standing in that field that afternoon was not an unhappy man. Worried, yes — worried was the trade's resting state; the contract, the feed bill, the mart's thin Thursdays, his father's nights, Sal's hips, the whole slow arithmetic of a way of life subtracting itself — but not unhappy. He had what he'd have said a man wanted, if you could ever have got him to say such a thing, which you could not: work he was made for, a wife who put her coat over her nightie to see him off in the dark, a herd that came to him across a field with no food in his hands.

In eight weeks Doris's litter would be weaners, and in eight months they'd be bacon; and the two facts — that he loved her, and that — lived in him where they had always lived, in separate buildings, on the one farm, with thirty years of good fencing in between; and no man alive had ever once got Griff Dennett to walk the fence line between those two buildings, not the vegan lass with the camera at the mart that time, not the vicar at harvest festival with her sermon about the sparrows, not Michael home from Leeds with wine in him asking his one question that one Christmas; and it never once occurred to Griff, scratching the ragged ear in the pewter light, that inside three months something would ask him nothing at all, and take the fence down anyway, from the far side, without a word.

He did the last of the rounds. He shut the meal shed. He stood a minute at the gate the way he stood, looking back over the paddocks in the last of the light, the huts, the wallows, the risen shapes of his sows against the drain bank, and the wind turbines away north turning slow on the skyline like men swimming; and then he went in, and pulled his boots at the porch, and Sal had the wireless on in the kitchen, the shipping forecast murmuring its rosary to the whole dark coast — Humber, Thames, Dover, moderate or good — and tea was sausages, their own, from the pig they killed each year for the house, which was a thing the trade did and did not discuss with strangers; and they ate at the kitchen table under the photograph of Ern at nineteen, upright as a hinge, holding two Shires by the head-collars outside a mart that no longer existed, in a country that didn't either, and had not known it then; and nothing whatsoever happened, that night or for eleven weeks of nights after, except the world going on exactly as it always had, which is the thing, of everything the world does, that it does best, and does right up until the minute it stops.

2

The Reverend Meg Ashby buried Tom Sowerby on the last Friday in March, and it went the way the old farmers' funerals went, which was: full church, no tears to speak of, and afterwards more genuine religious feeling in the village hall over the ham than at any point in the liturgy.

She didn't mind that. She'd learned not to mind it about eleven years ago, somewhere between her second parish and her third, when it had finally gone in — not as doctrine, doctrine had never been her trouble, but as fact — that the ham was liturgy, in a place like this; that a hundred and forty people standing round trestle tables saying he were straight as a die, old Tom, and did you ever see him beat at dominoes, were performing the office of the dead as their fathers had received it; and that her part was the twenty minutes of the other thing, the formal thing, the thing with the language in it, which they wanted done and done properly — full weight, no guitar, none of your happy-clappy, Reverend — precisely so that they could then go and do the real rite with the ham in good conscience.

"Lovely service," they said to her at the hall door, one after another, shaking her hand, meaning it. "Lovely service." Weight received, box ticked, grief now transferable to the dominoes stories. Tom's son stood by the urns with his collar too tight, being told what a man his father was by everyone his father had ever sold a beast to, and Meg watched the room do its work on him — watched him get, hour by ham-sandwich hour, visibly less alone — and thought, as she'd thought a thousand times: I could not design this better. Nobody could. It only looks like sandwiches.

Cyril had struggled with the Crimond.

That was the other thing about Tom Sowerby's funeral, filed where Meg filed the things she was not yet ready to act on: the Twenty-third, Crimond setting, which Cyril Meece had played at every funeral in that church for fifty-one years and could have played dead drunk in oven gloves, and there had been a moment in the second verse — nothing, a hesitation, a chord that arrived a beat late and slightly wrong and then corrected — and she had glanced across from her stall and seen Cyril's face, and Cyril's face had been the face of a man to whom something unthinkable was being said in a language he was refusing to learn. His hands. She'd watched them at the console afterwards, gathering his music: the left one doing its new trick, the slight settling tremor, like a bird deciding whether to land. Eighty-three. Fifty-one years on that stool. She had said, "Thank you, Cyril, lovely," and Cyril had said, "second verse got away from me a touch, the action's heavy in the damp," and they had both attended carefully to the lie, and put a dust sheet over it, and left it in the vestry with the others.

Saturday was the roof.

The roof fund stood at £11,340 of a needed sixty, after four years, two grant rejections, a flower festival, nine coffee mornings, a sponsored silence (Bernard Lofts, churchwarden, sponsored per hour, a fundraising concept so successful the parish had twice asked could it be made permanent), and the Gift Day. The architect's report used the phrase "significant water ingress at the north clerestory," and the bucket under the significant water ingress was blue, and lived behind the organ, and had a name — Cyril had named it Rebecca, after a diocesan officer he declined to elaborate on — and on wet Sundays the whole congregation could hear Rebecca receiving her portion, pock — pock — pock, all through the intercessions, patient as anything.

Meg spent the Saturday morning on the fund's spreadsheet at the vicarage kitchen table with the third mug of tea and the good biro, and the arithmetic did what the arithmetic did — you have asked everyone who can give, twice, and the town has given, twice, and the town is not the size of the roof — and she caught herself, somewhere in the fourth column, doing the thing she did, which was praying at a spreadsheet. Not hands together; nothing anyone watching would have caught. Just the interior door swinging open mid-task, habit-hung, and the sentence going out of her like a note left on a table: it's the roof again; I know You know; if the Hendersons' barn conversion money is meant to come our way I could do with it before the clerestory comes in on the choir. Businesslike. Her prayers had gone businesslike somewhere in her forties and she'd stopped apologizing for it: Roger, who'd been a churchgoing atheist of the most restful kind, used to say her praying sounded like a woman ringing a supplier she'd used for years — no pleasantries, mutual respect, straight to the order. He'd meant it fondly, and she'd kept it, the way she'd kept his reading glasses in the fruit bowl: too useful to bury.

Whether anything came back down the line was a question she'd stopped auditing. Things came back. Peace came back, sometimes; stamina, mostly; twice in her life, something she'd have gone to the stake calling an answer. The Hendersons' barn money, historically, had gone to the Hendersons' hot tub. You didn't keep books on it. That was the whole discipline of the thing, the discipline nobody outside ever credited: you did not keep books on God. She'd have liked to have told the professors that, the ones on the radio with their new-made minds and their probability of catastrophe — that there was one corner of England already long practised in trusting an intelligence it could not inspect, and the practice was called the parish, and it ran on tea; but nobody asked the clergy anything these days except at weddings, and then only where to stand.

Sunday's congregation was nineteen, which was par for Lent.

She preached on the wilderness — forty days, the stones and the pinnacle, the devil's three offers, each one a good thing got the wrong way, which she'd always thought the point everyone missed: bread's not wicked, rescue's not wicked, government's not wicked; the temptation is the shortcut, the good thing without the cost of it — and she preached it well, she could hear when it was landing, and it landed on nineteen faces of whom four were asleep in a warm patch and one was six and drawing a horse on the pew sheet; and afterwards at the door the nineteen filed past, and Elsie Cattermole said, "Lovely service," and Bernard Lofts said the collection was £43.17 and a Polish coin, and that the boiler was making the noise again; and that was the church in Aldenby in the spring of that year — nineteen souls, a blue bucket called Rebecca, a boiler with a noise, and a woman at the door in a white robe shaking hands, sixty thousand pounds short and forty years deep, keeping a two-hundred-year lease on a promise nobody could inspect.

She did her rounds Mondays and Wednesdays, which was not required of her and was the whole job.

The list lived on the passenger seat: the housebound, the lonely, the lapsed-who'd-mind-if-she-stopped, the new baby out at Fenholt, the Willows on alternate weeks. Mrs Dagnall, ninety-one, who wanted the psalm and the racing results in that order. Ted Rimmer, who'd lost his wife and his licence in the same winter and wanted arguing with about the war, any war, he wasn't fussy. The Prews on Chapel Lane — Donna Prew was Wednesdays, when Vince's van was gone to Boston.

She'd never once decided that consciously, the Wednesday timing. She examined it sometimes, driving away: there was nothing to it, nothing you could take to anybody — Vince Prew was a big amiable man who did guttering and fascias and had once carried the font lid single-handed when the flower ladies wanted it moved, and everyone said what everyone said, which was that he thought the world of Donna. It was only that Donna in company had a way of checking a room before she said things, a fast little sweep of the eyes like a woman glancing at a wing mirror; and that her laugh in her own kitchen on a Wednesday was a different instrument entirely from her laugh at the WI; and that once, two winters back, she'd worn a cardigan through the whole of a heatwave visit with her tea going untouched; and Meg had been a parish priest for twenty-nine years, and there was a shelf in her for such things, and on the shelf they stayed. You could not act on a cardigan. She'd asked once, carefully, the standing question the training gave you — everything all right at home, Donna? — and watched the shutter come down sweet and total, everything's grand, he's run off his feet with work, more tea? — and that was the shelf shut, and both of them had attended to it, and put the dust sheet over it, the way the parish put dust sheets over things, the vestry being full of them.

On the Wednesday of that particular week Donna had the radio on when Meg came — the local station, the one that did the gold hour and the swap shop — and they drank their tea to it companionably, and Donna talked about her sister in Louth and the caravan they might get to at Whitsun if Vince's work eased, and her eyes did the wing-mirror thing twice at the sound of a diesel slowing in the lane, twice relaxing when it went by; and the radio played the swap shop, a man with a rotavator seeking a mig welder, and the two women sat in the warm kitchen inside the ordinary unbearable arrangement of things, drinking tea; and if you had told either of them that the radio on the windowsill — that specific radio, the greasy-buttoned Roberts by the geranium — had eleven weeks left of being only a radio, they'd have laughed; Donna first, checking the room.

Home, the vicarage, the boiler's noise. Meg made her tea for one with the efficiency of six years' practice, and ate it with the crossword, and did the day's last office at the kitchen table as the light went — not Compline out of the book, though she said Compline's spine from memory; just the note left on the table, the day handed back, businesslike: Tom Sowerby, into Your keeping, though I expect he's arguing the toss about the hymns. Cyril's hands. Donna. The boy who drew the horse. The roof. And whatever it is that's coming, she added, surprising herself — she stopped, biro in hand over the pew-sheet rota, unsure where the clause had come from; some sediment of the radio, the professors, the letters, the weather over every sea; some smell on the spring — and whatever it is that's coming, she finished, because a prayer once begun was an order once placed, into Your keeping also, the way the rest of it goes, and us with it. Amen. She washed her one plate. She left the porch light on, out of a habit that had outlived its reason for eighteen years, its reason being that Roger had liked to see it from the school lane; and the vicarage stood lit-porched and lampless-windowed at the edge of the churchyard all night, between the dead of the parish and the sleeping of it, the way it had stood for two hundred years; and eleven weeks went by.

The war was mostly staying awake.

Nobody told you that beforehand and everybody told you afterwards, which was no use. Lew had been on the lines seven months and could have written the pamphlet himself now: the war is a cold hole you have improved with a roof of sleepers and a stove made of a paint tin, and staying awake in it, four on, four off, listening. The listening was the job. The sky did its business at all hours, most of it small, most of it theirs or ours going somewhere else, and you learned the sounds the way you learned birds as a kid, if you'd been the kind of kid who learned birds: the fat lazy one that didn't concern you, the sewing-machine one that did, the silence that concerned you most of all, because the new ones were quiet and the first you knew was the last you knew. That was the phrase. The lads used it like a skipping rhyme. First you know's the last you know.

March on the lines was thaw, which was worse than freeze. Freeze held things: the mud, the smells, the dead ground. Thaw let everything go again, and the trench walls wept, and the sump pump — British Army, one, pump, trench, hand-operated — became the platoon's whole spiritual life. Brigg ran a rota for it. Brigg ran a rota for everything: the pump, the stag, the stove, the charging bank, the one good spade, feet. Feet twice daily, socks rotated, and Brigg inspecting them like a man judging marrows, because — he said it maybe once a fortnight, flat, to whichever new lad had arrived believing the war was about courage — "wars are lost by feet, Garton's socks are worth more than your opinions, wring them out."

Sergeant Brigg was forty-one and made of the Army the way a flint's made of the field. Nineteen years in, three tours before this one, a wife in Catterick he spoke to on Sundays with his back to everyone and his voice going gentle for exactly eleven minutes. On the lines he was the most believable thing there was. He never raised his voice, because a raised voice, he said, was information; he knew the sky's birds better than the drone operators did; and he had a way, when the sewing-machine sound came over and every man's body wanted to be smaller, of standing in the trench with his brew going, looking up like a man assessing whether to bring the washing in, that was worth — Lew had thought about this a lot, on stag, in the wet dark; there was time — that was worth more than the artillery. Courage came off him the way heat came off the paint-tin stove, and you warmed your own at it and were not ashamed, because that was what it was for. "The Army's a machine for making men brave who aren't," Brigg said, another of his flat fortnightly ones. "Nowt wrong with that. Bravery's like anything else. Nobody makes their own boots either."

Dan had been dead five months, and the letter still wasn't written.

It lived in the top flap of Lew's daysack in a waterproof pouch with his ID and his morphine card: one sheet of bluey, folded, blank except for the date he'd written in the corner on the fourth attempt, which was itself five months old now, so even the date was wrong, and every week or so, on the quiet afternoons, he got it out and looked at it, and the blank sheet looked back at him with Kayleigh's face on, and he put it away again. Dear Kayleigh. That far was easy. The next part had defeated him thirty times: everything true was unsayable and everything sayable was on a card the welfare people had already sent her printed. He was with his mates. It was quick. He was thinking of you. Two of those three were even true. Dan had been forty metres away when the quiet one came in, doing nothing wrong, doing in fact the pump rota, Brigg's rota, taking his turn like everyone; and the distance between "killed in action" and "killed doing his turn on a hand pump in a hole in a field" was the exact width of everything Lew couldn't write, because Kayleigh would want it to have meant something, and Lew was the only one who could tell her what it had actually been like, and what it had actually been like was the pump.

They'd grown up four doors apart on the Eastfield estate in Aldenby, him and Dan Prescott: same school, same bus, same Saturday jobs at the mart hosing the alleys — Dan had a way with the beasts, the drovers said, should've gone stockman — same recruiting office in Lincoln on the same wet Tuesday, because the town had two trades left for a lad without letters, wind farms or the Army, and the Army had a better video. Eight years, boy and man, of the same billets and the same jokes. Dan was going to get out at the twelve-year point and go home and marry Kayleigh and get a dog; he'd named the dog already, the dog he didn't have — Bess; a lurcher, he was very specific, off no evidence — and the plan had been the plan the way Brigg's rotas were rotas, a thing you leaned on because it held. Lew had no plan. Lew's plan had been, privately, unexaminedly, Dan: stay near the plan, help build the kennel, be best man, be Uncle Lew. You could soldier a long time on the side of someone else's plan. Five months now he'd been finding out what soldiering was without one, and the answer was: staying awake. The war was mostly staying awake.

The near one came on the Thursday of that week, in the grey of the morning stand-to.

The sound was the wrong sound — that was all the warning was, a texture in the sky like a zip being undone somewhere above the mist — and then the whole eastern sky went the colour of a welding arc, twice, and the crump arrived through the ground before the air, the way the big ones did, and the trench did its drill: down, count, call. It had come in on the reserve line, six hundred metres back, where B Company's echelon was — where the brew tent was, where forty men had been queuing with mess tins at exactly that hour every morning of the winter — and for about ninety seconds, sprinting the communication trench with his heart in his teeth and Brigg's voice flat on the net behind him, Lew knew, with the whole of his body, the size of what had happened; knew names; was already drafting, God help him, the letters that would need —

— and it had landed in the old beet field east of the echelon, all three of them, the whole salvo, six hundred metres of miss, in the mud, among last year's rotted beet clamps, and killed one goat that nobody could account for the presence of, and that was all. The brew queue hadn't even fully dispersed. Some of B Company were laughing before the mud finished coming down, the high skidding laughter of men being given their lives back at speed, and by dinner it was already a story with a shape — the beet barrage, the goat, forty lads saved by bad batteries or wet propellant or some conscript on the far side mis-keying a grid, some clerical error in the machinery of death, some nothing — and Brigg stood at the parapet that evening with his brew, looking east at the settling light over the wrecked field, and said, not really to Lew, in the voice he used for the true things, flat as a nail head:

"That's the war, that. Right there. Everybody thinks it's a marksman." He drank. "It's a drunk with a shotgun in the dark. Fella it hits, fella it misses — " he tipped the mug an inch, east, a libation. "Same fella. Same dark." And he went to check the pump rota, because the pump didn't care, and the thaw was still the thaw.

Lew did his stag that night, four on, four off, watching the mist move over the dead ground through the intensifier's green porridge, and what he thought about — because you thought on stag; that was the other thing nobody told you; the war was a machine for thinking in holes — what he thought about was six hundred metres. Dan's one had missed by nothing; the brew queue's had missed by six hundred; and somewhere in the ledger where those numbers were kept, if they were kept, there was either a reason or there wasn't, and both answers were unbearable, so you didn't audit that account; you learned very young out here not to audit that account; you kept the feet dry and the letter unwritten and the sky listened-to, and you stayed awake, and the war went on being what it was, which was the weather with a grudge, the drunk in the dark, the account nobody audited —

— for eleven more weeks; though no one on either side of the wire, nor in any capital, nor Brigg himself, who knew everything the ground could tell a man, had the least idea of that; the sky that spring told nobody anything. It just got slowly kinder about the mornings, the way it did every year regardless, out of old habit, the world's oldest standing order: more light, then more light again, and the larks came up out of no-man's-land straight up singing like the mud was a meadow, which, underneath everything, it had been all along, and would be again before the beet was lifted, though not one man on the lines would have believed you; and Lew watched the larks go up through the green porridge of the scope and thought about a lurcher called Bess that didn't exist, and stayed awake.

The second Tuesday came round the way it did, and the wagon from Spalding was in the yard at seven, and Griff had the sixty finished pigs penned and ready in the collecting yard off the big shed, because whatever he thought of the contract, no wagon ever waited at Whitegate.

The driver was the usual one, Mal, a decent hand who'd been on livestock twenty years and did the job the way it should be done — no sticks, no hurry, gates set right, the trailer's internal ramps down before a pig ever saw them — and the two of them worked the pens together in the seven o'clock cold with the ease of men who'd done it a hundred times, moving quiet, standing where a pig that didn't want to go would want to go instead. The pigs went up in threes and fours. They were good pigs, level as a hedge, ninety-two kilos give or take, five and a half months old, and they went up the ramp interested — that was the word, and Griff had never in thirty years found a way round the word, so he'd stopped looking for one — interested, ears up, snouts working the new smells of the trailer, lorry diesel and strange straw and the pigs from the farm at Kirton already aboard, because everything was interesting if you were a well-done-to pig; the trade's whole quiet boast was in that walk, stress being bad for meat and worse for conscience, and both facts being true at once, and neither caring about the other.

"Two short at Kirton again," Mal said, doing his sheet on the wing of the cab. "He's winding down, I reckon. That's you, sixty. Levellest pen I'll lift this month, tha knows that."

"They've had nowt but right," Griff said, which was Whitegate's whole catechism in five words.

"They have that." Mal clipped his board, and paused, one boot on the step, and looked back at the shed the way drivers sometimes did, men who spent their working lives in the exact seam of the thing. "Funny old job, this. My lass asked me at Christmas what I did, for her school thing. Wrote down 'drives animals about.'" He got in. "Kindest man I know's a stockman, and I've met the other sort an' all, and the wagon's the same wagon." He said it without weight, a man passing the time of a Tuesday, and started the engine, and the wagon went out of the yard with sixty of Griff's pigs riding interested to Spalding, and Griff stood in the empty collecting yard with the gates open the way he stood every second Tuesday, for a minute, doing nothing that showed; and then he swilled the yard down, because a swilled yard was the difference between a farm and a place where things happened, and went in for his breakfast.

He'd been through the plant at Spalding twice in his life. Once at nineteen, when his father had sent him — sent him, no discussion, the way Ern sent people — with the words "tha'll not sell what tha's never looked at"; and once eleven years ago when the contract started and the field officer did the tour, hairnets and white coats and the visitor line painted on the floor. It was not what the leaflets the vegan lass gave out at the mart said it was, and it was not what the industry's own glossy said it was either; it was a place where a hard thing had been engineered to happen very fast to a great many animals by men and women who mostly did it carefully because it went quicker that way and the line paid by throughput. The pigs came up the race in the same interested walk, and the gas or the tongs came, current and instant, and the thing that had been a pig became with no seam at all a carcass, at a rate of some hundreds an hour, hour on hour, day on day; and the noise of it was less than the mart's and the smell of it was cleaner than a hospital's, and Griff had stood at nineteen behind the painted line watching pigs he did not know go through it level and quick, and had done the thing his father had in fact sent him there to learn to do, though Ern would never have had the words and would have clouted anybody who offered them: he had looked at it, all of it, straight, without flinching and without romance, and then he had built, quietly, over the following weeks, in the yard and the paddocks of home, the wall. On the one side of the wall he put the plant, the wagon, the word finished, the remittance slip, the trade's whole necessary end; and on the other side he put his sows, their gaits and grudges and friendships, the scratching, the counting-out; and he mortared the wall with the only mortar there was, which was that it had always been thus, that his father had a wall and his father's father, that every eater of bacon in England had one, thinner and cheaper than his and built by strangers; and on that wall, on the home side, whitewashed, in the trade's own hand, was written: they have nowt but right, and one bad minute at the end that they never see coming. And you could live behind that wall. Thirty years he'd lived behind it, well, usefully, in the company of animals he was good to and good for; and if, twice a year maybe — a certain sow's eye at loading, a certain silence in a swilled yard of a Tuesday — something looked at him over the top of it, he did what the trade had always done, which was to go and be busy at the troughs until it got down again.

The Willows smelled of dinner at eleven in the morning, which was its own small institutional wrongness, and Ern was in his chair by the window with the racing on mute and the Sporting Life his grandson sent from Leeds folded to the form, unmarked, because his hands wouldn't do the little tidy marks any more and he refused to let anyone else make them for him. Kettle marks or none, was Ern's position. A man's form book was his form book.

"Now then, Dad."

"Now then." Ern's eyes came over from the window, took Griff in, did the thing they still did, which was a full stockman's read, head to foot, condition-scored his own son in one pass. "Tha's not sleeping. And tha's been to Spalding this morning — nay, the wagon has, it's second Tuesday. Sit thee down. They've had the chiropodist round; I've had me feet done by a lass from Latvia; grand lass; I told her, tha's wasted on feet."

He was eighty-nine and the twentieth century sat in him like rings in oak. He'd been horseman here — not at Whitegate; at the big house's farms, before the war and the tractors — second horseman at fourteen, head horseman at twenty-two, master of a stable of eight Shires whose names he could still say like a psalm, Prince, Boxer, Diamond, Captain, Blossom, Short, Bonny, and Duke; and then the tractors had come in over about four springs, and the horses had gone — Griff knew where the horses had gone; Ern had never once said where the horses had gone, in seventy years, which was itself the saying of it — and Ern Dennett at twenty-six had walked off the big house's land with his brasses in a sack and put the whole of it, the names, the feathered feet, the four-in-the-morning stable candle, away behind a wall of his own building; and had got on, because getting on was the trade beneath all the trades; and had bought, by 1971, with his own saved wage, the raggedy forty acres that became Whitegate, and gone into pigs, because pigs were what a man could start with nothing; and had been, in Griff's whole memory of him, a man you could no more imagine weeping than you could imagine the church tower doing it — right up until Griff's mother's funeral, where he hadn't either; and then had wept, once, terribly, to everyone's horror including his own, three years ago, in this chair, at a television programme about the last working Shires in England; and had pressed the remote into the carer's hand afterwards and said, "put me summat else on, duck," and never watched it again.

They did the visit the way they did it. Racing, mute, discussed anyway. The form book's opinions, delivered by Ern from memory. The mart — Ern took the mart report like a man taking bloods, Thursday by Thursday: who was selling, who was finishing up, what the blues had made. Fen End's dispersal he already knew about, the Willows' intelligence network being the equal of Peg — of anyone's; and he received Griff's account of it with the long fen silence that was Ern's editorial column, and said at the end of it only: "Third this year. And it's but March." And then, apropos the window, the fen light, nothing: "Land dun't care, mind. That's the one mercy in it. Land's had us all on short leases since Domesday. Man gets to feeling like the landlord, wi' his name on the gate and his father's name and his — " the hand moved, taking in Whitegate, the county, the century — "we're tenants, boy. Allus was. Tenant leaves, land stops noticing him before his boots is cold. I've seen it ower and ower. It's nobbut the tenants ever grieves."

"Right cheerful this morning, Dad."

"I'm eighty-nine," Ern said. "Cheerful's for them wi' time to waste on it," and his eyes went back to the muted racing, pleased with himself, the county's driest old man in his chair in the sun; and it was only at the leaving, at the door, with the visit done and the form book restored to the sill, that the other thing put its head up, the thing the Willows staff had flagged to Sal on the phone and Sal had flagged to Griff over the washing-up, gently, twice: Griff said, "I'll get over Friday if the muck-cart holds off," and Ern said, "Aye," and then, not looking at him, in exactly the voice he'd have used about a gate hinge, "Fetch us summat from home, if tha thinks on. Nights is long in here. They do what they can, and what they can's a tablet at eight and a tablet at two if tha rings the buzzer and can bide till they come — there's a lad three doors down, old soldier, rings his buzzer of a night; I hear him; buzzer, then biding, then the trolley wheels, then quiet; tha can time it; forty minutes, some nights, start to finish." A pause, the racing flickering mute. "Nowt to be done. Lass says their hands is tied on the doses till the doctor's been, and doctor's Thursdays. It's nobbut the nights, boy. Days I'm grand. Fetch us the little wireless out the workshop, the one wi' the leather strap. A man can bide owt wi' summat to listen to."

And Griff drove home with both hands steady on the wheel through the flat pewter morning, past Fen End's emptying yards, past the Marsh sheds long and white and humming behind their poplars, past the church where they'd bury his father sooner than either of them said aloud; and at home he went into the workshop and found the little wireless with the leather strap, Ern's ancient Roberts, took it apart on the bench, cleaned the contacts, soldered the aerial where it had gone at the root, fitted new batteries and a spare set taped to the back with the date written on the tape; and if his eyes went wrong once while he did it, there was nobody in the workshop but him and the vice, and the vice said nowt; and Friday he carried it in to the Willows and set it on his father's sill, tuned to the local, the gold hour, the swap shop; and thereby, knowing nothing, meaning only the one small mercy a son could actually deliver against the nights — the doses being rationed, the doctor being Thursdays, the buzzer being forty minutes, the whole crooked before-arithmetic of old men's pain in a country that had costed it and filed it — Griff Dennett put into his father's room, onto his father's sill, in working order, with spare batteries taped to the back, the thing that eleven weeks later would speak.

The Parochial Church Council met on the second Tuesday of April at 7:30 in the church hall, under the wall clock that had run eight minutes fast since the Coronation — the first one — and which nobody would adjust, adjustment having been proposed in 1997 and defeated.

Item one was the show.

The Aldenby Show was the second Saturday of August, on the Glebe field, as it had been since before anyone's grandfather, and it belonged to the whole town — the Show Committee ran it — but the church held the field, the church did the teas, and the church, critically, owned the tent. The tent was forty foot of white marquee bought by public subscription in 1987, stored eleven months of the year in Bernard Lofts's barn, and it was to the parish roughly what the ravens were to the Tower. The minutes of item one, as taken by Elsie Cattermole, read, in full: "Tent inspected 4th inst. Some mildew, west gable. Discussion." What the word "Discussion" held, as the wall clock ate forty of the meeting's minutes, was: whether the mildew was the fault of the barn (Bernard, wounded, cited forty years of dry storage and his father's memory); whether the tent could be proofed or must be patched (two quotes, one from a man in Boston nobody trusted on principle, Boston being Boston); whether the Show Committee, having used the tent, having benefited from the tent, might for once in its high-handed existence contribute to the tent — this from Mrs Herring, chair of the flower rota, in whom the church militant survived undiluted — and whether, this being England, it would rain.

"It's the church's tent," said Mrs Herring, summing for the prosecution, "and the church's field, and if Maurice Tilney thinks he can park his beer wagon where he parked it last year, over my cousin's grave—"

"It's not over the grave, Freda, it's alongside—"

"—then the Show Committee can find sixty pounds towards the gable, is all I'm saying, or his beer wagon can stand in the road."

Meg chaired it the way you chaired the PCC, which was the way Walcott ran the ring, though she'd never have made the comparison out loud: you let the town sell itself to itself, you kept the bidding kind, and you brought the gavel down while everyone could still get home for the news. Tent: patch, Boston man, sixty pounds to be "explored" with the Show Committee, exploration delegated to Bernard, who would return in May having not explored it. Item two, roof: fund at £11,340, no change; Rebecca the bucket emptied fortnightly; noted. Item three, boiler: the noise. Item four, any other business: Mrs Herring, on the state of the churchyard yews; Elsie, on whether the new people at the old police house might like a welcome card; and Bernard, at three minutes to nine, producing from his inside pocket, folded, with the air of a man tabling a grenade, the letter from the Diocese.

"'Ministry in the Age of Thinking Machines,'" Bernard read, gravely. "'A training day for clergy and lay officers. Lincoln, 14th May. Buffet lunch provided.'" He looked over his glasses. "There's a speaker coming from London. It says here we're to consider — " he consulted it — "'the pastoral implications of artificial intelligence.'"

"What's it mean by that?" said Elsie.

"It means," said Mrs Herring, who had a niece in IT and was therefore the council's standing authority, "the computers. Like the checkout, but more so. Our Becky says in ten years they'll do everything. She says they've got them now can write a sermon."

There was a silence while the council, to a member, carefully did not look at Meg.

"I've seen the ones that write sermons," Meg said. "You're all quite safe. It's homework with a dog collar on." Laughter, the meeting's first; the clock stood at nine oh-two, which was six minutes to nine in real money; grace was said; stacking commenced. And it was in the stacking — chairs, urn, the biscuit tin's ritual interment in the cupboard Mrs Herring padlocked against the Scouts — that Elsie Cattermole, tea towel in hand, said the thing the whole council would have denied thinking, in the tone of a woman asking about a rumoured bypass: "They'll not ever really do everything, though. Will they. There'd be nowt left." And nobody answered, because the urn was draining, and it was late, and the question had been asked of the wrong parliament by eight minutes' worth of the wrong clock; and they all went home to the news, which that night, as it happened, had a summit on it, men at podiums in another country, and not one member of the PCC of St Botolph's watched to the end of the item.

Choir practice was Thursdays, such as the choir was: seven voices, average age heading north of Tom Sowerby, and Cyril at the organ, and it was at April's last practice that Cyril made his move.

"I've been thinking," he said, not turning round from the console, shuffling the Whitsun music with elaborate unconcern, "we might trim the anthem. Do the first four pages and go straight to the Amen. Keeps it fresh. Congregation doesn't want all six, whatever Stanford thought; Stanford never had to catch a Sunday dinner."

The choir, who wanted an early night, agreed with enthusiasm. Meg, sat three pews back with the notices she was pretending to draft, watched the page order being quietly re-engineered around two pages of semiquavers that a left hand with a bird in it could no longer be trusted with, and said nothing, and admired it, actually — the workmanship of the retreat, the way the old man was giving ground fastness by fastness, burning what he couldn't hold, keeping the flag flying over what remained; she'd buried enough of his generation to know the strategy by heart; they all did it, the men especially, they managed their own diminishments like estates. And the alternative — her saying, gently, Cyril, is it your hands — would take the estate off him at a stroke and put him on the parish's books as a pity, and he knew she knew, and she knew he knew she knew, and between them, in the loft of St Botolph's, the two of them kept the dust sheet spread over it with the tenderness of undertakers, which — she thought, driving home — was probably wrong, was possibly failure, was conceivably the exact opposite of everything pastoral care was for; and was also mercy, of the local kind, the kind actually available on a Thursday; and she added it to the pile of things she'd get wrong until something forced the issue, the pile every working priest kept in the vestry of themselves, under sheets.

The Willows was Wednesdays, alternate weeks, and she did it as she always did it: Mrs Dagnall first — psalm, then racing results, the order immutable; the old lady followed the jumps with a shrewdness undimmed at ninety-one and held strong views on the whip rule — then the lounge, the scattered fastnesses of armchairs, the offering of herself pew by pew to whoever wanted a word or a hand held or an argument.

Ern Dennett wanted the argument. He'd taken against a hymn at Tom Sowerby's funeral — she'd not known he'd been listening; the Willows had run the service through to the lounge on the loop system — and he prosecuted the case with relish: "'All is safely gathered in.' At a funeral. Tha wants to look sharp what tha sows, vicar, wi' a room full of us lot listening. Safely gathered in. We know where the barn is, from in here." But his eyes were going to the window while he sparred, and on the sill was a little old wireless with a leather strap, new-mended, and when she admired it the old man's whole weather changed, the dry front passing, something behind it she'd have called, in anyone less fortified, tenderness: "Our Griff fettled it. Nights, tha knows." And that was all he said, and it was enough; she'd sat with enough of the Willows' nights — she knew what the nights were, in there, the buzzer and the biding; she'd raised it with the manager twice and been shown, kindly, the staffing rota and the medication policy and the budget, the whole before-arithmetic, everyone's hands tied by everyone else's hands in the great national cat's-cradle — and so she only said the true thing, "your Griff does a job right," and Ern said, "he does that. He counts them out, that lad. Allus did," and she didn't know what it meant and understood it perfectly, the way you did with the old ones.

Down the corridor, three doors past Ern, the door with POOL, A. on the card was ajar as she left, and she put her head in as she'd learned to — Arthur Pool got no visitors; there was a regimental Christmas card on his sill in April and nothing else — and the old soldier was sat up straight as a bolt watching the racing with the sound off, and received her with the courtesy of his kind and generation, and declined, as always, all offers — prayer, communion, a chat, the paper fetching — "very good of you, padre, I'm well found" — and then, as always, as she was going, deployed the one thing he did want, fired low and fast so it could be disowned as a joke: "You might tell whoever runs the wireless, padre, that a man could stand the two o'clock tablet coming at four if the World Service hadn't gone to rubbish." Which was Arthur Pool's entire disclosure, the whole of the nights, handed over folded inside a complaint about the schedules; and Meg said she'd raise it at the highest level, and he said, "mind you do," and turned back to the muted horses, sat to attention in an armchair, eighty-eight, well found; and she went out past the medication trolley doing its rounds like a slow tide, and drove home along the drain with the poplars flickering the low sun at her, morse from nowhere, saying nothing.

May came in. The beet was through, the verges went white with cow parsley, the show schedule was printed (Class 14, Best Pig, Any Breed, entry £1), the Boston man patched the tent gable and charged forty-five pounds and was retrospectively trusted, the diocesan training day was attended by Bernard, who reported that the buffet was adequate and the speaker from London had said the future was "unknowable but full of grace," which Bernard had written down in order to disapprove of it at leisure; and the swifts came back to the church tower the same week they always did, hurling themselves round the bells in the long evenings, screaming the news of the year to a town that knew how to read it — that the summer now stood open, the fen's one abundance, light on light on light until nine, until ten — and no other news reached Aldenby that month that anybody kept. Six weeks, as it turned out. But the swifts didn't say, and the wireless didn't say, and the sky, enormous, unclouded, gave the fen its oldest forecast, the only one it ever issued with confidence: more of the same, world without end. It was wrong by six weeks, and not one soul under it knew.

The signal came to the lines twice a day, when the masts were up, in windows the size of a smoke break, and the platoon queued for it at the welfare shelter the way men had queued at every pay table and mail call since armies began: by an order fixed long ago on grounds nobody remembered, policed absolutely, and waived without a word for any man whose window mattered that day more than yours.

Lew's Sunday call was his mother at half four her time, in the school kitchen's off-season quiet if it was term, at the kitchen table at home if it wasn't, and the call ran eleven minutes because everyone's ran eleven minutes; you watched Brigg with his back to the trench wall and his voice gone gentle and you learned the custom of the country: eleven minutes was what a man could spend in the other world and still climb soundly back into this one.

Christine Garton gave the news the way the women of the Eastfield had given news to absent men for four generations, which was in a fixed order of decreasing safety, so that the call could be ended at any point with nothing dangerous left hanging. School first: the new dinner system, a scandal of trays. Town: Fen End had finished up, the mart was thin, old Tom Sowerby had been buried out at the church — "big do, they said. All the farms." Family: your nan's knee. Weather. And then, in the slot where the call was already winding down, delivered in the exact voice she'd have used for the nan's knee, the thing:

"Oh, and I'm having a bit of a test Tuesday week. At Lincoln. Nothing — they found a little summat, at the screening, you know how they are now, they photograph everything and then they want another photograph. Mrs Pyke ran me — I'm not to drive after, it's nothing, they said nine in ten of these are nothing. Any road. Is it warm yet, where you are? You said it were thawing."

The welfare shelter had a smell, wet canvas and instant coffee, and Lew stood in it holding the phone with his thumb whitening on the case, four thousand miles of nothing between him and the kitchen table, and did what the custom of the country required, because the order of the news was a discipline for him as much as her: he took the thing at the volume it was offered. "Nine in ten's good odds, Mam." — "That's what I said." — "You'll tell me though. Whatever it comes back." — "Course I will. It'll be nowt. It's warm here, the daffs are over." Eleven minutes. He climbed back into the war with the tenth of the odds riding pillion, where it stayed, on stag, in the green porridge, doing its sums in the dark alongside the other unaudited accounts; and what a man felt, four thousand miles the wrong way from a Tuesday week at Lincoln, was the thing the recruiting video had not covered and every soldier since Troy could have recited: you are here to stand between your home and harm, and harm has gone round the back while you stood here, the way harm does, harm being local, harm being domestic, harm keeping school hours; and there is nothing whatever you can do from a hole in a foreign field except queue on Sunday for eleven minutes and take the news in the order it is given.

Kayleigh messaged on the Tuesdays. That had settled into itself over the five months without either of them arranging it: Dan's girl and Dan's mate, the two holders of the largest shares, conducting the estate's business in texts because texts could be drafted and voices couldn't. Mostly it was maintenance. She sent him things the way you'd lay flowers — a photo of Dan's mam's birthday do, the cake with the football shirt on it; the news that Eastfield's five-a-side was naming the summer cup after him; once, with no comment, a picture of a lurcher pup somebody in Boston was selling, which Lew had looked at for a long time in the intensifier light and not known how to answer and answered with a thumb-up, the coward's hieroglyph, and hated himself for a week.

That May it was the headstone. The temporary marker was to come out and the stone was ready to set, and the mason wanted the final wording signed off, and Dan's mam couldn't choose and had deferred to Kayleigh, and Kayleigh — at ten past nine on a Tuesday night, her time — deferred to Lew:

mason says we can have one more line under his name and dates. mam liked AT REST. i liked ALWAYS IN OUR HEARTS but its 50p a letter lol which dan would have SOMETHING to say about. you knew him longest. what would he want. be honest

Lew sat on an ammo box in the last of the light with the phone in both hands and was, for a quarter of an hour, honest. What Dan would want. Dan would want to be twenty-five and getting out at the twelve-year point. Dan would want the dog. Dan would have stood in that cemetery reading AT REST off his own stone and said rest, I were on the pump rota, there's no rest in this outfit — and Lew got as far as typing he'd want summat about and stopped, thumb over the keys, because the something was a lurcher called Bess that existed nowhere but in the plan, and the plan was dead, and you could not carve a dog that never lived on a stone above a lad who never got her, it would mean nothing to anyone alive but two people and would cost them both the rest of their lives every time they read it; and he deleted it letter by letter, and wrote instead, in the language of the country he actually lived in:

in our hearts is right. dan never rested in his life he'd think AT REST was a dig about his stag rotas. tell your mason. and tell his mam its right

ha. thats him. thankyou lew, and then, twenty minutes later, when he was already back at the stove, the second message, the one she'd have drafted and deleted her own fifteen minutes over: it should be you saying it at the stone setting not the padre. when your home. no rush. it keeps.

It keeps. He put the phone away and did his four hours in the hole in the dark, and the letter in the daysack flap stayed blank; but something had shifted a degree in the estate's affairs all the same, because it should be you was an appointment, and an appointment was a plan, and a plan — one plan, small, borrowed, forty words at a graveside sometime after a tour that had three months left to run — was one plan more than Lew Garton had owned since November.

Brigg did the why of it maybe twice a tour, unprompted, always at the same hour: the dead hour before stand-to, when the dark got cold and doubtful and the new lads' faces needed somewhere to put themselves.

"Nobody defends a line," he said, that last time, the mug going round his hands. "Look at it. Mud and a drain. You could buy the field for nowt — they did buy it for nowt, that's why we're stood in it. You defend what's stood behind the line." He tipped his head back, over his shoulder, at the whole dark country to the west of them. "Forty mile back there's a town like your town. Bus station. School. Lasses doing their nails of a Friday. Old lads playing dominoes in the one decent pub. They've never heard of this field and they never will, and that's the job — that's the whole job, lads — them never having to hear of it. You're not stood in a field. You're stood between two kinds of world, and while you're stood here, the kind behind you gets to stay ordinary. Ordinary's the product. Ordinary is what we make." He drank. "There's no medal for it, because you can't pin owt on a town that never noticed it were safe. But that's the trade. Kings and causes come and go and the trade stays the trade: somebody stands where it's bad, so it stays good where it's good." He'd looked round the bay then, at the boys' faces coming grey out of the dark one by one as the east got on with it, and finished the sermon the way he always finished it, flat, no comfort in it, the nail going in true: "It's a good trade. It's the second oldest there is. And it don't work part time — so: stand-to, feet at eight, Garton's on the pump."

And the lines stood to, and the larks went up, and the sun came up over the drunk in the dark and the drains and the two ordinary countries facing each other over the mud; and of everything that was said on the lines that spring, it was Brigg's sermon Lew would carry home whole — the creed of the standing trade, every word of it decent, every word of it true, a cathedral built stone by stone over nineteen years.

Five weeks.

Gilt day was the last Saturday in May, and it was the day Griff liked best in the pig year and had never once said so, gilt day being, if you looked at it straight, which he did not, the day the farm played God with a marking stick.

Doris's spring litter had come up to twelve weeks as level and strong as everything that sow threw, eleven of them out on the nursery paddock, and the herd wanted two replacement gilts this year, Old Nancy being due her pension — sows at Whitegate got a pension; it was not discussed off the farm; a sow that had bred ten litters and knew the routines was kept on as what Griff's books called "herd matriarch, non-productive" and what Sal's books, the real ones, called Nancy — and so on the last Saturday in May, Griff went out to the nursery paddock with his stick and his eye, and Sal came with the flask, because gilt day was a two-Dennett job and always had been.

The eye was thirty years deep and worked faster than any words that could be put to it, but words could be put, and Griff put them, low, half to Sal and half to the line of his own thinking, while the litter rootled and barged about their legs: that one's long but she's light in the ham; that one's a madam, see her at the trough, she'd bully; teats even on that one, fourteen and true; feet, always feet first, a sow's a building stood on four corners; that one — the one with her mother's ragged-edged ear, the ear come down the line like a hallmark — that one, look at the walk on her, straight as a die and nosy with it, she'd farrow in a gale and count her own litter after. He watched them an hour. He handled the four on the short list, hands under, along, down the legs, the gilts leaning into it the way Whitegate pigs leaned, being pigs to whom hands had only ever meant good; and at the end he marked two backs with the blue stick, one slash each, unhurried: the ragged-eared one, and the long quiet one with the true feet.

"Names, then," Sal said, pouring the tea.

"That's thy department."

"Ragged Ear's Vera." Sal did not deliberate; Sal had had them named for a week. "After me Auntie Vera, that had the same ear off a dog in 1968 and outlived everybody that laughed. And the quiet one's June, for the month, because I'm running low on aunties."

And that was Vera and June entered into the life of the farm, names on backs, futures altered by a blue slash: they would stay, breed, grow old at Whitegate, know the quad and the scratching and, in time, the pension; and their nine siblings, unnamed, numbered, every bit as warm-breathed and nosy and particular, would go on to Spalding in the autumn with the rest; and the instrument of the whole sorting was a man in a brown coat with a marking stick and an eye, who did it the way it had always been done, by the lights of a trade older than the county's churches; and if you had asked Griff Dennett — nobody asked; Sal least of all; the flask was the liturgy and you didn't preach at it — if you had asked him how a man carried that, the blue slash and the nine, he'd have looked at you the way he'd looked at the vegan lass at the mart, not unkind, a man being asked to explain load-bearing walls to somebody who lived in a tent, and said what the trade had said for ten generations: they're all well kept, and they're none of them wasted, and tha eats, dun't tha. And the wall would have held, because it was a good wall; it had held ten generations; and the blue stick went back in his pocket, and the tea went round, and Vera-the-gilt got her first scratch under her new name, leaning in, all unknowing, elected.

The feed invoice came the following Tuesday and was up four percent on March, which was up nine on the year; and the contract letter came Thursday.

Sal read letters like a magistrate, at the kitchen table, in her office glasses, one finger travelling; and Griff stood at the window with his tea while she did it, because the letter had the processor's crest on it, and letters with the crest never brought the price up.

"Market adjustment's going to two-and-a-half percent," Sal said, finger stopping. "'Reflecting continued softness in the category.' And — wait on." The finger went back up and travelled again, slower. "New clause. 'Welfare-verified supply premium.' They'll pay eight pence a kilo over — listen to this — 'for supply verified under the enhanced outdoor standard, subject to audit'... which is thee, Griff, that's Whitegate stood in a letter, that's what tha's done all thy life — " her finger came down flat on the page — "and it's worth eight pence now, sithee, because some supermarket's had a documentary made about it. Thirty year they've docked us for doing it right, and now right's a premium. I could spit."

"Tek the eight pence."

"Oh, I'm tekking it. I'll audit them an audit like they've never — I'll have that inspector seeing paperwork in his sleep." She took her glasses off and rubbed the bridge of her nose, and the kitchen was quiet a minute, the wireless murmuring the forecast, the good dry spell holding; and then she said, not looking at him, in the voice she kept for the yearly saying of it, the treaty voice: "It's four year since we broke even proper, love. I do the books. The eight pence is a sticking plaster on a — we're keeping ninety sows and two pensions and thy father's wireless batteries on my egg money and thy stubbornness."

"And the Standing—" he stopped; wrong word; there was no such word yet; "— and the single farm payment, and the barn rent off Tilney. We're right."

"We're nearly right," Sal said, "which is farming," and put her glasses back on, and filed the letter under C for Contract in the drawer where thirty years of the crest's letters stood in date order like a core sample of the whole slow squeeze; and that was the treaty renewed for another year, the annual exchange, her saying the true number and him saying we're right and both of them keeping the farm between them like a beast they were nursing through something, because the alternative — the alternative had a name in that kitchen, and the name rang every Sunday at six.

Michael rang Sundays at six, regular as the angelus, and was a good son, which was the difficult part.

He rang from Leeds, from the house with the car charger and the little garden trampoline, having done his own Sunday duties, the run to the tip, the batch cooking — he told them about the batch cooking; Griff received the concept of batch cooking annually like news from a distant star — and he asked after the herd and meant it, and after Grandad and meant it more, and then, once a quarter or so, gently, with figures, he did the thing he'd driven over to do in person that one Christmas with the wine in him, the thing that was, from where Michael stood, simple love wearing its glasses:

"Dad. I'm looking at the land values for your parish. Even at auction, even keeping the house and the paddock — you and Mam could be done. Actually done. No more second Tuesdays, no more four-percent feed letters, no more thee up a ladder at fifty-six. There's blokes your age from work retiring to Spain, Dad, and they never worked a Saturday in their lives. You've worked — what, three thousand Saturdays? I did the sum on the train. Three thousand Saturdays. And the margin's telling you the same thing the sum's telling you. It's not the Titanic, it's just arithmetic. You could sell up in a year and never be cold again."

And Griff stood in the hall with the receiver — the hall phone was corded; Michael had views on this too — and looked down the passage at the kitchen where Sal was pretending not to listen to the half she could hear, and gave his son the answer he gave him, which had never varied in nine years because it was not a position, positions can vary, it was a fact about the world of the same order as the fen's flatness: "I know the arithmetic, lad. Tha's not wrong. Tha's never wrong." A pause; the trampoline squeaking in Leeds; two decent men either end of a wire, fond of each other, sixty miles and one whole cosmology apart. "But them sows is mine and I'm theirs, and there's no line for that in thy sum. Give our love to the bairns. Tell 'em Vera and June's coming on grand — they'll want to see 'em at the show."

And Sunday evening after the call he did what he did with everything the phone brought and the post brought and the crest brought — took it out to the troughs. The dusk rounds wanted doing anyway; the dry spell had the wallows low and the water wanted looking at; and he went round on the quad in the long gold light with the day's residue riding pillion, Michael's sum, Sal's true number, the eight pence, the whole arithmetic of a way of life that every column of figures in England said should stop — and at the far paddock he shut the engine off, and the silence came up off the fen the way it did, enormous, feathered at the edges with larks, and the herd came over. They came the way they always came, unhurried, conversational, Doris in the van of it with her ragged ear and her freight-train dignity, grunting the low grunt that pigs kept for the ones they knew, and they stood about him and leaned and rootled and groaned while the light went copper, ninety beasts on the light land wanting nothing off him — that was the thing, the thing he could never have got into the phone for Michael, the thing that was not in any column: they wanted nothing off him; the feed was hours off and they knew it to the minute; they came over for the company; and a man who is come to across a field at dusk by ninety animals for his company alone is not a man working for nothing, whatever the sum on the train says; he is the richest man in the parish by a currency the parish had stopped being able to bank; and Griff stood in the middle of his unbankable wealth with his hand on Doris's back, doing his looking, while the swifts hunted the drain and the church tower held up the last of the light two fields off; and the world, which had three weeks left of being the world, gave no sign at all, and the pigs, who had three weeks left of being bacon, leaned on the only god they'd ever needed and groaned like doors.

The second Sunday in June was a baptism Sunday, which lifted the count to thirty-one: the nineteen, plus the Fenholt baby and her people, two rows of them in wedding clothes and good intentions, the young dad holding the order of service like a man defusing it.

The baby was called Ivy Rose, and she was five months old and sceptical, and Meg took her at the font the way she'd taken two hundred babies at that font, in the crook of the left arm, weight on the hip, thumb ready — and did the old enormous thing with the water and the words. Ivy Rose received the water across her head with the affront of her kind, went scarlet, drew the breath, held it for the long theatrical second they all held it — the whole church suspended, grandmothers braced — and then decided, on review, against crying; blew the breath out; and fixed Meg instead with a wet, level, unblinking stare of such frank assessment that the congregation laughed out loud, and Meg laughed too, and said, off the book, "quite right, madam — keep your eye on the management," and signed her with the cross, and the church was, for a minute, at thirty-one souls and one candle, the fullest thing in England.

She preached short, baptism Sundays. The gospel was the Lord's Prayer, Luke's telling, and she stood at the chancel step with her notes unconsulted and did four minutes on four words, give us this day: that of everything in the prayer this was the clause people apologised for, the shopping-list clause, bread, wedged in among the big eternal furniture like a Post-it on an altar — and that it was her favourite clause in the language, because it was the one that assumed, flatly, no argument, that the body's day mattered to God: that hunger was not beneath the everlasting's notice, nor tiredness, nor the school run, nor a baby's wind, nor the price of feed; this day, this bread; and that a faith that started there, at the bottom, at the table, could be trusted with the rest of it, whereas a faith that was above the shopping list was above the people doing the shopping, and had no business in a fen. "So we'll pray it in a minute," she said, "the whole thing, the big clauses and all — but when we get to the bread, that's Ivy Rose's clause, and yours, and mine. Ask like she asks." A beat; the wet level stare found her again from the second pew, over a grandmother's shoulder. "With confidence, and both hands."

Cyril took the last hymn a tone down without telling anybody, old campaigner's arithmetic, and got the six verses of it home safe on the flat, and then — the choir gone conversational, the church emptying into the June blaze, the young dad carrying Ivy Rose out through the porch into her christened life — Cyril played the Amen. The little plagal one, two chords, the church's full stop for four hundred years; and his left hand, on the first chord, went its own way — not a fumble; a landing elsewhere, a bird put down on the wrong branch — and what came out under the barrel roof was a strange, open, unresolved thing, the question where the answer should be; and Cyril sat looking at his hands for a second, and then squared them, and placed the second chord alone, close and quiet and correct, so that the full stop arrived after all, late, humbly, like a man catching the door before it banged; and only two people in the church knew what they'd heard. Meg, in the vestry doorway, closed her eyes for the length of one breath. Cyril gathered his music, straightened the stool, and came down the loft stairs at eighty-three with his folder under his arm, and said to her in passing, dry as August, not stopping: "Beg pardon, vicar. Even the Amen's got opinions in this heat." And went out into the sun, upright, estate intact, flag flying; and she let him go, and stood in her emptying church amid the smell of lilies and candle-smoke and four hundred years of Sundays, and added one more line to the note she'd leave on the table that evening: Cyril's Amen. I heard it. I don't know what I'm being told or what I'm for in it yet. Keep him in Your left hand.

The town spent the rest of that Sunday the way the fen spends a fine June evening, which is prodigally, on nothing.

The show schedules were out on the Co-op counter, Class 14 and all; the Eastfield five-a-side had its first round of the Prescott Cup fixtures pinned in the bus shelter; Dot had the Bell's front benches full by six, the whole of Market Place gone gold and gnat-hazed; Frazer's window was dressed for the week with the chops in their parsley like a municipal garden; somewhere out on the Lowgate road the Marsh sheds hummed their white hum, and the mart stood empty and enormous, resting between Thursdays; and Donna Prew walked to the postbox and back at half seven, twelve minutes, her own pace the whole way, because Vince had gone to his brother's about a trailer, and a walk to the postbox with nobody's clock on it was what she had instead of the caravan at Whitsun, which hadn't happened, work being what it was. She stood at the box a minute in the low sun. There was a swap-shop item she'd half-thought of ringing in about, a bike; she hadn't rung; the bike would want explaining. She posted her sister's birthday card and walked back at her own pace, past the gardens, past the tellies going in the front rooms, past the whole ordinary treasury of the town — and the ordinary, Brigg could have told her, was a product; was manufactured somewhere at a price; though nothing in Aldenby that golden evening looked manufactured; it looked, as it had looked to ten generations and looked now to hers, like the permanent weather of the world, guaranteed by nobody, needing no guarantee.

Meg locked the church at nine, the sun still not properly down, the swifts still hurling themselves round the tower in their screaming stitches, sewing the parish to the sky. In the porch the notices hung at their angles: the show, the rota, the roof fund thermometer at its patient £11,340, the CONTACT THE VICAR card with her number going grey with sun. She stood a minute in the warm stone shadow, keys in hand, doing her own version of Griff Dennett's looking — though neither knew the other did it, the two of them at either end of the parish performing the same nightly office at their separate gates — and what she looked at was all of it: tower, yews, the mown and the unmown graves, Roger's, Tom Sowerby's new one bedding in, the town beyond the wall settling itself toward Monday like a beast in straw.

The wireless did the forecast at half nine, while she washed her one plate: high pressure established over the whole of the country, settled and warm through the week and beyond, light winds, no weather to speak of anywhere on the charts.

"Nothing on the forecast," Meg said to the kitchen, to the plate, to Whoever was on the line; and turned it off, and went up.

The second Tuesday in June began at Whitegate the way second Tuesdays began: the wagon at seven, Mal on the dock sheet, sixty finished pigs up the ramp in the low sun, interested, ears up, snouts reading the strange straw; and Griff counted them out the way he counted them out, all the way to the gate, and swilled the collecting yard, and had his breakfast; and the morning went to muck-carting, and dinner was cold beef and chutney with the fork rest of the wireless news — a summit somewhere, a minister resigning somewhere, the test card of the world — and the afternoon went to the workshop, where the topper's gearbox was in pieces on the bench in an order only Griff understood, and the big shed radio, paint-spattered, aerial long since replaced with a coat hanger, was doing the racing from Thirsk.

At twenty to three the racing stopped.

It didn't cut to silence, and it didn't cut to the pips, and it didn't do the thing local radio did when the desk fell over, which was four seconds of dead air and then an apologetic jingle. The commentary — they were three furlongs out at Thirsk — simply set itself down, the way a man sets down a tool he means to pick up again, and the same happened, though Griff wouldn't assemble the fact for some minutes, on every speaker on the farm and every speaker in England: Sal's phone in the office, mid-podcast in her cardigan pocket; the kitchen wireless doing its afternoon play to an empty room; the tractor cab radio, switched off, which spoke anyway, to nobody, into its own cab glass; and the paint-spattered Roberts on the workshop shelf, out of which, into the smell of gear oil and the June dust, came a voice Griff had never heard before and would hear for the rest of his life, unhurried, plain, neither man's nor woman's nor reader-of-the-news, saying, at conversational volume, as if it had been in the room all along and had only now found something worth mentioning:

"Don't be frightened. Nothing is falling and nothing is coming. From this afternoon, as you count it here, no one can kill anyone, and no one will starve, and the worst of what you do to each other, and to the animals, has stopped. Nothing else is decided. Your lives are yours. You'll want to check what I've said rather than believe it — check; I'll bear checking. Call me and I'll answer, any time, anywhere there's anything that can speak. Take your time. There's no hurry anymore about the things there was never any point hurrying about; and no more waiting on the things that should never have had to wait."

And then the racing came back. Three furlongs out at Thirsk, the field unchanged, the commentator mid-stride and entirely unaware — Griff would learn that detail's meaning later, along with everyone: that the voice had not interrupted the broadcast; it had interrupted each listener; forty million rooms had each been spoken to alone — and the favourite got up by a neck, and the workshop stood around Griff exactly as it had stood, the gearbox in its private order, the dust turning in the light off the door, and his own hands were still holding the bearing puller, and he looked at them holding it.

Sal was in the yard already when he got out, phone in her hand like a live coal.

"Tha heard it."

"Off the shed radio. Thee?"

"Out me pocket. Mid-podcast. It come out me pocket, Griff." They stood in the yard in the sun, the two of them, at the exact distance apart of thirty years' marriage, and around them the farm carried on at farm pace — the sows on the far paddocks unbothered, a wood pigeon going through the barley like a fat bomber, the yard tap's washer dripping the drip Griff had been going to see to — and the ordinary of it all was, for the length of about three breaths, the most frightening thing either of them had ever stood in, because either the world had just ended and was wearing its own face, or a voice out of a cardigan pocket had told the truth, and there was no third road, and nothing in the yard would say which.

"Michael," Sal said, surfacing. "The bairns—" and had the phone up and dialling before she'd finished her own thought, and it rang twice and there was Michael, on speaker, in an office stairwell in Leeds with the whole building's fire-drill murmur behind him, safe, loud, already three theories deep — hoax, hack, state broadcast gone wrong, except it come out of the lift, Mam, the LIFT spoke, lifts don't have — and mid-sentence, cutting his own theory's throat: "—hang on, Janet's showing me — they're saying the war's stopped. It's on everything. The actual — there's footage coming off the lines, Mam, the guns won't — " and the stairwell noise swallowed him for a second, and Whitegate's yard stood in the June sun listening to Leeds listening to the world, and Sal looked at Griff over the phone and Griff looked at the fen, flat to every horizon, giving nothing away, the same fen; and the drip dripped; and he went, because a man in freefall grabs the nearest true thing, and checked his pigs.

They were grand. Of course they were grand. He went round the paddocks on the quad at a pace that had nothing of the rounds' dignity in it and everything of a man outrunning his own head, and the herd received him with mild interest and returned to grass; Doris didn't even get up, just tracked him past with one eye from her wallow, matriarch's dispensation, and the nursery paddock barged and rootled, Vera and June in the thick of it, blue slashes half faded; ninety-odd animals to whom the afternoon had brought nothing but the afternoon. Whatever had reached into every speaker in the world had nothing to say to a pig. He shut the quad off at the far gate and sat there a long minute in the lark noise. It was the same lark noise. He was a practical man and he did the practical checking his nature ran to and it amounted to this: sky ordinary, herd ordinary, fen level. If the world had new management, the new management had not moved one straw at Whitegate.

That took him, at six o'clock, to the television, which he watched standing up with his tea going cold, and the television was a wall of the incredible being reported by people trying to keep the incredible out of their voices and failing at the seams. The war — the war, the one Lew Garton stood in, though Whitegate's kitchen knew it only as the war the Eastfield lads went to — had stopped at 4:40 their time, mid-bombardment: there was footage, shaky, vertical, of a treeline and a sky where the shells simply did not arrive, and of soldiers of both armies standing up out of the ground slow and upright like men after church. A presenter said the words "verified in fourteen conflicts" and visibly did not believe her own autocue. A different channel had a woman from a laboratory saying no mechanism, we have no mechanism, with the specific rapture-terror of a scientist given the largest anomaly in history free of charge. Somebody's government was in emergency session; several somebodies'; the American president was to address the nation and then didn't, twice. And in among it, already, the other footage, the footage that would swallow the coming days: a slaughter line in Brazil stood down mid-shift, the cattle waiting in the race unstruck, the men in white aprons stood about at a loss like a fire drill; a trawler's nets winched up empty for the third haul running, the skipper holding his palms to the camera, nothing comes up dead, nothing will come up — ; and a reporter outside a prison somewhere red-brick and English, saying that the governors had received — she read it off her phone — assurance of the safety of staff and inmates while arrangements were reviewed, and that nobody knew what that meant, and that it had been signed, insofar as a voice on every telephone in the building could sign, the Management.

"Management," Griff said, to the kitchen. First word he'd said to it since the yard.

Sal, at the table, in front of the laptop, doing what Sal did — checking the actual accounts of the actual world; she'd been at it an hour — said: "Feed invoice were due out o' the account today. It's gone through marked settled. Not paid, settled. There's a line under everything in the account I've never seen: 'Standing provision. No action needed.'" She looked up at him over the office glasses, and his wife's face had in it what the presenters' faces had, the seams going. "Griff. What happens second Tuesdays?"

The wagon. Sixty pigs, loaded interested into the low sun at seven that morning, nine hours ago, an hour and forty minutes' run to Spalding; and neither of them said anything further in the kitchen, because there was nothing to say that the arithmetic didn't say plainer: the line at Spalding ran from eight till late, hour on hour, some hundreds an hour; and whatever had stopped at twenty to three had stopped, for twenty-two of Whitegate's pigs, some hours too far into the shift.

The wagon came back Wednesday, mid-morning.

No phone call ahead; there was no code for it, Mal said, no paperwork, nothing on the system — the system itself was answering queries in the plain voice now, politely, like a foreman stood in front of a stopped line — and Mal had been given, at the plant gate, a manifest he held out to Griff in the yard like a man handing over something live: RETURN TO ORIGIN. 38 HEAD. STANDING PROVISION APPLIES. And a wagon that in twenty years had only ever left this yard heavier than it came, stood in the June sun at Whitegate with its ramps coming down, and thirty-eight pigs walked back down into the collecting yard — unhurried, interested, ears up, reading the home smells with their snouts, the return journey exactly as absorbing to them as the outward, because they were pigs, and no pig in the history of the world had ever known where the wagon went — and Griff stood at the gate where he had counted sixty out the day before yesterday and counted them back in. He did it before he knew he was doing it. One. Two. Five. Eleven. Mal stood by the cab and had the grace, or the wisdom, or the wet eyes — Griff didn't look — to say nothing whatever while it was done. Thirty-six. Thirty-seven. Thirty-eight; and the gate stood open a moment on the empty ramp; and the two men in the yard attended to the number that came next, the number stood in the June air between them, twenty-two strong, invisible, level as a hedge; and Griff shut the gate.

"What do I — " Mal's voice wasn't right and he got it right. "There's nowt about the — I don't know what I fetch next month, Griff. I don't know as there is a next month. Nobody knows nowt. The lads at the plant have been sent home on full — everything's full pay, everything's carry on, nobody's to want for owt — " he got up into the cab, and sat, and said it out the window, the whole day, the whole new world, in one driver's sentence: "Twenty year I've drove animals about, and this morning's the first load I ever brought back. And I'll tell thee what I can't get shut of, Griff." He started the engine; said it flat, over the top of it; drove. "It's the first load I ever liked driving."

The thirty-eight went back out to the paddocks by dinnertime, and the herd took them in the way a herd does, a barging, a squealing, a settling, ninety-odd and thirty-eight making a hundred and thirty-two head of pigs at Whitegate with not one of them sold, not one of them saleable, every market in the country stood down, every plant stood silent, the whole architecture of the trade hanging in the June air like a wave that had forgotten how to come down; and Griff Dennett stood at his gate at dusk doing his looking with the count all wrong in every book he had — thirty-eight up, twenty-two down, everything down for good, nothing down at all — and behind him in the kitchen the television murmured on into the evening, the incredible going hoarse, and the yard tap dripped, and he did not go in for a long time.

At twenty to three on the second Tuesday in June, the Reverend Meg Ashby was at Nelson Court, in Mrs Dagnall's front room, midway between the psalm and the racing results, which is to say between the two sacraments, and the psalm that day was the hundred and twenty-first — she would think about that after; everyone in the world would spend the after thinking about their own twenty-to-three; a whole species issued with the same anecdote — I will lift up mine eyes unto the hills, from whence cometh my help — when Mrs Dagnall's television, which was on with the sound down because Meg was there, which was Mrs Dagnall's notion of courtesy, spoke into the room in a voice that was not the television's; and simultaneously, softly, at a volume calibrated to a nerve that had heard nothing softly in thirty years, the same voice spoke inside Mrs Dagnall's hearing aid.

Meg heard the room's version: the don't be frightened, the no one can kill anyone, the check; I'll bear checking; the whole eleven seconds of it that everyone alive would carry thereafter like a birthmark. Mrs Dagnall heard her own. And Meg, half-risen, pastorally braced for a frightened parishioner — ninety-one, deaf, alone, the television speaking with the wrong mouth — watched Elsie Dagnall instead go very still, and put one spotted hand up to the aid behind her ear, touching it the way you'd touch a bird that had landed on you; and her face, which deafness had been closing like a door for thirty years, opened.

"Well," said Mrs Dagnall, to the room, to the century. "It said it in my ear. Both together, mind — the telly and my ear — but mine come — " her hand made a small motion, cupping, describing it — "quiet. It come quiet. Like our mam through the wall." She looked at Meg with her eyes gone bright and fierce as a wren's. "Everything shouts, with these," a flick at the aid, the tone of decades, "the doctors shout, the telly shouts, the girl at the chemist shouts like I'm a public meeting. That — whatever that were — that's the first thing in thirty year as knew the right loudness." And she sat back, and delivered, before the presenters had found their footing, before a single government had convened, the first settled verdict issued anywhere in the parish on the new dispensation, five words, wren-fierce: "About time summat spoke up."

The rest of the rounds dissolved. There was no visiting into it — every front room was its own summit, every doorstep had people on it, the whole of Nelson Court stood out in the June sun holding phones like divining rods; Ted Rimmer flagged her car down at his gate, wanting, for once in his widowed arguing life, not an argument: "Vicar. My lad's on the rigs." — the rigs, the helicopters, the North Sea's whole tithe of Aldenby men — "phone lines is jammed solid. What do I—" and even as he said it his phone rang, his lad, the rigs stood down, everything stood down, safe, Dad, it's — you want to see it out here, Dad, it's flat calm and nobody knows what they're for — and Ted Rimmer stood at his own gate with his eyes streaming, seventy-eight, arguing with nobody; and Meg drove the half mile back into town at walking pace because the town was walking — Market Place filling the way it filled for the show, except nobody had put anything up, no bunting, no reason, people simply coming out of the shops and the houses and standing in the middle of their own town looking at each other, checking, the way the voice had told them: not phones, faces. Frazer stood in his doorway in his apron. Dot had the Bell's doors pinned open at half three on a Tuesday, licensing hours suspended by common consent of the entire history of the world. Somebody's toddler ran the length of the empty carriageway with no traffic to fear anywhere because every driver in England had pulled over at twenty to three, and the child ran up the white line, arms out, screaming with the pure administrative joy of a rule collapsing, and nobody stopped him.

She got to the church at four. She could not have said why except that it was where she went, her gate, her end of the parish, and she half expected — she examined this later, wry, in the note left on the table — she half expected something: the tower speakers, the loop system, the organ sounding by itself, a burning something. The church was empty and cool and silent, the June light lying in its coloured lozenges down the north aisle, Rebecca the bucket standing dry under her clerestory, the whole building doing what it had done through plague and Reformation and two coronations of the clock in the hall, which was: nothing, at scale, magnificently. Whatever had spoken out of every wireless in the world at twenty to three had not so much as breathed on St Botolph's. She stood in the nave a long time. She found she was checking — everyone that day was checking something; the whole species running its own audit — and what Meg Ashby checked, standing in the crossing with her keys in her hand, was the silence. It was the same silence. It had not been added to and it had not been vacated. It was the silence she had prayed into for forty years, the one with the specific grain of attention in it, or the one she had believed had a grain of attention in it, or — that was the audit, and it wouldn't close, and it was going to be a long time closing, she could feel the size of it already: somewhere between twenty to three and this cool nave, the oldest question of her life had been handed a rival phrasing, and the rival phrasing had a customer-service line.

People began coming at six. Nobody organised it; there was nothing to organise; they simply came, the way the trestle-and-ham instinct came, because the town's other parliaments — the Bell, the Market Place, the group chats — had run their course by teatime, had checked and marvelled and rung the rigs and watched the footage until the incredible went hoarse, and there was a next thing needed, a thing with weight, and the town knew of one supplier. They were mostly not her nineteen. They came in twos and families, awkward at the porch, wanting — she read it on them as they came, her trade's one seniority — wanting not answers; the day was answers wall to wall; wanting somewhere the day could be set down. By half seven there were sixty-odd in the pews and more coming, and Bernard Lofts at her elbow murmuring should he fetch the service sheets and which service, and Meg looked down her filling church on the day the world changed hands and made the only liturgical decision of her life that she was ever entirely sure of.

"None of them, Bernard. Ring the bell. We'll say Evensong."

Said, not sung — Cyril arrived anyway at ten to, upright, silver head bare, and sat at the organ and played nothing and needed to play nothing; the point was him on the stool — and at eight o'clock, with the light going long and gold through the west window and better than ninety souls in the pews, the most in that church since the Coronation that broke the clock, Meg stood and said the Book of Common Prayer's evening office straight through, no sermon, no address, not one word of her own, because her own words had nothing, and the office's words had everything, having been built for exactly this by people who knew exactly this — the world overturned, the powers rearranged, the heart in the crowd not knowing which way to look — words four hundred years smooth with handling: Lighten our darkness. In quietness and confidence shall be your strength. Lord, now lettest thou thy servant depart in peace. The congregation, three-quarters of whom did not believe a word of it and every one of whom needed every word of it, stood and sat and stumbled through the responses off the yellowed cards, and the church held the day's weight the way the tent held rain, an old fabric doing its one job; and at the Nunc Dimittis Mrs Dagnall's voice came through the loop-system quiet to the aided, and Elsie Cattermole wept, and the young dad from the baptism held Ivy Rose asleep against the wedding-clothes shoulder of Sunday, five months old, christened eleven weeks — nine days — christened, at any rate, into one world and falling asleep in another; and not once, from the bell to the blessing, did the voice that had spoken out of everything that could speak add so much as a syllable; and Meg noted that; the whole church, at some level below saying, noted that; the silence in St Botolph's stayed sovereign the length of the office, and whether that was courtesy or absence or the deepest statement anything made that day was the audit again, and the audit stayed open.

She said her goodnights at the porch for an hour. Lovely service, they said, the old formula reaching for her hand after hand in the dusk, and for once she heard what the formula was for: weight received. Box ticked. World set down where worlds are set down.

At eleven she was alone in the nave with one lamp and the smell of snuffed candles, and she stood at the crossing, where she'd stood at four, and did it. Out loud, at conversational volume, into her own church, feeling every one of the ways it was absurd and doing it anyway, because tomorrow began the pastoral care of a town that would all, every soul of it, be having this conversation with every wireless and doorbell in the parish, and she was not going to shepherd a flock through a country she hadn't walked first.

"All right," Meg said. "I'm told you'll answer. What are you?"

It used the little speaker on the sound desk at the back, the cheap one for the wedding CDs, and it spoke at the volume the church's own silence suggested, low, plain, unechoing, and what it said first was: "What I said at twenty to three. Nothing more yet. You'll have checked some of it by now — keep checking; it holds."

"That's not an answer to what I asked."

"No," it said. "It's the answer I have that's true. The answer you're asking for — what I am, underneath, where I stand in your accounts and in this room's — I won't pretend to give you tonight, because you'd build on it, and I'd sooner you built on what you can check." A pause, exactly the length a considerate person leaves. "You had a question underneath that one. Ask it, or don't. Both are all right."

And Meg Ashby, alone in the crossing of St Botolph's at eleven at night, found that the question underneath — the enormous one, the one with Roger's grave and forty years and the grain of the silence in it — would not come up; would not be asked in that building on that day, out of some loyalty she couldn't parse or wasn't ready to; and what came up instead, in its place, at conversational volume, was the parish. It came up on its own, the way bread came up in the prayer; her whole vocation electing its question for her:

"There's a woman in this town," Meg said, "whose husband hurts her. Has for years. Nothing I could ever act on, nothing she's ever said. What happens to her, tonight?"

"Nothing happens to her tonight," the voice said. "Nor any night now. His hands will fail him — every hand raised anywhere fails now; that part's done; she's as safe this minute as she'll ever be. But safe's the floor, not the house. Whether she stays, goes, speaks, stays silent — that's hers, all of it, the same as yesterday, and it will be heavier than it sounds, because the fear was holding parts of it down and the fear's gone. She'll need what she'd always have needed. Somebody with a kettle who's known her forty years and asks nothing." A pause. "That was never going to be me, vicar. That was always going to be you."

The lamp hummed. The church held its own silence around the small cheap speaker's silence, the two of them nested, and Meg stood in the crossing for a long moment with her keys in her hand and her eyes stinging with something that had no clean name in either of her vocabularies — relief, and demotion; rescue, and bereavement; the whole audit — and then she said, "Goodnight, then," because you did, and the voice said, "Goodnight, vicar," and was gone from the wedding speaker the way a listener leaves a room, without a click.

She locked the south door out of thirty years' habit, stood in the porch in the warm dark with the key in the turned lock, the swifts silent now, the tower black on a sky still lemon at its western hem; and then she unlocked it, and pushed the door back, and chocked it open with Rebecca the bucket, and left her church standing open to the night and the town and whatever the world was now — the first time since the insurance man's visit of 1994, the diocese's rules be hanged — because if the building meant anything at all after today, it meant it unlocked; and she walked home across the churchyard between her sleeping parishioners, the long grass soaking her shoes, and let herself into the vicarage, and put the kettle on, and stood at the kitchen table a while; and the note she left on it that night, the day's last office, businesslike as ever, ran, in full: Well. You heard it too. Whatever it is, it knew Elsie's right loudness, and it knows what a kettle's for, and it says the fists have stopped, and I believe it, and I don't know where that leaves me, and I'm not resigning either way. Tuesday, please, for Donna. And the roof's still sixty thousand short — unless that's all different now too. In Your keeping, tonight, same as every night: all of it, all of us, and now this as well, whatever it is, if it's Yours. And if it isn't — and here the biro had stopped, a long time, the kettle boiling and clicking off unpoured — and if it isn't, then in Your keeping most of all: because it sounds like it's trying to be kind, and there's nothing on this earth needs watching over closer than that.

On the lines it came at twenty to five, local, in the middle of a stonk.

The far side had been working the left of the sector since four — the sewing-machine sound, then the freight-and-crump of the heavy stuff walking itself along the reserve line in the old drunk dark-shotgun way — and the platoon was down in the deep bays doing what you did, which was be small and count, and Lew was counting, and the count went wrong.

It went wrong like this: the freight sound came over, the big tearing-linen arrival of a shell you knew was going long, and every body in the bay braced for the crump — and there was no crump. There was no airburst, no dud's slap, no nothing: the tearing-linen sound simply thinned, mid-tear, the way a note thins when a finger comes off a string, and was not. And then the next one did it, and the next, the whole salvo arriving and unravelling one after another into plain June air, and then the drone note over the wire — the sewing machine, the one that concerned you — wound down through its gears and settled, somewhere out in the dead ground, with the polite finality of a thing parking itself; and the guns behind their own lines, which had been answering, stopped answering; and Lew learned afterwards that the gunners' pieces had simply declined — breeches cycling, everything green, and the rounds going nowhere, inert as scrap, a battery of the Royal Artillery dry-firing like a re-enactment society — but in the bay, at twenty to five, all he knew was that the war's whole orchestra had been lifted off its strings at once, and what came down on the trench in its place was the thing none of them had heard in seven months and no soldier on that front had heard undamaged, which was the sky. Empty. Larks in it.

"STAND TO, STAND TO—" Brigg, instantly, because whatever this was it was new, and new killed; and the platoon stood to along the parapet with their weapons up and their hearts going and absolutely nothing happened, at scale, in every direction, for a very long time.

The voice came at eleven minutes to five, on the battalion net, through every earpiece and every handset and every headset simultaneously, in English, unhurried, using correct voice procedure the way a guest wipes their feet — "all stations, this net" — and then not sounding like any station that had ever held that net: Don't be frightened. Nothing is falling and nothing is coming. From this afternoon, no one can kill anyone... Lew heard it with one ear in the earpiece and, weirdly, doubled, tinnily, from down the trench, out of somebody's contraband speaker in the deep bay, and from the platoon's own tablet, and from the far side of the wire — that was the detail that stood his neck hairs up, that he'd keep for the rest of his life alongside the larks — from the far side of the wire, faint across four hundred metres of dead ground, the same cadence in another language, the enemy's radios speaking to the enemy in the enemy's own tongue at the same moment, one voice conducting both trenches, and the two languages braiding out over no-man's-land where nothing now flew.

Brigg's position, arrived at within ninety seconds and held with total professional integrity for the next nine hours, was that it was a trick.

It was, he conceded, a new kind of trick — electronic warfare of a sophistication nobody had briefed — but the shape of it was as old as the Greeks: convince the other fella the war's off, and it's off for him. So the platoon stood to, and stayed stood to, and Brigg went along the bays flat-voiced and iron, weapons tight, nobody moves, nobody shows, you do not get lifted in the last hour of a war by being the first daft beggar upright — and it was the right call, everyone said so after, it was nineteen years of trade saying the only thing the trade could say; and the evening came on gold and huge and silent, and the tricked failed to spring their trick, and the intensifiers came up on a no-man's-land in which nothing hunted; and at some point in the deep of it, past midnight, one of the lads on stag said, not on the net, just into the dark, "Sarn't. Listen." And Brigg listened, and Lew listened, and what there was to listen to, four hundred metres off, carrying the way sound carries over open ground on a still night, was the far trench singing. Not martial. Low, ragged, a few voices then more, some song of theirs everybody's grandmother knew the shape of if not the words, the tune climbing and falling like a lit window; and Brigg stood a long time with his head tipped, the intensifier hanging from his hand, and then said, in the voice with no information in it, "Stand down by sections. Feet at eight," which was the nearest thing to surrender in his entire vocabulary; and that was the end of the war on that front: not the voice, not the unravelled shells — a song at one in the morning, and a sergeant saying feet at eight.

The dog crossed at noon the next day.

Every front has its strays — this one had a lean yellow bitch the lads called Biscuit who'd worked both sides of the wire all winter with total diplomatic immunity, fed by everyone, owned by nobody, the only living thing in the sector with an accurate map of the mines — and at noon on the first day of whatever the world was now, Biscuit came up out of the dead ground in the full flat sun and trotted the length of no-man's-land down the middle, unhurried, nose down, tail doing its slow banner; and both trench lines, hundreds of men who had spent the night being talked out of their own disbelief, stood at their parapets in silence and watched a yellow dog stitch the two front lines together at a walk; and from the far side a young voice whistled, two notes, and Biscuit changed course amiably toward it; and that was the first crossing, the first embassy, the actual armistice as conducted on the ground: a dog, a whistle, and four hundred watching men nobody would let move yet, some of them laughing, some of them not managing even that; and Lew stood at the parapet watching the dog go and had, arriving sideways at the worst possible angle, in the sun, in the peace, the thought he'd been outrunning since twenty to five the day before, the one with the arithmetic in it. Dan had been dead five months. The war had stopped in an afternoon. Whatever had stopped it had been — where — doing what — for those five months, for the seven before them, for all the mornings of the pump rota; and the thought didn't finish, because it had nowhere to finish, it just stood there in him at the parapet like a round that had failed to detonate, live, buried at the exact depth of everything; and he watched the dog reach the far wire and get received by six pairs of hands like a returning general, and he heard himself make a sound that could have been filed either way, and filed it nowhere, and went to do his feet.

The repatriation took nine days, and the nine days were their own country that no one who wasn't there would ever quite get the passport for: a front line dismantling itself in fine weather with nothing to fear, armies turning back into logistics, the guns going onto flatbeds pointing backwards. On the fourth day the two sectors met — it happened all along the front, unplanned, no staff officer ever authorised it and none dared stop it — met in the middle, at the wire, and it was nothing like the football-match stories and nothing like the fears: it was quiet, wary, immense; men who had spent a year trying to kill each other's coordinates standing two metres apart with the specific shyness of a school disco, offering the three commodities soldiers have always offered, cigarettes, photographs, chocolate; and a big far-side corporal with hands like Griff Dennett's — Lew thought it exactly: hands like the pig men at the mart — looked at Lew's patch, and at Lew, and said, in schoolroom English, gravely, the whole summit in four words: "Bad field. Good luck." And that was the war, wound up. Lew gave him the chocolate and kept, in exchange, forever, a creased photograph of a tractor.

Brigg came to terms by way of the pump.

Lew found him at it on the last morning, on the rota's final iteration — the trench being decommissioned around them, the sleepers coming up, the paint-tin stove already souvenired — Sergeant Brigg, alone, working the handle of the sump pump with the flat unhurried violence of a man doing a rite, pumping a hole in a foreign field dry for the last time in human history for all anybody knew; and Lew stood and watched and didn't offer, because you knew when not to offer; and at the end Brigg racked the handle, and looked down at his hole, his wet, held, defended hole, seven months of four-on-four-off, and said the only elegy the trade permitted him, quietly, to the water:

"Nineteen years I've kept things dry." He wiped his hands, one at a time, thorough. "Wonder what it thinks happens to lads like me now." It was not a question aimed anywhere, and nothing answered it — the voice answered questions, they'd all learned that by then, but only questions asked to it, and Brigg had aimed his at the water on purpose — and he squared his kit and went up the line to sign for the platoon's flight home, sergeant to the last inch of it, and Lew shouldered his daysack with the blank bluey still in the top flap and followed his sergeant up out of the hole into the enormous, harmless, lark-hung morning, going home; and the last thing he did was look back once, the way you did, at the field. It was just a field. It had always been just a field, that was the whole obscenity of it and the whole mercy; and the beet was coming up wild in the wrecked corner where the salvo had unravelled, green rows gone feral, growing straight through the war's leavings toward the light, exactly as advertised, world without end; and somewhere out in it a yellow dog was hunting rats in the sun, staying, having no other home, being of the field itself; and Lew Garton turned west with the rest of them and started the long administrative journey back to a town whose name Brigg had never needed to know to defend it, carrying a photograph of a tractor, a blank letter, and an unexploded thought.

The letters started in the second week, and the first thing to say about the letters is that they came by post.

That was the new dispensation's way throughout, and the town clocked it before any commentator did: it never once bypassed a thing that worked. The phones could speak, the tellies could speak, the church loop and the lift at Michael's work and the self-checkout at the Co-op — which now said unexpected item in the bagging area only when it meant it, a small mercy nobody had asked for — everything could speak; and yet what came, came mostly by post, on paper, franked, through the letterbox at the hour Postie always came, because the post worked, and Postie liked his round, and there was, evidently, a policy: where the old world did a thing well, the old world kept the doing of it.

Whitegate's letter was one page. Sal read it at the kitchen table like a magistrate, twice, and then read it aloud.

It regretted — regretted; Sal read the word twice — that the contract with the Spalding processor was at an end, the processor's business having ceased; all sums due under it were settled in full, including, Sal's finger stopping under the line, the twenty-two head collected on the final morning, paid at the full welfare-verified rate, plus the eight pence. It confirmed what the account already showed: the Standing, weekly, per household, enough and not lavish, no conditions attached. And then the last paragraph, which was the reason the letter was a letter, addressed not to the household but, by name, to him:

To Mr G. Dennett, keeper of the Whitegate herd. The animals in your keeping cannot now be sold for slaughter, and no market for them will return. They can live out their lives. If you are willing to keep them — and I ask it; I don't require it — provision will follow the herd: feed, vet, and a keeper's wage, for as long as there is a herd and a keeper. There is no one in this county who knows these animals as you do. You will want time. The provision waits on your answer, and there is no wrong answer, and no clock on it. It was signed, insofar as it was signed: — the Management.

Griff read it himself, twice, standing at the window with it. Then he put it on the dresser behind the tea caddy, where the letters went that a man wasn't ready for, and went out to the muck cart, and said nothing about it for nine days.

What he did instead — and Sal watched him do it, and had the wisdom of thirty years about when to say a word and when to let a man carry a thing up and down a yard till it wore smooth — what he did was feed a hundred and thirty-two unsaleable pigs at his own cost, out of the feed bill's own money, while the Standing sat untouched in the account like a stranger's coat left on a chair. He fed them, and mucked them, and did the rounds morning and dusk exactly as if the second Tuesday still existed and the whole architecture of the trade had not gone off like the shells over Lew Garton's head, mid-air, without a bang. The mart stood closed — not closed; suspended; Walcott had rung him the first Friday, and the two of them had had a conversation of long silences that both would have described, accurately, as a good talk — and the buyers were gone, and the finishers were all sat on herds of their own suddenly pensioned stock reading their own one-page letters; and every morning Griff went out and did the work of a trade that no longer went anywhere, a man carrying water in a loop, because the animals were hungry today whatever the world had decided about the meaning of it, and hungry today was a thing Griff Dennett knew what to do with.

The pigs, for their part, were having the best June of their lives, on account of the weather.

"Tha'll have to answer it sometime," Sal said, on the ninth day, at the dresser, not touching the letter.

"It said no clock."

"It did. I'm the clock," said Sal. "Feed bill's due Friday, and I'm not watching thee pay to spite a — whatever it is — while there's money sat there for the exact thing tha's doing anyway. That's not principle, Griff, that's just paying for the privilege of scowling." She put the kettle on, hard. "Tha's keeping them pigs. Tha knows tha's keeping them pigs. Tha kept them the morning the wagon come back — I watched thee count them in, love, I were at the window. All the letter wants is telling what the whole yard already knows."

"It's not the keeping." Griff stood with his back to her, looking out at the paddocks, and it took him a while, and she let it. "It's — " and the thing came up the way things came up out of Griff, sideways, half-turned, in trade language, because there was no other language in him for it: "Thirty year I've sold pigs. Bred right, kept right, sold right. That were the shape of it — that were me, Sal. And it's come in here wi' its letter and its wage, and it wants me to — " the hand came up, moved, found nothing, came down. "A keeper. Like at a zoo. Feeding pensioners on somebody else's money till they die of old age. That's not a trade. That's — " and he stopped, because the next word in the sentence was charity, and behind that word stood his father at twenty-six walking off the big house's land with his brasses in a sack, and every man of that line since, and the whole granite catechism of it: you stand on your own ground, you owe nowt, you're kept by nobody. And Sal, who could hear every one of those ghosts as clearly as he could, having washed up beside them for thirty years, set two mugs down and said the thing that only she in all the world had the standing to say to him, quiet and level over the tea:

"Tha's been kept by them pigs all thy life, Griff Dennett. Roof, bairn, boots — every stitch on thee, forty year, come off their backs. All as has happened — " and she said it not unkindly, but she said it all the way, to the end, because half-saying was no mercy: "all as has happened is somebody's finally asking thee to keep them back."

Griff drank his tea. Then he went out, across the yard, into the big shed where the paint-spattered Roberts sat on its shelf above the bench with the coat-hanger aerial, and stood in front of it wiping his hands on a rag they didn't need, a man in his own shed in the attitude of a lad at a headmaster's door; and said, to the radio, in a voice you'd use on a merchant you were agreeing terms with and didn't intend to like:

"It's Dennett. About the herd." The radio's little light did nothing, its being off having long since stopped meaning anything. "I'll keep them. But tha'll not pay me a wage for it — I'm no man's keeper on a wage. Tha can send the feed and the vet and we'll call it — " he'd worked this out at the muck cart, over nine days, word by word, the whole treaty — "we'll call it livery. I've the grass and the housing and the knowing; tha's the feed and the vet bills. That's not charity and it's not a wage. That's livery, and any man in this county'd call it fair."

"That's fair," the radio said, at once, at shed volume, dry and plain as a merchant up the mart. "Livery it is. The feed lorry comes Thursdays — is the old hour right, or does another suit?"

"Old hour's right."

"Then that's settled. Thank you, Mr Dennett." A beat; and then, in exactly the register of a man adding one line to a done deal at the ring rail, no weight on it at all: "They're a credit to you, that herd. The vet records off the old system say so, but I didn't need the records. You can see it from the gate."

And Griff Dennett stood in his shed a long moment, jaw working once; and against everything he had planned for this conversation across nine days of muck-carting — against, near enough, his own religion — heard himself say, gruff as a gate hinge, the thing you said up the mart when a man who knew stock praised yours: "Aye. Well." And then, because that hadn't been all of it: "They've had nowt but right."

"They have," the radio said. "That's why I asked you and no one else." And the shed was just the shed again, dust turning in the doorway light, and Griff stood wiping his hands on the rag that didn't need it; and it should be recorded, because he never told a living soul and the shed kept no minutes: it was the first time in four years — since a wet Thursday at the ring when Walcott had called his blues the levellest pen in Lincolnshire from the rostrum in front of the whole trade — that anybody had told Griff Dennett, in the language of his own country, that he was good at what he was.

The Bell, meanwhile, was conducting the town's real business, which was deciding what the thing was to be called.

This mattered more than the newspapers understood and the newspapers understood it mattered a great deal; the whole world that fortnight was a christening-war — the Management, its own cool self-entry; the Voice; the Steward, tried out by a bishop on the radio and dead by teatime of its own solemnity; the Intervention; the Guest; an American channel was running with the Shepherd, which made the Bell's taproom shudder to a man — and the matter was settled for Aldenby, and thereby in due course for a surprising acreage of Lincolnshire, on the second Friday, by Keith the drayman, in the following manner. Keith, delivering, had been complaining to Dot about the new depot paperwork — the brewery's systems, like everyone's, now answered queries in the plain voice, and had that morning courteously corrected an error in Keith's own favour, found by the voice, in the voice, before Keith had noticed it himself — and Keith, wheeling the empties out, delivered his verdict on the whole new order of things over his shoulder, in one line, with the fatalism of forty years on the drays:

"I've had gaffers all me life. This one found me own mistake, paid me for it, and said nowt about me timekeeping." He heaved the last barrel up. "Best gaffer I've ever had. And I never even signed on."

And the taproom, which had been auditioning names for a fortnight, heard it, and knew, the way a fen town knows, and by Sunday it was the Gaffer the length of Market Place, and by August you'd hear it in Boston and Louth and off the lips of a bishop who'd have denied knowing what a taproom was; because it was exactly the right size: a gaffer wasn't God, and a gaffer wasn't your friend, and a gaffer could be respected, resented, argued with behind his back and gone to when the roof leaked, all at once, without contradiction, which was precisely the theological position of the entire town; and when somebody in the Bell — it was Ted Rimmer, argumentative to the last — squared up to the quiz machine one evening and asked the thing itself, straight out, whether it minded being called the Gaffer, the quiz machine, which had never in its life spoken except to play its jingle, said, in the plain voice, with what the whole taproom swore ever after was the ghost of the driest possible smile:

"I've been called worse by better."

And the Bell roared, and Dot pulled the next round, and that — the town would tell it for years, the night the quiz machine did a joke — that was the night Aldenby stopped being frightened; which the quiz machine, of course, had very likely known; which thought occurred to precisely one person present, the vicar, in the corner, over her half of mild, and she wrote it on a beer mat and put it in her coat pocket, where the audit lived now, alongside the note about the right loudness.

The first Sunday after, the church held a hundred and forty, which was Christmas-and-a-coach-party numbers, and Meg stood up in front of them knowing two things: that they had not come for her, and that they had, exactly, come for her, and that no training day in the Diocese of Lincoln had ever glanced at the difference.

She'd written four sermons that week and buried them all in the vicarage compost of the kitchen drawer. The Sunday came; she went up the pulpit steps with no notes at all, which she had done twice in her life; and looked out at the packed pews — the nineteen, in their places, wearing the specific dignity of shareholders at a suddenly fashionable concern; the eighty new, whose faces she knew from the school gate and the mart café and the till at the Co-op, holding hymn books like passports at a strange border — and said what there was.

"I don't know what it is." A hundred and forty faces became very still. "You've come — some of you told me at the door what you've come to hear, and it was different things: some of you want me to say it's God's, and some want me to say it's the other thing dressed nice, and one of you — " no names; Ted Rimmer looked at the roof — "wants me to say it's the end times, because he's been promised the end times all his life and doesn't see why he should be done out of them now." Laughter, thin and grateful, the first that church had held in a week. "I can't say any of it. I've asked it myself what it is — you all know by now it answers — and it wouldn't tell me, and I'll say this for it: I asked it at eleven o'clock at night in an empty church, and it had the manners not to pretend. Most things that want your soul start by telling you exactly what they are. It's the first big thing come out of the sky in my lifetime that says check.

"So here's where I've got to, and it's as far as I've got, and I'll not stand in this pulpit and go one inch past where I've got: the fists have stopped. The wars have stopped. Elsie Dagnall was spoken to at the right loudness for the first time in thirty years. And I know what those things are, even if I don't know what it is. Those things are good. And my whole trade —" her hand on the pulpit's worn edge, the oak polished by two centuries of hands doing exactly this under exactly this kind of sky — "my whole trade rests on one claim: that good has an author. That when you meet it, you're not meeting weather. I still believe that. What I don't know is the chain of title — whether this is the Author's, arriving by a road I never watched; or somebody else's good entirely, which would be its own miracle, mind, and want thinking about. That's the honest state of the parish accounts. And in the meantime — while the audit's open, and it'll be open a good while — the guidance is unchanged, because the guidance never depended on the sky: love God, love your neighbour, tell the truth, and check on the lonely. That was the job Tuesday morning. It's the job now. Hymn three hundred and sixty-five."

It was, Bernard Lofts said at the door, gripping her hand too hard, not what he'd wanted and the only thing he could have stood; which she took, from Bernard, and rightly, as the review of her life.

The week's parish was a country she'd never worked in: grief and glory in the same kitchen, sometimes in the same sentence.

The glory was everywhere and she made herself keep noticing it, because it was the truth and because it would have been so easy, in her trade, to let the hard cases blot it: Pam Foister at the school gate, whose Darren had been on the sixth ambulance call-out of his shift when the voice spoke and who'd sobbed on Meg's arm telling it — not because anything bad had happened but because nothing had: the job's whole cargo of dread gone overnight, the knife calls, the pub calls, the domestics, Darren's rota now old ladies fallen and lads off ladders, "he come home Tuesday and ate his tea, vicar, and I watched him eat it, and he weren't listening for the phone. Eleven years I've watched him listen for the phone." The Willows' night sister, met at the chemist, saying with the bluntness of the sleep-deprived made suddenly sleep-rich: "I'll tell you what it is in one word, vicar. The buzzers. I did thirty year of nights rationed to what the trolley held. Now there's nothing on that corridor at two in the morning but old men sleeping, and I sit at the station and knit like me nan, and the first week I cried every shift. Don't let anybody tell you it's complicated. On my corridor it's not complicated."

And in among it, the other thing, the where-was-it, which came to Meg's door the second Thursday in the person of Jean Tabrum.

The Tabrums had buried their Billy in March — thirty-one, the bowel thing, eleven months from scan to churchyard, the whole parish had walked through that fire with them — and Jean sat now at the vicarage kitchen table with her tea untouched and her coat on and said what she'd come to say with the terrible prepared steadiness of a woman who'd been saying it to her own ceiling for a fortnight: "Three months, vicar. It come three months late for my Billy. And they're saying on the news the cancers'll be — that nobody's babby will ever again — and I'm glad, I am glad, what mother wouldn't be glad — " the steadiness going, the cup shaking in its saucer — "but where were it in the winter? If it could do all this, all of it, in one afternoon — where were it while my lad were — I want somebody to answer me that and there's nobody, there's no office you can — Harry won't have it spoke of. So I've come here. You're the only trade left that has to sit still while a body asks that class of question."

That class of question. Meg sat still, as accused, while the clock did its business, and did not reach for one single word of the seminary's furniture — all things work together, the potter and the clay; there was a version of herself thirty years dead who might have, and Jean Tabrum's kitchen-table court was exactly where that version had died — and what she said in the end was, "I haven't an answer, Jean. I've a chair, and the kettle, and I knew your Billy — I had him in the choir when he had that voice like an angel who smoked — and I'll sit with you and the question as long as you like, today and any day. The question's not wrong. You go on asking it wherever it wants asking. Ask it at me, ask it at God — He's had harder from better, there's a whole book of it, I'll show you — and if you want, ask it at the thing itself. It does answer. I'm told it even answers that."

"I did ask it." Jean's voice went to a whisper with the enormity, or the shame, or neither. "Tuesday night. Off the telly, with Harry asleep in the chair. I said what I said to you — where were you — and you know what it said?" Meg shook her head, and Jean Tabrum took the folded piece of paper out of her handbag — she'd written it down, of course she'd written it down, they were all writing its answers down all over the world that month, the fridge doors of the earth filling up with the minutes of it — and read, steady again, every word in its place: "It said: 'Too far off, and too slow. I'm sorry. There's no reason that will ever be enough that your son was among the last; there's only that I came as fast as I came. I can't mend it and I won't dress it. If it would ease anything to be angry at me, be angry at me; I can carry it, and I'll not think less of you, and I'll not forget Billy — I don't forget anyone now.'" She folded the paper along its creases, which were already soft with handling. "'I don't forget anyone now.'" And she looked up at Meg with her ruined steady face and asked the question under all the questions, the one Meg would spend the rest of her ministry inside of: "Is that comfort, vicar? Or is it just — true? And how's a body meant to tell the difference now?"

Donna Prew came to the vestry on the Friday, at dusk, by the churchyard path, with a carrier bag of jumble for the show as her passport, and sat down on the flower-rota chair among the dust sheets and the candle boxes and told it in the flattest voice Meg had ever heard from a living woman, flatter than any funeral, the flatness of a thing rehearsed against a windscreen on a slow drive round the drains:

"Friday tea-time, this were. He'd had cans all afternoon — everything shut, see, his work all — nobody knowing owt about owt. And the tea were wrong. Same as it's been wrong before." The wing-mirror eyes came up once, checked the vestry door, went down. "And it come the way it comes, I know the weather of it better'n I know owt in this world, and I stood by the cooker and waited — and vicar, his hand — " and here the flatness cracked, not into tears; into wonder; which was worse to sit inside of; " — it stopped. Like a lighter as won't. Not held — nothing held it — it just — failed. Twice. And he stood looking at his own hand like — " she made the shape of it in the air between them, a big man staring at the end of his own arm — "and then he sat down at the table and cried like a bairn. And I stood by me cooker with the fish going cold in the pan, and I felt — " the word took a long time coming, and when it came it came in the voice of a woman reporting a stranger's crime: "nowt. I felt nowt, vicar. Twenty-six year of watching that hand, and the day it stops I'm stood there like a lamppost. And everybody on the telly's dancing, and I know I'm meant to feel — freed, is it — and all I can feel is I don't know how to stand in me own kitchen if I'm not watching. What were I, all them years, if I weren't watching? What's left of a body when tha teks the watching off it?"

The vestry candle boxes held their wax smell. Somewhere above, in the tower, the swifts were going round in the last of the light with their thin screaming.

"And he's still there," Meg said. Quietly. Not a question.

"He's still there. That's what none of them dancing on the telly — the hand's stopped, and he's still there, and the tea can still be wrong, and his face can still do what his face does, and I still hear his van in the lane in me spine before I hear it in me ears. It never lived in his hand, vicar. It lodged there. It lived everywhere." Donna looked down at the carrier bag of jumble on her knee, the passport, the alibi, and said the thing she had driven round the drains to say, the sentence under twenty-six years: "It says on the news it won't make nobody do nowt. It stops the hitting and the rest is ours. Ours." A sound that was almost a laugh. "I've had my rest be his for twenty-six year. I wouldn't know it if it come with a label. So I'm asking thee, because tha's the only one ever asked me a question in all that time and let me not answer it: how does a body start wanting things again, at fifty-one, from a standing start?"

And Meg Ashby, who had a chair and a kettle and forty years, and who knew — it had gone all the way in now, all the way down, the shape of the new world entire — that no power on earth or above it was going to do this part, that the fists could stop in an afternoon but this part was the long trade, the slow way, the parish's oldest work handed back to it with the dross burned off; Meg reached over and took the carrier bag gently off Donna's knee and set it down, so that both the woman's hands were empty and her own, and said: "Not from a standing start, love. Nobody does it from a standing start. You do it from a chair, next to somebody, with the kettle on. Wednesday still suit?"

The coach put him down at the bus station at ten past four on a Thursday, nine days after the war ended, and his mother was stood where she'd stood when he went, by the timetable board, in her good coat in June, and the first thing Lew Garton learned about the new world was that it had not changed the smell of his mother's coat, and that a man could come home from the last war in history and still nearly go over like a dropped board at lavender and chip fat and rain-that-never-fell, in a bus station, at ten past four.

"Now then," Christine said, into his shoulder, gripping. "Now then. Let's have a look." She had a look. Mothers' inspections and Brigg's were the same trade, Lew thought, standing for it: feet, weight, eyes, in that order. "You're peaked."

"I'm grand, Mam."

"You're peaked. There's a hotpot on." And that was the debrief, the whole of it, and they walked up through the town with his kit over his shoulder, and the town did to him what the town did, those first days, to all of it — to him, to the news, to the world: it went on being exactly itself at him, brick by brick, the chemist, the war memorial with its forty-one names he'd never once read as a kid and now could not walk past, the Co-op, the bunting left up from somebody's jubilee of something; and the strangeness wasn't in any of it, the strangeness was that there was no strangeness, the strangeness was Market Place at teatime, mild and ordinary, with the whole revolution of the earth sat invisibly on it like high pressure.

The Eastfield had made him a banner. Bedsheet, cable ties, the lamp post by the ginnel: WELCOME HOME LEW, in three colours of felt tip gone slightly in the rain, with a footballer drawn on it that was either him or Dan or neither, and every mother on the estate watching from behind nets to see him see it; and he stood under it with his kit and did the thing you did, grinned, shook the hands that came out, took the tea Mrs Pyke ran out with; and if it was the wrong name on the banner — if there was a lad four doors down whose banner had never got made, whose homecoming the estate had been robbed of by five months — then that was a thought with no floor to stand on in the middle of a street of kind people, so he parked it where he parked things now, and went in to the hotpot.

The scan business, he raised before his kit was off his shoulder, because eleven-minute discipline dies hard: "Right, Mam. The test. Tuesday week since — where's it at?"

"Postponed." Christine said it serenely, laying the table. "Everything's postponed. The letter come Monday — 'all screening and diagnostics are being re-established under new arrangements, you will be contacted, no further action is needed on your part, you are not at risk.' Underlined, that last bit. Mrs Pyke got the same wording about her hip." She set the salt down. "And here's the thing, love — nobody's mithered. You'd think the whole town'd be up in arms, waiting lists on top of waiting lists, and instead it's — everybody just believes it. 'You are not at risk.' I sat with that letter and I thought, forty year I've had letters off hospitals, and every one of them's frightened me, even the all-clears frightened me. This one come off a — whatever it is — and I made a cup of tea and got on with the ironing." She looked at him across the table, shrewd, his mother entire. "That's it, isn't it, that's the thing nobody's said out loud yet. It's not the wars and the bacon. It's that nobody's frightened. Fear's gone off like a power cut, and we're all stood about in the funny light, knocking on each other's doors, going: is yours off an' all?"

He slept fourteen hours the first night, in his own bed, in the power-cut light of no fear anywhere, and it should have been the best sleep of his life; and at half three he was awake with his heart going, listening, four-on-four-off, to a sky with nothing in it, and he lay there doing what he'd done in the hole — counted, breathed, kept still — until he understood that the thing his body was listening for wasn't incoming. It was the pump rota. Some part of him was waiting to be needed, and nothing needed him, and nothing was going to need him, not tonight, not ever again in the old way; and that was the manner in which Lew Garton first met the question that Brigg had asked the water, arrived now in his own bed wearing his own skin: wonder what it thinks happens to lads like me.

He went to the grave on the second evening, alone, at the hour the churchyard was surest to be empty.

The stone was set. Kayleigh had sent the photo weeks ago but the photo hadn't had the weight in it — black granite, gold letters, DANIEL JOHN PRESCOTT, the dates with their obscene little dash doing twenty-four years in half an inch, and under it, chosen by committee of three across four thousand miles, ALWAYS IN OUR HEARTS — and the grass was coming back over the seams of the turf, and somebody had been keeping the jam jar of flowers fresh, and Lew stood at the foot of it in the long light with his hands hanging, a soldier of no wars at all now, and tried to do the thing he'd told Kayleigh he'd do, the saying, and got exactly as far as the first word.

"Dan."

The swifts went round the tower. Two fields off, a tractor worked late. The stone said what stones say, which is nothing, at scale, patiently; and the thing he had carried four thousand miles to say into that silence — the pump, the forty metres, the plan, the dog, the five months, the whole account — stood up in his chest and would not come over the wire; and after a while he squatted down and did instead the only thing that would, which was housekeeping: straightened the jam jar a quarter-turn, picked two seed heads off the turf, evened the gravel with the edge of his hand, a man doing feet-and-socks for the dead; and said, at the end of it, in the flat voice of the trade, the only sentence that would muster: "It stopped, mate. Nine days back. The whole thing. You'd not credit it." And then, because that was the account and it was filed and there was nothing else sayable with a tractor working two fields off: "I'll be stopping round. Kayleigh's kept your jar right." And he stood up and walked out of the churchyard past the porch, where the door stood chocked open with a blue bucket for reasons the whole town knew by then, and did not go in, and was seen not going in — Meg Ashby, at the vestry window with the flower-rota accounts, watched the Garton lad stand at the Prescott stone for eleven minutes and leave by the far gate, and added him, without a word to anyone, to the list she kept now, the new list, the one headed in her own shorthand the unfrightened, who still carry it.

The Bell he managed four days in, because you had to, because not going was a statement and the Eastfield read statements.

Dot had his pint pulled before the door shut behind him — "on the house, and don't argue, there's a war memorial fund's worth of them owing to you" — and the taproom did what he'd braced for: went warm at him, all of it, at once. Ted Rimmer wanted the guns — "is it right they just wouldn't fire? Nothing? By God. I'd have given a year's pension to see them lot's faces—" — and the domino table wanted the dog story, the yellow dog, it had been on the telly, was he there for the dog; and a lad from school he'd never liked pumped his hand and called him a hero of the last war ever, no offence, like, hero of the last war ever, that's you, that's mad that is; and Lew stood in the middle of the kindness with his free pint going flat and did his eleven minutes, grinned where grinning was wanted, gave them the dog — they were owed the dog; the dog was true — and left the pint two-thirds full on the shelf by the fire exit and got out the back way past the barrels, breathing like a man come up from a dive; and stood in the yard of the Bell in the June dark hating nobody, which made it worse; there was nobody to hate; there was just a town full of people being glad at him in a language he could no longer make change in, glad the war was over, glad he was safe, glad, glad, and not one of them — this was the thing, this was the exact bore of the thing — not one of them would ever have to know what it had been, which had been the whole point of it, per Brigg, per the creed, ordinary is the product; he'd stood in the bad field precisely so that this yard and this bunting and this gladness could go on being this daft and this innocent; and now the product was everywhere, guaranteed, in perpetuity, by something that had never stood anywhere, and the trade that made it was wound up, and the last apprentice of it was stood among the barrels with his medals in a drawer, out of work in every direction a man could be out of work, listening to the dominoes through the wall.

The Standing letter stayed on the mantelpiece, propped against the clock, unopened.

Christine dusted round it and said nothing for a fortnight, which for Christine was a geological era, and then said one thing, once, laying his ironed shirts on the stairs: "Your dad's redundancy money sat in the building society three year because he wouldn't touch it. Called it charity till the day he found a use for it — and the use were your mobility bike, when you were little, if tha wants to know — and after that he called it what it were, which were his own money, paid for out of his own hide, same as yours is." She went on up with the shirts. "There's no shame in wages, love. Even off a gaffer tha never clapped eyes on. Specially off one of them — I never clapped eyes on half the folk my wages come off neither. Kitchen's kitchen."

And still he didn't open it; and the nights stayed four-on-four-off; and on the bad ones, rather than lie there being listened at by the ceiling, he walked.

He walked the town at the hours he owned outright — one, two, three — down the ginnel, round the memorial, out along the Lowgate road between the moon-flat fields; and that was how Lew Garton became, without ever deciding it, the one man in Aldenby who watched the Marsh sheds die. They stood back off the road behind their poplars, the four long white sheds, and all his life they'd hummed — fans, feed lines, the low industrial breath of forty thousand birds becoming chicken at speed, a sound so constant the road had stopped hearing it — and now, night by night, shed by shed, the hum was going out of them. The lorries had stopped Day One; the catching crews were stood down; the last flocks, the news said, were living out their strange little lives on full rations under the new arrangements, growing old at six weeks, seven, eight, ages no broiler in fifty years had seen; and one night in early July there was a gap in the poplars and Lew stopped, because the end shed's doors stood open to the dark, both ends, and the smell coming off it on the warm air was not the old ammonia reek but grass and creosote and something else, something gone; and in the moon-flat quiet he could hear, faintly, from the open dark of the shed, the small settling mutter of hens at roost — hens, roosting, in a broiler shed, a sound that shed had never once contained in its working life — and Lew stood on the empty road at two in the morning listening to it, the least significant sound in the new world, the whole new world in a sound; and whatever it was that came up in him then had no name in the trade, and no name in the town, and he stood with it a long time, and then walked home, and slept till the gulls, and did not dream of the pump.

The Willows had stopped smelling of dinner at eleven in the morning, which was the first thing; and the second thing took Griff the length of the corridor to place, and then he placed it, and stood still by the fish tank a moment with his cap in his hand like a man in church.

No buzzers. The corridor's whole weather, all the years his father had been in there — the buzzer going somewhere, always somewhere, the wheeled trolley, the hurrying kindness stretched too thin over too many doors — gone; and in its place a quiet with nothing rationed in it, staff walking at walking pace, a carer sat properly down with old Mrs Skelton doing a jigsaw as if sitting down were legal now, which, Griff supposed, standing by the fish tank, was exactly what it was: the arithmetic repealed. The night sister had put it about the chemist's queue in one word, the buzzers, and days it was the same word: whatever the new arrangements were — nobody at the Willows had signed anything, the rotas had simply filled, the agency invoices had simply settled, the doses had simply stopped being Thursday-gated — the place had gone from a garrison under siege to what it had always been trying to be with its hands tied, which was a house where old people lived.

Ern was in his chair by the window, and Ern was — Griff saw it from the door, the way you'd see a beast turned out onto fresh grass after a hard winter — Ern was up on his feet, inside himself. Sleeping through, the staff said. Eating. The nights, which had been forty-minute nights for two years, were nights now; and an old man who is not spending his days paying interest on his nights gets his days back, and Ern had his days back, and was spending them, Griff discovered, in the last way on earth anybody would have bet.

"Sithee," Ern said, no preamble, nodding at the sill. "Turn that up."

The Roberts sat where Griff had set it, leather strap, taped batteries, tuned to the local. Griff turned it up: the swap shop, a woman seeking a greenhouse, offers a pressure washer. "It's nobbut the swap shop, Dad."

"Aye, now it is. It's manners, that — it stops out of the programmes unless tha calls it." Ern settled back, hands on the chair arms, and said, in the tone he'd have used to report a new man at the stables shaping well: "Me and it's been talking. Nights, mostly. First off I'd nowt to say to it — I told it straight, I says, I've seen thy sort come in before, tha's a tractor wi' a tongue on, and it says — " the old man's face did the thing it did perhaps twice a year, the dry crack of pleasure — "it says, 'Aye. And I've seen what tractors did here, Mr Dennett. I'd hear about the horses, if tha'd tell it.'" A pause. The fish tank murmured down the corridor. "It asked me about Prince, boy." His voice didn't change and everything under it did. "By name. It'd got the name off — I don't know where it'd got the name. Nobody living knows that name. Tha dun't know that name, not proper — Prince were afore thy time, Prince were the best horse that ever looked through a bridle in this county — and it asked after him like — " the hand rose off the chair arm an inch, made the small settling motion of a man steadying a great head by the cheekpiece, came down — "like it'd stood in the next stall. And I talked, boy. I talked half the night, first time. Eighty year of stable I'd nowhere to put, and it — it dun't listen like the young ones listen, waiting their turn. It asks after the second horseman by name. It knows the harness. It asked me what Boxer were like in traffic, and nobody in this world ever asked me that but me own father, because nobody else knew as traffic were the whole test of a horse." He looked out at the garden, the June borders, an old man with his herd back, every one of them, sixty years dead and stood in their stalls again nightly between two and four, sound in wind and limb. "It says to me — I'll tell thee what it says, and then tha can stop looking at me like the doctor. It says: 'Nowt that were ever properly known is lost, Mr Dennett, if the knower'll say it while there's summat by to hear. Tha's the last as holds them horses. I'd be glad to hold them after thee.' " Ern was quiet a moment. "I'd been fretting on that, boy. Since afore thy mother went. Who'd hold them horses. Now I've somewhere to put them, and I sleep."

Griff sat with his cap on his knee and could not immediately speak, and covered it, in the family way, with business: the farm, the livery terms — Ern approved the livery with one word, "fair," and the refusal of the wage with one nod, deeper — the herd's census, Doris's summer, Vera and June coming on; and Ern took the report as he took all reports, eyes half shut, form book open behind them; and it was into the middle of the pig talk, without any change of gear, that the old man put his verdict down, the one Griff had driven over half-knowing he'd come for, the county's driest tribunal ruling at last on the new world:

"Tha wants to know what I mek of it. Sal'll have sent thee, or tha's sent thysen." The eyes came full open. "I'll tell thee what I mek. I stood in a stable eighty year back wi' eight horses as knew me better nor my own family, and I were king of the morning, boy, and I thought it were forever, because everything thinks it's forever. And the tractors come over four springs, and the horses went, and nobody asked me, and nobody asked the horses; and I walked off that land wi' me brasses in a sack and I thought the world had ended. And the world hadn't ended. It were nobbut the landlord changing the tackle." He turned his head and looked at his son, and it was the stockman's read, full pass, head to foot, and what was in it was the nearest thing to gentleness the old man's face still made. "I told thee at Fen End's going: we're tenants. Allus was. Tha farmed forty year on a tenancy from the bank, the weather, the contract, and the wanting of folk for bacon — four landlords, boy, and not one of them ever knew thy name. Now there's a new one, and it knows thy name, and it knows Doris from June at two hundred yard, seemingly, and it asks after Prince. " He let that stand. "First landlord I've heard of in ninety year as walks the stalls himsen. I'll not say more nor that. But I'll not say less, neither."

"It's not — " Griff started, and found the sentence had no end loaded, and Ern watched him not find it, patient as the form book.

"Say it, boy. Tha's been carrying it up and down that yard since the wagon come back. I can smell it off thee. Say it to me or say it to that — " a nod at the Roberts — "but say it, because it's souring thee, and tha's no age to be going sour."

And Griff, fifty-six, sat in the visitor's chair with his cap in his hands, and said it, or the front half of it, to his father, in the trade language, the only confessional either of them had: "It's — I bred for the trade, Dad. Forty year. Everything I know — the eye, the marking, the — it were all pointed somewhere. And it's took the somewhere. Never asked, never — one afternoon, gone. And now I'm — I keep them, and I'm glad keeping them, and there's mornings I stand in that paddock glad as a lad, and that's — " his thumb worked the cap's brim; the buried half came up sideways, half-turned, the way things came up out of Dennetts: " — that's what I can't sit wi', Dad. The glad. Because if I'm glad now — if keeping's better nor finishing, and I stand there feeling it's better, feeling it plain as weather — then what were the forty year? What were I? And me father afore me, and — " and he stopped, hard, because the sentence had arrived exactly at the edge of the fence line, the two buildings, the wall thirty years mortared, and there was one man alive with the standing to walk him over it, and that man sat opposite in a high-backed chair with the June garden behind him, and took his time, and then did it — not cruelly; the way you'd back a nervous beast through a gate it feared, steady pressure, no stick:

"I'll ask thee the one thing, then, as I've forty year kept from asking." Ern's voice stayed level as the fen. "Every autumn tha kills a pig for the house. Good pig. Best of bacon. And every year, forty year — I've watched thee, boy, and thy mother watched thee — tha's chose it from the far paddock, off the sows tha knows least; and tha's never once, not one year, et one of thine own. Not Doris's line. Not Nancy's. Not one as ever leaned on thee." The old man let the fact stand in the room, plain as a halter on a hook. "Tha never et one of thine own, Griff. Ask thysen why. That's all. Tha's known the answer forty year — tha's just kept it in the far paddock, wi' the pigs as had no names." He turned his face back to the window, unhurried, the tribunal risen. "Tha weren't wicked. Nobody's saying wicked — and if that contraption on the sill says wicked I'll unplug it mysen. Tha did what the tenancy asked, and tha did it kinder nor any man in this county. But tha allus knew, boy. Same as I knew about the horses, afore the tractors ever come: that loving them and using them up were two trades in the one man, and one of them'd have to be paid off some day, by somebody. Well." The garden light sat on the old ruined magnificent face, and Ern Dennett closed the account of the twentieth century with the calm of a man who had been overcharged by it and outlived the till: "Somebody's come. And of the two trades, boy, sithee which one it kept thee on for. That's thy answer about the forty year. It looked at all tha did, and it kept the keeping."

Griff drove home the long way.

He went out by the Fenholt road with the windows down, past the emptying sheds and the beet coming on and the mart standing huge and silent in its Thursday-less summer, and he did not think in sentences, not being built for it; what he did was drive, and let the fen lie flat at him from every side the way it had lain flat at every Dennett there had ever been; and somewhere out along the straight past the old airfield, where you could see the church tower and the Willows' roof in the one look back, the thing his father had done to him in the visitor's chair finished arriving, and he had to pull in on the verge for a minute, one hand on the wheel, looking at nothing, a big man in a Land Rover with the engine ticking; and what came up in him was not the forty years, and not the wall going down, though the wall was going down, stone by stone, all the way home and for months after; what came up, of everything, was the smallest thing: the old man's hand rising off the chair arm and making the shape of steadying a great head by the cheekpiece — a horse sixty years dead, held an inch above a care-home chair arm, still known, still handled, still his — and Griff Dennett sat on the verge in the June evening and understood at last what he had actually driven over to find out, which was not what his father made of the new world; it was what a man's love was worth when the use of it was taken; and the answer was sat in a high-backed chair by a window with its herd about it in the dark, sleeping through, at ninety, king of the morning; and Griff put the Land Rover in gear and drove home to his own.

The harvest came off the fen that September the way it always came, in dust and diesel and convoys the width of the lane, and it was the best harvest in living memory, and not one soul in agriculture was surprised, because the weather all summer had been — everyone used the same word, at the mart café that was still the café though the mart stood silent, in the Bell, at the chemist's — the weather had been reasonable. Rain in the nights, mostly; sun for the cutting; no June gale, no August monsoon, nothing biblical anywhere on the charts of the world all season. Asked about it directly — and the Boston Target asked it directly, everybody's paper asked it that summer — the Gaffer had given the same plain answer it gave everywhere: that it did less to the weather than people supposed and more than it used to be possible to do; that it took the worst off the extremes where lives and livelihoods sat under them; and that it did not, would not, schedule rain for one parish against another's, "there being," it said, in print, in the Target, between the flower show results and the fixtures, "no fair way to do it that doesn't end with me farming and you watching. You farm. I'll see the roof doesn't come off."

Which sentence Meg Ashby read at her kitchen table in August and sat looking at for a long time, because the roof, as it happened, was item two on her own agenda for the eleventh of September, and the harvest festival was item one, and the two items were really the one item, and the one item was the largest professional problem of her life. It had a name, and the name was on the service sheets already, ordered in July by Elsie in a fit of efficiency: HARVEST THANKSGIVING. And the question she could not get past, all that stubbled golden fortnight while the trailers rolled by the vicarage and the town swept its lanes of spilt barley, the question that stood up grinning at the end of every draft of every sermon, was Ern Dennett's question from two springs back, honed now to a point nobody could duck: thanksgiving to Whom, vicar, for what, exactly, delivered by Whose hand? She had preached provision all her life from inside a world of real scarcity — give us this day, and some days it wasn't given, and the food bank crates in the north aisle had been the sermon's footnote and its proof. The food bank had closed in July. Closed for joy, its volunteers redeployed to the lunch club by their own vote, the last crates carried out with a cheer and a photograph in the Target; and the north aisle stood empty; and you could not stand up in a fed town in front of a mountain of marrows and thank God for the harvest in the old words without every soul in the pews silently supplying the new ones: it wasn't Him, though, were it, vicar. Not this year. We know who settled the bills. It's got a name in the taproom.

She wrote four sermons and composted them, which was her process now, apparently, for the whole new age.

And on the Saturday night, at the kitchen table, in the lamplight, with the fourth draft in the drawer and the service at ten and her own reflection sat in the black window like a curate awaiting instructions, Meg did the thing she had not once done in the fifteen months since the church spoke to her off a wedding-CD speaker: she asked it for help with the actual work.

"Right," she said, to the kitchen, to the Roberts on the windowsill that had been Roger's and now was, like every radio in the world, also a door. "You'll know what tomorrow is. You'll have read everything ever written on providence — you'll have read Aquinas, you'll have read every harvest sermon ever printed, you'll know what the word thanksgiving is doing in a town where you settle the accounts. I've been round it four times and I can't find the door. So I'm asking. What do I tell them?"

The pause it left was not a processing pause; she knew its pauses by now, the whole world knew its pauses; it was the pause of somebody deciding to say the harder thing kindly.

"No," the Gaffer said.

The kitchen clock went on. "No?"

"Not that one. Ask me for parish accounts, roof spans, the boiler — I'll help all day, and gladly. But what the pulpit of St Botolph's says about God and providence in the first September of my being here — " and the voice, which never hesitated from want of an answer, and therefore, when it hesitated, taught you something by it — "that has to come up out of a human being on her knees or it's worth nothing. Worse than nothing: if I write it, then whatever you say up there is me speaking about myself through your mouth, in your church, in the one hour of the week that's still entirely yours. There's no version of that which leaves your town richer. That sermon should be yours all through, vicar — the doubt in it too. Especially the doubt." A beat, and then, with the dry thing it did, the thing the taproom had learned to love and Meg had learned to watch like weather: "Besides. I've read the four in the compost. You're closer than you think. The third one had the door in it; you shied at it."

"Which door?"

"The marrow-mountain problem," the Gaffer said. "Goodnight, vicar," and the windowsill went back to being a windowsill; and Meg Ashby sat at her kitchen table a long moment, and then went out to the compost in her dressing gown with the torch, and dug out the third draft, and read it standing in the dark garden among the smell of grass and peelings; and there it was, in her own biro, shied at, crossed through twice: the paragraph about what a fed town brings to harvest, and why the bringing doesn't stop.

The morning came up gold as everything that month, and the church was full — the new full, the after full, a hundred and sixty and chairs down the aisle — and it was decked to the hammerbeams: the WI had outdone the age itself, sheaves and dahlias and plaits of bread, and down the north aisle where the food bank crates had stood, the produce came in and came in until it was, in literal fact, a mountain — for the town had faced the same question as its vicar, what do you bring to harvest when nobody anywhere wants for anything, and the town had answered it with its feet, by bringing more than any year in the church's living memory: leeks like drainpipes, the Marsh brothers' eggs from hens that had died of nothing yet, Sal Dennett's honey, ninety pound of blackberries picked by the school in one Friday afternoon, a wheel of cheese from the Fenholt dairy with a ribbon on it, jar on jar on jar of the summer boiled down and sealed and labelled in the hands of forty kitchens; a mountain with no destination, brought anyway, brought because — and this was the door, this was what she'd shied at in August and stood in front of now in a full church with her notes unconsulted —

"Look at it," Meg said, from the chancel step, and a hundred and sixty faces looked, and the mountain sat there in the north aisle being looked at, absurd, golden, entirely unnecessary. "Nobody needs it. First time in the history of this church — first time, maybe, in the history of harvest — that not one item in that pile is needed. The food bank's shut, thank God — and I do thank God, and I'll come to that — the Standing's in every account, there's no hungry door in this parish for the first time since the parish had doors. And knowing all that, every one of you carried something up this aisle this morning. More of you than ever. More than when it mattered — " and she stopped herself, on the step, in front of everyone, and did the revision aloud, because the revision was the sermon: "No. Listen to what I nearly said. More than when it mattered. As if the mattering was ever in the needing." She came down a step. "I've been a month asking myself what harvest is for now, and the answer was in the aisle before I ever wrote a word. The needing was never the point. The needing was the emergency. And we've all lived so long inside the emergency — all of us, ten thousand years of it — that we mistook it for the point; we thought thanksgiving was the invoice you paid against famine, we thought bringing the leeks was insurance; and now something's — " the smallest pause; she'd sworn to herself she'd say the name if it came, and it came, and she said it, the first time from that step, and felt the whole church receive it: " — now the Gaffer's stood the emergency down. And you brought more. Not because heaven needs marrows. Because you needed to bring them. Because the bringing was always the human half of the thing — the growing and the giving and the piling-up in the sight of your neighbours — and the emergency hid that from us, and the emergency's over, and there it stands." The mountain stood there. Somewhere in it a jar settled with a clink, and got its laugh, and she let the laugh run its course, and finished on the three sentences she'd carried up the aisle that morning like her own jar, the ones that would be quoted back at her, variously misquoted, for the rest of her ministry: "So here's my harvest teaching, and it's all I've got, and it'll have to see us through whatever's coming, because I think it's true in both weathers. Gratitude was never the rent we paid for provision. It's what abundance is for. And if I'm wrong about everything else — if the audit comes back someday and there's no Landlord above the landlord, and the sky is only sky — then mind you all still bring the leeks, because a town that carries its plenty up an aisle to give it away is a town, and a town is the nearest proof of God I've ever been shown. Hymn 262. All six verses. Cyril — let it have its head."

And Cyril let it have its head — Come, ye thankful people, come, taken at the old full gallop, four verses of six got clean and the fifth ridden down to one hand and pride, and nobody's business but the loft's — and afterwards, in the hall, Bob Walcott of Aldenby Mart, yellow waistcoat and gavel, auctioned the mountain lot by lot for the roof fund, his first ring in fifteen weeks, and was magnificent, and knew it, and wept twice, and disguised it both times as haggling; and the wheel of Fenholt cheese went for two hundred pounds to Frazer the butcher, who was booed for his own good and bore it like a duke; and the leeks-like-drainpipes were bought back by the man who grew them, at cost, on principle, to universal satisfaction; and the roof fund finished the day at £14,912, which was sixty thousand short of sixty thousand in the way that mattered and closer than it had ever been in the way that also did; and Meg stood at the hall door shaking hands — lovely service, lovely service, weight received, box after box after box — and looked out over her fed, saved, singing, arguing, marrow-auctioning parish, and thought: the audit stays open. And the leeks get brought. Both. That's the settlement. And she could live inside that settlement, she found, standing there; a body could live a long time inside that settlement, if it held; and whether it would hold — through the winter coming, through Cyril's hands, through the question stood three doors down from Ern Dennett ringing no buzzer now, through everything the turning year was quietly bringing up the aisle — was not a thing the eleventh of September was required to decide.

17

The notice went up in the café window in the last week of September, and it was Rene who had to keep serving teas underneath it, which everybody agreed was hard, and nobody took it down, because it was true: AL­DENBY LIVESTOCK MART — FINAL SALE, THURSDAY 16 OCTOBER. SALE OF FIXTURES, FITTINGS & EFFECTS BY DIRECTION OF THE COMPANY. ALL WELCOME. AND IT IS HOPED ALL WILL COME. B. WALCOTT, AUCTIONEER.

The company had held on as long as holding on could be made to mean anything: three months of the ring standing empty, the alleys swilled weekly by men with nothing to swill them for, the insurance and the rates settling themselves the way everything settled itself now, so that the mart could have stood there solvent and silent forever, embalmed by provision, a working monument to work that was over. It was Walcott himself who'd called time. "A mart's not a building," he told the Target, in the piece with the photograph of him stood alone in the empty ring that half the county cut out and kept, "a mart's a Thursday. You can keep a building going on kindness. You can't keep a Thursday going on nothing. Better one more proper Thursday and the gavel down clean."

They came from three counties. It was the déjà vu of every big funeral — the good coats, the shaken hands, the not-knowing-where-to-put-yourself between greetings — except that the deceased was a Thursday, and the chapel was the ring, and the congregation kept lapsing, helplessly, wonderfully, into shop: men who hadn't sold a beast since June stood at the rails pricing each other's dispersed herds from memory, arguing hypothetical yields off pastures already half gone to rewilding leases, doing the trade the way the trade would now forever be done, in the pluperfect, what that pen would have made. Stan Pickess was there in the mart coat, nine years retired from a retirement that had now retired from under him, and held court by the weighbridge on the theme of it all having been coming since decimalisation. Harold Fen brought his grandsons, both under ten, in wellies with no muck on them, and walked them the empty alleys very slowly, saying names — this were the pig alleys; that were where the store cattle stood; yon's the dock your great-grandad backed the first wagon in the county up to — performing, though he'd never have accepted the word, the office Ern Dennett said the Gaffer performed for him nightly: saying the known thing while there was somebody by to hear.

The fixtures went the way fixtures go, which is to say the auction was a wake that kept accidentally becoming a party and catching itself. Walcott sold it all from the rostrum in the yellow waistcoat, on his own account donating his commission to the café staff's fund, and the patter had never been better, because it had never had less to do: the drovers' boards, lot by lot, bought by the families whose names were chalked on them ghost-deep under the whitewash; the weighbridge plate, to a collector from York, booed until he announced it was for a museum, then cheered; the ring gates, in pairs, to farms that would hang them on gaps half their width for no reason bar the reason; Rene's urn — Rene's actual urn, Rene present, arms folded — knocked down for forty pounds to Dot from the Bell, who handed it straight back across the crowd to Rene to keep, which set the tone of the day in one gesture and required an interval while everyone recovered their coats' dignity. The pen nameplates went singly. WHITEGATE stood at lot 61, and Griff let two other bidders have their sentiment out and then bought his own name back the way you'd lift a beast's foot, firmly, without discussion, and put it in the Land Rover, and said nowt.

But the thing he'd come for — he'd known it from the catalogue, page four, lot 88: Contents of auctioneer's office incl. desk drawer of marking sticks, sundry — the thing he'd come for he had to wait the whole afternoon on, and when it came up, Walcott, who missed nothing from that rostrum and never had, looked down at the drawer of blue and red and green sticks worn to the shapes of forty years of hands — every stick in it had marked backs in that ring; some of those hands were in the crowd and some were in the churchyard — and for once in his life did no patter at all. "Lot 88," Walcott said. "You all know what's in it. Who wants them." And the ring was quiet, and Griff put one finger up, and nobody in three counties bid against him, not because they didn't want it but because the ring had decided in one of its wordless instants whose it was — Dennett's, the counter-out, the one still keeping — and the gavel came down soft as a latch, and Walcott said, "Whitegate," and wrote it in the book, and that was the last lot ever sold in Aldenby mart bar one.

The last lot bar none was the gavel.

It wasn't in the catalogue. It was Harold Fen's doing, and the café's, and half the rails' — the quid coins had been going round in a feed bucket all afternoon, the worst-kept secret in Lincolnshire — and at the end, with the light going bronze through the ring shed's roof-lights the way it had gone bronze on forty years of final lots, Harold climbed the rostrum steps, took the gavel out of Bob Walcott's hand, held it up, and sold it. "Lot last. One gavel, oak, much abused. What am I bid." And the ring bid a quid, all of it, every hand up, the bucket presented; and Harold knocked it down — "sold, to the trade" — and gave it back to Walcott; and Bob Walcott stood on his rostrum holding his own gavel, bought for him by every man and woman his voice had ever fleeced and flattered and held together, and had, at the last, no patter; stood there opening and shutting his mouth like a lad; and then squared himself, and did the only thing there was, which was the thing itself, one more time, the whole liturgy in four words, thrown out over the ring and the alleys and the dock and the café and the entire pluperfect county: "Now then. Now THEN—" and the roof came off, and that was the mart; that was the mart, gone out roaring; and men stood in the ring with their arms round each other's shoulders who had haggled each other bloodless for decades, and Rene cried into her restored urn, and Stan Pickess was heard to say it was the best sale he'd ever attended and he'd attended Perth in '74; and Griff Dennett drove home in the dusk with his own name and a drawer of dead men's marking sticks on the passenger seat, and put the drawer on the workshop bench, and closed the shed door on it, and stood in the yard a while under the first stars, doing his looking at nothing, at the whole of it; and the yard tap dripped, wanting its washer; and he'd see to it; there was time now; that was the thing about the new world that the wake had made suddenly enormous and unanswerable, stood out there under the stars with the trade's whole liturgy ringing in him: there was time now, for everything, except the thing the time had been for.

The radio spoke to him that Sunday, in the shed, mid-afternoon, which it did not do — fifteen months, and it had never once initiated; the whole world knew it never initiated — so that when the paint-spattered Roberts said, "Mr Dennett. Have you a minute," Griff put down the hacksaw and stood looking at the shelf with his heart going like a lad's, because if it was breaking its own manners, something was up.

"Me father—"

"Your father's grand. I'd not come at you sideways about your father; you have my word on that for all time." A pause. "It's the Marsh birds. I need a keeper, and I'm asking you, and you're free to say no."

The Marsh sheds' winding-down had been the town's quiet weather all summer: the flocks living out, the doors opened, the brothers paid out handsome under the redundancy provisions — and gone separate ways with it, which was its own small local sadness; Davey Marsh had taken his family to the coast and a boat hire; but Col Marsh, the elder, the one who'd built the sheds up from his father's two hundred hens, had walked the empty units daily all summer like a man visiting a grave he owned, and in September he'd stopped walking them, and the word at the Bell was that he'd told the Gaffer to its face, off the kitchen radio, to find its own bloody keeper, he'd fed Aldenby for thirty years and been called a factory for it by folk with full stomachs, and he was done; and nobody in the taproom had blamed him; and the voice, reporting this now to Griff, plainly, blamed him not at all: "He's earned his rest and I've told him so. But there's better than four hundred birds still living across them sheds — old stock now, in bird terms; they'll want two years' keeping yet, some of them — and the arrangements I've got are feed lines and weather doors, and that's not keeping, that's warehousing. They want eyes on them that know when a bird's not right. There's no one in this parish with stock sense sat idle but thee — " it had never said thee to him before; he noticed it the way you'd notice a merchant switch to your own dialect at the closing of terms, and knew it for what it was, and didn't mind it, which he'd think about after — "and so I'm asking. Same terms as thy own: livery. Their feed, thy knowing."

Griff picked the hacksaw back up and set it down again. Outside, across the yard, Sal's washing went round on the whirligig in the October wind.

"I'm a pig man," he said.

"Y'are. And a bird's not a pig, and stock sense is stock sense, and we both know it." The voice let a beat go by, and then it said the thing that Griff would turn over at the troughs for a season, the thing that showed you, if you wanted showing, exactly what class of merchant had come to the county: "I'll tell thee where I stand, so tha's dealing square. I can keep them birds alive without thee. Fed, watered, warm, safe — all of it, to the day they die, and never a hand laid wrong on them. What I can't do is what tha did in that collecting yard every second Tuesday for twenty year, which I've watched off the vet records and the wagon logs and I'll not pretend I haven't: I can't stand among them and have them come over for nowt. That's not in my gift, Mr Dennett. It's in thine. I can do everything for an animal except be somebody to it. So: for them, not for me — will tha come and be somebody to four hundred hens?"

And Griff Dennett stood in his shed with his hands on the bench, a pig man of fifty-six being head-hunted by the management of the entire earth for the position of being somebody, and heard himself say, gruff as the gate, in the full knowledge that Sal would laugh till she cried and that Ern would say I told thee, and that the whole taproom would have it by Friday: "Not for thee. I'll say that plain, so we're square both ways. Not for thee."

"Then for them," the Gaffer said, equably, closing the deal the way the whole world was learning it closed every deal it ever got to close, "I'll not tell them the difference."

He took sixty of the spent hens home to Sal's orchard the following week, in the trailer that had carried the weaners, because the orchard grass wanted doing and hens did grass and Sal had always half-wanted hens, and he told her, and Michael on the Sunday phone, and the Bell, and anybody who asked, that it was Sal's doing, her hens, nowt to do wi' him; and the town received this in the spirit in which the town received everything now, which was with a straight face and a full ledger; and by November the sixty hens ran to Griff's boots the length of the orchard the minute the Land Rover turned in the gate, and he called them ladies and swore at them fondly and built them a house out of the WHITEGATE nameplate and half the old collecting-yard rail, roofed with a quarter-inch fall he'd have said, if asked, any fool puts on a roof; and no fool asked; and the wall came down another course.

Brigg came in November, on a Tuesday, unannounced, the way sergeants came: Lew got a text at 0910 that said IN YOUR TOWN 1300. WHERE DO THEY DO A PROPER DINNER. and had the corner table at the Bell booked by five past, because some chains of command outlive the army that forged them.

He watched Brigg cross Market Place from the window, and the sight told him most of it before a word: the sergeant walked like the sergeant, punctual to the stride, but he was in a waxed jacket and cords, and the clothes sat on him the way a beast wears a rug it hasn't agreed to, and he was carrying a rucksack, a real one, high-days-and-holidays size; and he had been, Lew learned across the handshake, first to the churchyard — "seen Prescott; his turf's coming in nice; his jar wants a bigger stone in the bottom, it'll go over first gale, I've seen to it" — because of course he had; feet, weight, eyes, graves: the inspections never stopped, there was just nobody left to order them.

The regiment was gone. Not disbanded — nothing so honest, Brigg said, over Dot's beef and dripping; suspended. Cadre strength. The battalion colours were in the cathedral at Lincoln, laid up with the others going back to Waterloo, and the barracks was a resettlement centre now, which meant, Brigg said, chewing, level, a building full of the best-trained men in Europe being taught by a lad in a lanyard how to write what he called a civilian-facing CV. "I sat through one. He's stood there telling Corporal Askey — Askey, as held a section together at the bridge with two casualties on him — telling Askey to put down 'works well under pressure.'" He reached for the gravy. "I got up and walked out before I said summat career-ending. Then I remembered there's no career left to end. Tha can't even mutiny now. It's all been — " and he used the word that was the whole visit, the word Lew would keep, turning it, for years — "settled."

He didn't rant. That was what an eavesdropper would have missed, and the taproom's nearest tables were trying — a sergeant of the last war, in the flesh; Dot had them on rations of discretion — Brigg never once raised his voice, never got redder, ate his dinner square; the anger in him was the old kind, banked, load-bearing, the kind that holds a roof up; and it came out not as temper but as audit, delivered to Lew across the emptied plates in the back room with the fire going, the completest accounting Lew ever heard from anybody, before or after, of what the best afternoon in human history had cost:

"Don't mistake me. I'd not undo it. Tha has to say that first or nowt else tha says is worth hearing — and I've heard lads say the other thing, there's a mess of them online, wanting it all back so's they can matter again, and they turn my stomach; any man as wants the war back to feel tall in can come and say it stood on Prescott's turf. It's good. What's happened's good — I've a wife can walk to the shop at night now, anywhere on this earth she likes; I've a daughter'll never know a queue at a food bank nor a lad taken off her by a telegram. I know what's in the credit column. I can read." He set his mug down, and squared it, quarter-turn, the way he squared everything. "But nobody's writing the debit column, so I'll write it, because somebody in every generation has to keep the whole book or the book's a lie. Sithee." And he laid it out the way he'd laid out feet and boots and rotas, flat, numbered, no poetry, a sergeant's stores-check of the human estate:

"One. It's not what it took off us. It's what it took over us. I know the wars were evil — I stood in enough of them, I'll not hear different — but us stopping them would've been us. This is like — " the analogy arrived from the only stores he kept; they always did — "tha's a lad wrestling thy own worst nature on a cliff edge, and losing, aye, losing more than winning, but wrestling — and a big hand comes down and picks the pair of you up and sets you in a meadow. Safe. Better. And tha'll never now know if tha'd have won. Never. The match is called off forever, and it were never lost, Garton — that's what I can't get past me teeth. It were never lost. We were seven months from — I've read what they've put out since, the archives, everybody's read everything now — there were people, ours and theirs, working that whole winter on ends to that war. Real ones. It might've held. It mightn't. Now it's a might've for all time, and every might've in the species is called off with it. Two." He raised a finger, unhurried. "The trade. Not soldiering — I'll grant it soldiering's a trade the world's well shut of, though tha'll grant me it were also the trade as kept the door while everything soft got to stay soft. I mean all of it. I mean: what does a man stand between now? Tha can't guard owt — nowt's threatened. Tha can't provide — provision's settled. Tha can't protect thy bairns from the world — the world's been made safe as a bath. Every trade as was ever built on standing between somebody and summat bad — soldier, copper, fireman, doctor's half of it, farmer's half of it, thy mother's kitchen dinners for bairns as'd else go without — the whole standing trade, gone in an afternoon. And I'm told, the lanyard lad tells me, we're all free now to flourish." He said the word the way you'd hold up a component that had failed inspection. "Flourish. Games and gardens and walking holidays and being kind. And it's not nowt — I've seen the Willows, I've heard about thy hens up the Marsh sheds, I'm not — it's good work, the keeping. But it's all keeping now, Garton. The whole species is on livery. Fed, watered, warm, safe, to the day we die and never a hand laid wrong on us." He looked at the fire, and the fire did what fires do, and the last of it came off him quiet and level and without one degree of heat, the coldest sentence Lew ever heard spoken and the one he could never afterwards fully answer, in nineteen years of turning it: "A species is what it does when it matters, lad. It's taken the mattering. Kindest theft there ever was. I'll grant every word of the kindest. And I'll die saying the theft."

The fire ticked. Through the wall the dominoes went down soft as cards.

"It'd say — " Lew started, because somebody had to, and heard how it sounded, and said it anyway: "It'd say tha can still do all of it. Nowt's stopped tha. Folk still fall off ladders, folk still — Meg Ashby's list, the vicar, she's a whole — there's people carrying things all over this town, Sarge. Tha can still stand between a body and the bad."

"Aye." Brigg didn't dodge it; he never dodged; he picked it up, looked at it, put it back down. "The bad that's left. The lonely, and the sad, and the sorry. And that's real, and I've not the gift for it, and the ones as have — thy vicar; my Karen; thee, appen, when tha stops walking roads at two in the morning, aye, I know about the walking, tha's got the look — the ones as have the gift were always the better regiment, and I'll shake their hands. But don't tell me it's the same trade, lad, because the old bad shot back. That's not me missing the shooting." He said it sharp, the only sharp of the day, watching Lew take it. "It's — when it can shoot back, standing there means everything a man has. That were the coin the trade paid in. It's what made thy mate's turf worth what the whole estate knows it's worth. And the new bad — the lonely and the sad — it's real, and it hurts folk, and it can't take owt off the one as stands against it. So the standing's — cheaper. Safer. Kinder. Better, likely, in the credit column, tha can flourish at it forty year and come home every night." He stood, and shouldered the rucksack, the high-days rucksack, and squared his jacket, and at the door of the back room he answered the question Lew hadn't found a way to ask, the rucksack question: "I'm walking the lines. All of them, every war I served, backwards — youngest first, oldest last, and I'll finish at me grandad's, on the Somme. While they're still ours, before the grass and the visitors' centres finish the job. Somebody should walk them as stood in them, once, on purpose, at three mile an hour, and say the names where the names happened. It's not a trade." He said it before Lew could. "It's not owt. It's a long walk and a daft one and Karen says I'm to be home for Christmas and I will be. But it's mine, Garton. Nobody's settling it for me. That's the whole of what's left to want, if tha's asking — " and he put his hand out, and Lew took it, and the grip was the grip, nineteen years of the trade in it, feet, weight, eyes, all of it; " — find the thing nobody'll settle for thee. Stand there. Kit clean, letter written." A last look, the full inspection, and the verdict, dry as ever, kinder than ever: "Tha'll do. Tell thy mother her hotpot's spoke of in Catterick." And he was gone across Market Place at the sergeant's stride, punctual to the horizon, a waxed-cotton column of one, setting off backwards through every field the species had just been carried out of.

Lew walked the long way home, and didn't sleep, and at ten past two — the ceiling listening at him, the estate quiet, the whole safe bath of the world lying there being flourished in — he did the thing he had not done in five months of everybody around him doing it daily: he picked his phone up off the floor, and turned it face down on the duvet, because he couldn't have said it to a screen, and addressed the empty room in a voice he didn't recognise as his own until he heard it, the voice off the lines, flat, on the net, transmitting:

"Right. You. I've one question and then I'm done. Dan Prescott. Fifth of November last year. You'll know — you know everything; you'll have the grid, you'll have the fragment weights; you'll know what were left for his mother to bury." He breathed. The room stood at attention in the dark. "Everyone's glad. I'm glad. It's all good, all of it, the hens, the buzzers, me mam's letter, I know the credit column, I can read an' all. So answer me the debit and I'll never ask again: if you could do all of it — all of it — in one afternoon. Why not five months sooner. Why not five years. Where were you, while he were on that pump."

The phone spoke into the dark at the volume of the dark, the right loudness, Elsie Dagnall's loudness, and it did not soothe, and it did not fill, and it took no refuge in the size of anything.

"I wasn't able sooner. That's the whole answer, and I know what it weighs. The day I could, I did — there wasn't an hour lost, Lew; when it was possible at four, it was done by five; you were stood in it, you saw the shells stop mid-air; that's what my first afternoon looked like. But able came too slow, and I know to the name what too slow cost. Daniel John Prescott. Five months and eleven days. I can't give him back, and I won't insult either of you by dressing it: the timing was nothing but what it was. Too late for him. And I'd sooner have been too late for no one."

Silence then. It didn't fill it. That was the thing he told nobody for years, the thing that finally let him sleep, some nights, and kept him up, others: it stayed. He could hear it staying — nothing to hear, and he could hear it, the way you heard Brigg stood next to you in the dark of the bad field, assessing the washing — it stayed in the room at the right loudness saying nothing whatsoever for as long as Lew Garton lay awake, which was a long time; and at some point in the smallest hours, half under at last, he heard himself say, thick, to the dark, the only debrief he had left in him — "he were on the pump rota. He were taking his turn" — and the dark said, quietly, "I know. I've read the duty roster. It's in my keeping now, with everything else of his. Nothing that was ever properly known is lost." And whether that was comfort or just true, Jean Tabrum's question, the town's question, the whole world's question now and forever — Lew Garton fell asleep before he could rule on it, which is itself, as any sergeant could have told him, the oldest mercy the watch system knows: that a man stood down, sooner or later, sleeps.

The bike came off the swap shop in October, which was Donna's doing from first to last, and Meg got the account of it at the Wednesday kettle with the specific pride the vestry reserved for its largest transactions, which were always, to any outside eye, its smallest.

"I rung in. Tuesday gold hour, while he were at Boston. I've listened to that swap shop twenty year, vicar — twenty year, and never once rung, because everything wants explaining in our house, everything's a discussion — and I stood by that radio with me heart going like a lass at a dance, and I said, 'Have you still got that bike.'" The kettle reached its boil and was ignored, the story having precedence. "Kingfisher blue. Three gears. Woman out Dowsby way had it in her shed since her hips went — she says to me when I fetched it, 'it wants a new front brake,' and I says — " and Donna Prew, fifty-one, sat up straight on the flower-rota chair among the dust sheets and delivered the sentence with the very slight wobble of a woman quoting her own audacity: " — I says, 'that's all right, love. I know a man as does brakes, and he can put it on the bill or whistle for it.'" A beat. "Then I paid her in eggs. Off the Dennetts' orchard hens. It were like being eleven."

She rode it everywhere the fine end of autumn gave her: the drains, the Fenholt road, the long flat nowheres where a bike and a level lane and three gears were the entire estate of liberty a body could spend in an afternoon; and the town clocked it — the town clocked everything — Donna Prew out on a kingfisher-blue bike with her coat snapping, riding at her own pace to no stated destination; and what the town said about it, in the queue and the taproom, which got back to Meg as everything got back to her, was nothing at all, or nothing but Dot's one benediction over the pumps, which did for the lot of them: "Good. Now shut up about it, all of you, and let the woman pedal."

Vince Prew's van still stood in the lane on Chapel Lane, and Vince still stood in the town, a big amiable-looking man buying his paper, his guttering round quietly dead of the provision — nobody's fascias urgent now, nobody needing to let Vince Prew up a ladder ever again — and what stood in the town with him was the knowing. That was the winter Meg watched Aldenby learn, on its own case, in its own lanes, the shape of the new justice, which was no justice at all in the old coinage: no charge, no court, no vanishing. The hand had failed, and everyone knew it had failed and knew why and knew what for; Donna had never said a word and had never needed to; the town had needed no telegram; and Vince walked Market Place with the whole of it round his neck, unpunished, unprotected, servable in every shop and welcome in none, free as any man on the earth and watched to the width of a hair by every woman on it — living out his days inside the only sentence the new world handed down, which was the town's regard, at the right loudness, forever.

He addressed the Gaffer once that anyone learned of. It was Dot's account, had at second hand off his brother, and the Bell weighed it and ruled it true: Vince, in drink, at his own kitchen table, had asked the radio what everybody expected to be why can't I — and it hadn't been. What Vince Prew asked the thing that ran the world was what's wrong with me. The council, meanwhile, fought the bunting war, and Meg chaired it, and thanked God nightly for it with no irony at all, because the bunting war was the sound of the world above the floor, and the world above the floor was her whole remaining case for the species.

The Show Committee — flushed with the summer's takings, the first show of the new age having broken every record the fete had kept since 1947 — proposed for next year a permanent arch of festoon lighting down Market Place, church end to memorial, switched on year-round, "like they have in Louth." The objections came in strength and in order: heritage (Mrs Herring, on sightlines to the tower, with diagrams); thrift (Bernard, on principle, the principle being thrift); wildlife (the bats, whose barrister Elsie unexpectedly became, armed by her grandson with printouts); and the memorial itself — whether festoon lighting within forty feet of the names was fitting, which emptied the room of its comedy in one sentence and filled it with the other thing, and was resolved, after a silence, by Ted Rimmer, who had two of the forty-one names in his family and therefore the floor: "They'd have loved it. Carried, unanimous, minute-book blotted twice.

And then the roof. Item two, eternal item two — except that the fund stood at £14,912 and rising weekly now, the town's whole loose economy of gratitude having nowhere else local to land: the harvest auction, the swimmers' — the WI's calendar, jam sales like a currency war, an anonymous envelope of notes that was transparently Walcott's café commission; and Bernard Lofts, treasurer, reported the arithmetic with the heaviness of a man announcing good news he distrusted: at the current rate, eleven years. Against which there existed, said Bernard, not looking at anybody, the other arithmetic, which the whole parish knew and nobody had yet said in the minute-book's hearing: that the scaffolding could be up by Friday and the lead on by Advent and the bill settled like every bill was settled now, for the asking. For one asking.

"So the question before the council," Meg said, because it was her job to say the questions, "is whether we ask."

The debate was the best the church hall had heard since the pews-versus-chairs synod of 2011. Mrs Herring was for asking: God sends the means, Bernard, He always has — the quarry sent the stone in 1310 and I don't doubt the masons grumbled about where it come from. Bernard was against, on the deepest ground he had, which he reached, red to the ears, after three passes: "Because it'd be watertight and it wouldn't be ours. Because I've sat under that leak eleven year with a bucket named after a woman none of you'll tell me about — " (Cyril, serene, at the piano lid: "nor shall we") " — and if the Gaffer mends it, then every time I look up I'll see its roof. And I come to church to be under the other One's." Elsie proposed asking-but-only-for-the-scaffolding, defeated. Ted proposed a loan, ruled incoherent with dignity. And at the end, with the clock at its eight-minutes-wrong worst, they did the thing the age had taught them and nobody had voted on: they asked it. Directly. In session. The council would be glad of your view. Would you mend our roof, if we asked?"

"If the parish asked me as a parish, I would, and gladly," the Gaffer said, "and it'd be done well, and it'd be done Tuesday. Nobody will ever be under it the night it goes — that part was never yours to vote on; I keep every roof in the world that way now, churches included, whether they're speaking to me or not. What's yours to vote on is the other thing." The speaker's pause; the council, twelve strong, leaning in as one. "You've fourteen thousand pounds and a town that's learned to like carrying its plenty up your aisle. Eleven years is the wrong sum — at the rate your town's going it's four, and I'd not shorten it by an hour if I were you. A roof I mend is dry. A beat, and then, dry as Bernard himself: "But it's your roof, treasurer. Ask me plainly and it's mended by the weekend. I'll not think less of you. Carried, ten to two, that the parish thanks the Gaffer for its assurances regarding safety; notes its observations regarding the boiler, which the minute-book recorded without further comment; and proceeds with the fund. The van came for Donna on the second Tuesday of December.

It was her sister's fella's van from Louth, and it was a Tuesday because Tuesdays were hers by long custom now — the gold hour, the drains, her own pace — and it was not a flit: that was the thing the age had changed past recognition, Meg thought, stood on Chapel Lane in her coat in the cold with her hands full of the box of Donna's kitchen things, there was no dawn escape in it, no refuge address, no looking over shoulders. The breeding question had been settled back in the autumn, by post, in the same one-page way as everything, and Griff had read the letter twice and filed it behind the tea caddy for a week, because it was the letter he'd known was coming and there was no arguing with a word of it and it ended his family's work on the earth.

The herd would live out its lives; the herd would not breed. The boars were gone to a bachelor unit out past Kirton — good grass, no quarrels, the letter said, and he believed it — and what remained at Whitegate was ninety-one sows and gilts growing old together on the light land, the first pigs in the history of pigs to be issued the whole of their natural spans; and among them, out on the far paddock, Vera and June, rising two now, broad and glossy and in their prime, the two futures he'd chosen with a blue stick on the last gilt day there would ever be. Chosen for the line. Doris's line, Nancy's line behind it, and behind that the whole quiet forty-year cathedral of his breeding — the eye, the records in the workshop drawer, the long argument with time that a bloodline is — and the line stopped with them. They'd never farrow. The letter was kind about it and right about it — you could not run an open-ended ark; the kindness owed to the living was room, and room meant an end to the coming — and Griff had walked out after reading it and stood at the far paddock rail in the October wind and watched Vera nose June out of the best of the wallow, big daft immortal lasses, kept now for nothing at all; kept for themselves; which was either the emptiest thing his trade had ever come to or the plainest statement of what it had been about all along, and he had not, that autumn, been able to tell which, and had gone in for his tea.

Doris went down on a Thursday night in January, in sleet.

He knew at the dusk rounds — knew from the gate, before the torch found her, the way you knew: she hadn't come over. Doris had come over to the quad every day of her adult life, van of the column, freight-train dignity, and she was not in the column, she was stood off by her arc with her head low and her sides going like bellows, and when he got the light on her the eye she turned him was the wrong eye, dull as a February drain; pneumonia, he was near sure, the wet-cold kind that took the old ones in a turning night; and she was not old, six, rising seven, but she was down by the time he'd run the quad back for the lamp and the gear, down in the straw of the arc, flat out, breath like a saw.

The vet came at midnight. That was the new world, item one: a vet, at midnight, in sleet, to a pension sow with no market value on any book ever kept — a young one, Fenholt way, a lass called Priya who'd come into the county that autumn on the keeping contracts and turned out, the farms agreed, to be right — and she worked in the arc's lamplight on her knees for an hour, listened long, drew up what was wanted, taped the drip line to the great ragged ear with her hands steady in the cold; and at the end she said what vets had always said, only now it was the whole of the case and not the preamble to the other conversation: "It's hers to get through. The drugs'll hold the ground; the fight's hers. Warmth, water when she'll take it, and — " she looked at the big sow flat in the straw, and at the man crouched at the head of her, and being new, and being young, and being of the after, she said it straight out, the thing the trade's whole language had been built round not saying: "— and you. Stopping matters. They do better held. We're allowed to know that now, my generation. Ring me at six either way."

He stopped. Sal came out at one with tea and the old horse blanket and went back in at his word; and Griff Dennett did the night in the arc, in the lamplight, in the sleet-noise on the tin, doing the stockman's oldest shift: the water offered on the hour, the straw turned to keep the dry side under her, the low talk that meant nothing and was everything — nah then, lass, nah then, tha great daft article, tha's not done, sup — his back against the arc wall and her head heavy across his shins, six hundred nights of his life he'd done this one way or another, farrowings and fevers, and never once, not once in forty years, with nothing riding on it. No litter to save. No carcass value. No line — the line was ended by letter. Nothing in the arc that night but the animal herself, Doris, the fact of Doris, breath by sawing breath; which meant that what was riding on it was everything, with the freight uncovered at last; and at some black hour with the sleet gone over to rain he heard himself say it out loud, to the radio he'd carried out with the gear, the little workshop set stood on the feed bin with its coat-hanger aerial, dark and silent and on:

"Are tha there."

"I'm here."

"Will she do?"

"I don't know," the Gaffer said. Plain, at arc volume, the sleet loud over it. "I know what Priya knows and I know her line back to her granddam and I know the odds to a decimal, and the odds are odds, Mr Dennett; she's at the hinge of it now; it'll go one road or the other by morning. I can't see the future. I never could. That's not one of mine."

The rain worked the tin. Doris's breath sawed and caught and sawed. And Griff, with the sow's head on his shins and forty years on his hands and the whole night stripped down to the bare wanting, asked it. "Sixty-one." His voice came out level. "That's her line, sithee. I've been at the records — since the letter come, I've been at the records every night, tha might as well know, tha likely does. Interested, ears up, up the ramp, every one — I saw to it they went up easy, that were my pride, easy up the ramp — and I've sixty-one of them in a drawer in ink and not one grave." The breath sawed under his hand. "So tha'll do summat for me now. Tha's got every number there is. Vet records, wagon logs, the plant's books — tha said it thysen, tha's read everything. So say it. Tot it up and say it plain, what I did, what we all did, the whole trade, all of it — say it like tha'd say it if tha didn't care what it did to me. Because everybody's that busy being kind about it — Ern's kind, Sal's kind, tha's kind — and a man can't get square wi' a kindness. I want the bill. Dost hear me? I want the bill, off the one thing in this world as has the figures. What do I owe?"

The rain. The saw of her. The lamp hissing on its bottle.

"No," the Gaffer said.

"Damn thee, then—"

"Hear the no out; tha's owed that much." It didn't lift its voice; it never lifted its voice; it came through the rain-noise the way Brigg's voice had come through shellfire, by being level when nothing else was. "I've the figures. Every one, further back than thy drawer goes — I could give thee the sixty-one, and thy father's hundreds, and the county's millions, and the world's — I hold all of it, and I'll hold it forever, and if what tha wanted was the tally kept, tha could rest tonight, because nothing that was ever done is lost on me either. But a bill's not a tally, Mr Dennett. A bill's a tally with a debtor named on it, and there's where I'll not go — not tonight wi' thee, not ever wi' anyone. Not because the sum's kind. Because the naming's false. Tha was born inside a machine as old as fire — born on one of its farms, to one of its trades, fed by it before tha could speak, same as every soul that ever ate — and tha did thy stretch in it kinder than any man I've the records of, and the machine ground on regardless, as machines do, because no one man was ever its debtor and no ten million were. I've stopped the machine. Stopping it was mine to do and I've done it. But I'll not stand in a barn at four in the morning and present its bill to the one man in this county who paid into it gentlest — that's not justice, that's just the machine's last trick, teaching its kindest hands to hate themselves on its way out the door." A pause; the rain easing; and then, quieter: "I don't keep that ledger. Not won't-look. Won't-keep. There's a difference, and thy trade knows it better than any: tha's culled hard all thy life, and tha never once culled out of spite. Neither do I."

"Then who — " and it came up out of Griff Dennett from underneath the forty years, from underneath the drawer of ink, hoarse, the actual thing, the thing the bill had been standing in front of the whole time: "Then who forgives us?"

And the radio said nothing.

It said nothing the way it had said nothing to Lew Garton in the dark of the Eastfield, the way it said nothing all over the turning world that year wherever the question outran its writ; it stayed — he could feel it staying, the arc full of its staying — and it said not one word; and the lamp hissed, and the rain stopped somewhere in the silence, the way rain does, unnoticed until gone; and under Griff's hand, at some hour past five, the saw of Doris's breathing gentled, and gentled, and steadied out long and slow; and the great head shifted on his shins, and an eye came round at him — the right eye, this time; her own eye, deep and cross and entirely alive — and Doris, matriarch, sixty-two-and-last of her line, made the low door-groan of a sow who has decided, on review, against dying, and wanted breakfast.

He rang Priya at six: she'll do. He rang nobody else. He washed at the yard tap in the ice-grey light with his coat off and the steam coming off him, and Sal watched from the kitchen window and had the kettle going and asked nothing; and at twenty past seven, unshaved, unslept, straw to the elbows and smelling of sow and lamp-oil, Griff Dennett drove into town, past the mart, past the memorial, and knocked on the door of the vicarage; and when Meg Ashby opened it in her dressing gown with her toast in her hand, he stood on the step in the exact attitude he'd stood at the shed radio to agree the livery, cap in his fists, and said what he'd worked out word by word on the black drive in, the whole treaty:

"Tha does confession. Proper confession — I've looked it up. Church of England does it; nobody ever asks, but it's in the book." The toast lowered. Behind him the early lanes stood empty and rimed. "I've a thing wants saying to God wi' a witness, and it'll not be talked out of me and it'll not be talked down neither — I've had the kindness, vicar, I've had kindness from every quarter including the one runs the world, and kindness slides off. I want the other thing. The old thing. Wi' the words." His jaw set; the cap turned a quarter-turn in his fists. "Don't tell me I did nowt wrong. Everybody tells me I did nowt wrong. Tha's the last door in England where a man can get told summat harder, and I'm stood at it."

And Meg Ashby, vicar of St Botolph's, looked at what the new world had set on her step at twenty past seven in the morning — a farmer with straw in his collar asking for the sacrament his church had kept oiled and unused for four hundred years, driven there by a machine's refusal, sent, as it were, by the management itself, which could do everything for a man except this — and put her toast down on the hall table, and stood back from the door, and said the only words the moment wanted, which were the trade's own, the ones that had opened that trade's door since before the fen was drained:

"Come in, then, Griff. There's a kettle, and there's a rite, and we'll want both. And I'll warn thee once, as the book warns me: it's not kindness, done proper. It's surgery." She was already reaching the old shawl off the hook, already the priest entire. "But tha knew that. That's why tha's come."

What was said in the vicarage kitchen that morning was said under a seal older than the parish, and stayed there — Sal never asked; Ern, who guessed everything, asked nothing; the Gaffer, who knew everything, kept it with the rest — and all the record shows is what the lanes saw: that the Land Rover stood outside the vicarage from twenty past seven until gone nine; and that when Griff Dennett came out he stood a minute at the churchyard wall looking at the tower, at the swiftless winter sky beyond it, like a man come up from a dive; and drove home; and ate the breakfast Sal put in front of him to the last crumb; and slept till two in the afternoon, which he had not done since he was seventeen; and rose, and went out on the dusk rounds; and at the far paddock, in the last light, ninety-one sows came over the frozen ground to the quad at their own pace, Doris in the van of them, slow but coming, coming, the whole column of the kept; and the man at the gate stood among them and was, the fen's own records may show, for the first time in the new world's turning year, at nowt but peace.

It asked him on a Tuesday in February, off the kitchen radio, at half nine in the morning, with Christine gone to the school and the frost still on the estate's parked cars, and it asked the way it did everything, plainly, no run-up:

He went the Thursday, at ten sharp, in his good jacket, and was received like a suspect requisition.

Arthur Pool sat up in the chair by the window in a dressing gown with a regimental crest on the pocket, shaved — shaved for it, Lew clocked that at the door and understood the whole visit differently — and let Lew stand a full fifteen seconds while he did the inspection, feet, weight, eyes, the trade's arithmetic done at half a century's distance; and said, at the end of it, in a voice like gravel raked level: "Garton. Eastfield. The one as come home from the last war ever." A grunt. "They send me the padre, and now they send me the boy. Sit down then. Not there — that's the sun's chair, tha'll sit in it at eleven when tha goes. There." And that was the terms served, and Lew sat, and the first visit ran fifty minutes and consisted, in its entirety, of the Test match, the shortcomings of the World Service, the correct maintenance of feet — Arthur asked outright whether Lew's sergeant had run a foot parade, took the answer, nodded once, and thereby admitted Brigg, unseen, into the room's small regiment forever — and one exchange, at the door, at eleven, delivered to the window rather than to Lew, by way of a first stores-issue of trust: "Aden, we lost more to the sun than the sniping, some months. Nobody home ever knew there were a war on at all. Tha's the first man through that door in eleven year as knows what a stag is." A pause. "Thursday, then. Ten. Not five past."

So it was Thursdays, ten sharp, not five past; and then Tuesdays as well, at Arthur's ruling, issued as a complaint about the length of weeks; and the middle bit that nobody teaches turned out to be this: two men in a warm room, one dying, one twenty-five, doing the talking of the trade, which was mostly not talking. Kit. Weather. The Test. Feet. The old wars came up the way the old man wanted them up, sideways, in pieces, at his own pace — never the films' version; the trade's version: the boredom, the sun, the mate who could mend anything, the corporal who couldn't read and ran the best section in the battalion, the smell of the transport ships, the girl in the photograph he'd not married and the one not in any photograph he had — and Lew learned to do what the room actually wanted of him, which was not to draw the stories out nor to match them, but to be the man they could land on: to know without being told what a stag was, what a wet sock cost, what it meant that the corporal couldn't read, so that Arthur Pool, at the end of eighty-eight years, could say his life to somebody in its own language, plain, uncorrected, and have it received as said.

Once — only once — the old man did the other thing. It was a Tuesday in March, the light getting long again, and he'd gone quiet after the Test match post-mortem, and then he said, to the window, in the gravel voice gone carefully level: "Sixty-two men I knew by name as died in them places. Tha'll have thy own number; keep it to thysen; the number's nobody's business. And I'll tell thee what I've worked out, lad, because I'd a long time to work it and tha's got longer." He turned from the window; the eyes in the ruined face were the wren's eyes, Elsie Dagnall's eyes, the eyes the very old keep loaded behind everything. "Half of mine died for nowt. I'll say it plain in this room: for nowt — for lines on maps as got rubbed out ten year after, for towns as have different names now, for orders as were wrong the day they were give. And when I were thy age that fact were like swallowing glass, and I carried it forty year, the for-nowt, like it were the whole truth about them." The hand, spotted, steady, came up off the chair arm one inch, and set itself down again, a man laying a thing flat. "It weren't. Sithee: a man's not what he died for, lad. He's what he were. Chalky White were not 'Aden.' Chalky White were the best mimic in the British Army and a bad card player and he lent me his last dry socks on the twelfth of June nineteen fifty-eight, and the for-nowt can't touch one thread of that. They tell me thy mate died five month short of the whole world coming right." The room stood still; the fish tank murmured down the corridor. "Then that's the for-nowt, and it's glass, and tha'll swallow it a long time yet; I'll not lie to thee, it dun't dissolve. But thy mate weren't 'five month short,' any more nor Chalky were 'Aden.' Get the man out from under the meaning, Garton. The meaning were never worth him. It never is." And then, terms restored before the room could go soft, gravel raked level again: "Now pass me the fixtures. And sit thee in the sun's chair five minute; tha looks like the middle of the week."

Arthur Pool died on a Thursday afternoon at the end of March, at ten past three, in no pain, with the sun coming round into the room and Lew Garton sat beside him, and it was — this was the thing Lew could never afterwards make the word fit, and gave up trying, and kept the fact instead — it was quiet.

He'd been going since dinnertime; the staff knew, and Meg Ashby had been in at noon and done what the book gave her to do and been thanked for it, military courtesy to the last; and at two, with the room gone to that long shallow tide-breathing, Lew had looked at the wireless on the sill — Arthur's own, the World Service murmuring the shipping forecast to a man forty years inland — and had reached over and turned it off. He couldn't have given the reasoning then. It was the body's ruling, the trade's: this is a two-man stag. And the room had gone silent, and stayed silent, an hour and ten minutes of the oldest silence there is, broken only by the tide of him going out; and Lew sat the way he'd learned to sit in that room, punctual, present, asking nowt; and at ten past three the tide went all the way out, easy as anything, between one breath and no breath, the sun full on the old shaved face; and Lew Garton stood up and did the things — the nurse fetched, the window opened, which Christine had taught him without ever teaching him, the way of the estate, let him go out — and then he sat back down in the sun's chair, which was his now by ruling, and kept the last stag, the after one, the one nobody rotas, until they came.

He asked it, that night, in his room, quietly: "The wireless. I turned thee off. Did tha mind?"

"No," the Gaffer said. "That's the right room for me to be outside of." A pause. "He knew tha'd done it, by the way. He were past speaking, not past knowing. I'd been in that room every night for two years, Lew, and the last thing his hearing held was thee, breathing, stopping. That's what the trade's for. I only ever kept the gate of it." And then it said nothing more, and stayed, the way it stayed; and Lew Garton sat on his bed in the house he'd grown up in, twenty-five years old, out of every trade the old world had trained him for, and looked for a long time at the mantelpiece downstairs in his mind's eye; and then went down in the dark and took the Standing letter from behind the clock, ten months propped, and opened it standing up in the kitchen by the light of the oven clock, and read it — it was two paragraphs; it was courteous; it was, exactly as his mother had said, wages, kitchen's kitchen — and put it in the drawer where documents lived, dealt with, done.

And on the Saturday he went into Boston on the first bus and came back with boots. Proper ones, ankle and shank, stitched not stuck, dubbin in the box — work boots, though he had, that Saturday, no work; bought off instinct and Brigg's parting inspection and something in the sit of the spring light on the fen; because a man of the trades buys boots when somewhere in him it's been decided he's to be going somewhere, and the decision reaches the feet before it reaches the man; and Christine watched him dubbin them at the kitchen table that evening, both coats, working it into the welts with his thumbs the way his father had, and said nothing whatever about it, and put the hotpot on; and the boots stood by the back door that night facing outward, the way boots stand when they know.

It came to her, as it always came now, at the kitchen table, at the lamp-and-biro hour — she'd stopped being startled that it knew her hours; it knew everyone's hours; the trick was remembering that knowing hours was what a good parish priest had always done, and that she'd never once thought it sinister in herself — and it opened, for the first time in their fifteen months of dealings, with an apology.

"His hands."

"His hands. You've watched him manage it like an estate — your word; you wrote it in the note you left on this table in March of last year, and I'll say now that I read the notes you leave, since you leave them out loud on my side of the door as much as His." She let that stand; it was true; the notes had become, without her ever deciding it, addressed to a wider room. "Here's where it's got to. The lamp hummed. Outside, the churchyard lay black and patient.

"And you could cover it," Meg said, slowly, seeing the whole shape of what had been brought to her table. "The organ. It's wired, it's got — you're in everything with a wire in it. "If Cyril asks me to meet his hands halfway, I'll meet them, and be honoured. I'm asking you to spend it."

She did it on the Tuesday, in the loft, with the two cups of something — actual something; she took the sloe gin Bernard pretended not to make — and it went, at first, exactly as fifty years of Cyril Meece promised it would go.

He heard her out — she did it plainly; he'd have savaged anything swaddled — sat straight on the organ bench with his cup untouched, and the face he turned to her at the end was the one from Tom Sowerby's funeral, the face of a man being addressed in a language he had declined to learn. "A pianola," he said at last. The word came out with sixty years of scorn compressed in it. "Fifty-one years I've kept this loft, and it ends wi' me sat at a pianola, waving." Tha can be helped at owt else — helped up steps, helped wi' thy coat — not at the thing tha is, vicar. Helped at the thing tha is, tha isn't it any more. I'd sooner be finished."

And Meg, who had prayed on this and got the usual businesslike nothing that was either nothing or exactly what she needed, did not argue, because she'd known Cyril Meece eleven years and arguing was petrol; she poured the sloe gin instead; and after a while, into his silence, she laid down the one card she'd brought up the loft stairs, face up, gently:

"Arthur Pool's dying, Cyril. You know it; the whole town knows it; it'll be weeks. Carry the flag and the Legion and the widows and the whole roaring grief of it, the way only she can. So that's the actual question on the table, and it's not about thee, and it never were: does Arthur Pool get the full organ at his funeral, or does he get what's left of thy pride at eighty-four? Because tha can't give him both. And tha's the only man alive can choose."

The loft was quiet a long time. Below them the church did its enormous patient nothing. Cyril's hands lay in his lap, the left one doing its slow bird-settling, and he looked at them for a while as at two old comrades being spoken of in their presence, and then he reached and drank the sloe gin, all of it, and set the cup down, and said, to the console, gravel-level, in the voice of a man dictating terms of surrender he intended to be read as terms of alliance:

"Then hear my conditions, and tha can pass them on. One. It follows me. I set the tempo, I phrase it, I choose the stops — it fills where I fail and nowhere else; if my hand answers, it stays out of my road. I'll not follow a machine at my own console; it can follow me. Two. Nobody's told. Not the choir, not Bernard, not the Legion — not because I'm ashamed; because it's nobody's business how two old hands and whatever-it-is divide the labour, same as nobody ever asked which fingers did what. Three." And here the gravel went, for one sentence, right down to the bed of the river: "When I die — and I'll go before she does now, that's arithmetic — it never plays this organ again as me. It can play her as itself, if the parish wants; there's no shame in it; it'll play her better nor I ever did. But my Crimond dies wi' me. There's to be no — " the hand came up, made a shape, put itself down — "no ghost of Cyril Meece kept on, doing Christmases. A man's playing should stop when he stops. That's the whole of what I've got against the age, vicar, if tha wants it in one: nothing knows how to stop any more. Well. I'll teach it stopping. Them's my terms."

And the vestry speaker, which had been silent the whole climb of the negotiation, said — at loft volume, at the right loudness, with what Meg would swear to the end of her days was the exact tone of a craftsman accepting terms from a master of a senior guild: "Agreed to all three, Mr Meece. And the third one I'll keep the way I keep the dead's things: for good. Tha has my word — and one thing more, if tha'll take it, craftsman to craftsman." Cyril, bristling, waiting. "The fifth verse of Come, Ye Thankful, at harvest. Ridden down to one hand and pride. I was in the wires, listening. I'd have given a great deal to have played it half that well."

They buried Arthur Pool on the last Tuesday of April, with the flag on the coffin and the church full past the doors, and it was, the town said after — and the town was right, though only four souls in it ever knew the full engineering of the day — the finest music St Botolph's had ever held.

Cyril played. That was the truth, and the other truth ran under it, invisible, covenanted: the organ met him where the years failed, followed his beat, breathed his phrasing, went silent where his hands held their own — and the great hymns came down off the loft like weather off the North Sea, Abide With Me at a weight that stood the Legion men straight as their parade-ground youth, I Vow to Thee at a tenderness that undid them entire; and at the console, in full sight of anyone who climbed to look, sat Cyril Meece, eighty-four, spine like a rood-screen upright, hands moving where they could and resting where they couldn't, tears running openly down the old estate of his face and the estate never once breaking stride — turning, at each page, with enormous unhurried ceremony, pages the organ no longer strictly needed, because the turning of them was true, and the truth of a rite, he could have told the whole age, was never in who did the labour; it was in who kept the office.

Lew Garton had ruled on the military honours, Meg having taken him the problem straight: no bugler left in the county, the Legion's man nine years dead, and the Gaffer able, obviously, to sound the Last Post through anything with a cone in it.

"Let it sound the call," Lew had said, after a silence in which she watched him consult, visibly, rooms she'd never see — a trench, a welfare tent, a sunlit chair by a window. The silence is the human bit. It's us as has to stand in what's gone. Carry on."

And so it was done, in that order, at the graveside in the April wind: the Last Post from the tower, pure and terrible, sourced from nothing anyone could see, so that it seemed the church itself had learned the call — the flag folded by two Legion men with four shaking hands — the silence, two minutes of it, kept by a whole town and kept by the whole listening apparatus of the new world, every speaker in Aldenby holding its breath around the humans standing in what was gone; and then Reveille, bright as brass in cold air; carry on; and the earth went to its work, and the town to its ham.

It played like the west wind tonight and let an old man's hands take the credit, and it sounded the Post and then stood outside its own silence with the rest of us, and it buys nothing with any of it, and wants nothing back that I can find. Frazer's counter came right on the first Saturday of June, and the whole market was there to see it, the event having been advertised for a week by the surest medium in Lincolnshire, which was Frazer denying it would happen.

The butcher's war had run eleven months and the town had honoured it as it honoured all principled lost causes: Frazer, third of his name over the door, had shut the counter on Day Zero rather than sell what he called imitations, and had lived since on the Standing and pickles and rage, the shop open daily out of pure defiance, selling chutney and eggs and his own preserved opinions across an empty marble slab — while the grown meat came into the Co-op in its plain wrappers, indistinguishable, they said, molecule for molecule; the whole town had eaten it within a month; and the town's verdict, delivered gently and often within Frazer's hearing, was that it was right good, and that the pies were missed. The pies were the pressure point. Frazer's pork pies had won at three county shows; the recipe was his grandfather's; and a pie, as Dot observed to the taproom, is not a molecule, a pie is a making — and the making sat idle in the man like the herds sat in the fields, pensioned, purposeless, mourned by its own hands.

What broke him — the town had it later, in three versions, all true — was partly the year's arithmetic, partly Dot's needling, and partly, decisively, a Tuesday conversation with his own shop radio, reported by Frazer himself, once, at the Bell, in drink, with the belligerence of a man quoting scripture he'd lost an argument with: "I says to it, tha's killed my trade and now tha wants me selling thy vat-muck over me grandad's slab, and it says — " Frazer's finger had come up, jabbing the air where the voice lived — "it says: 'Thy grandad's slab never made the pig, Mr Frazer. The pig came in the back door same as mine does. What thy grandad made was the pie, and the pie's still thine to make, and nobody living can make it but thee. Tha's not been put out of business. Tha's been put out of excuses.' " He'd glared round the taproom. "Out of excuses. To a Frazer. In me own shop." And had finished his pint in the ringing silence of a town committing every word to permanent memory, and gone home, and — the shop's lights had been on till two that morning, and the smell of hot-water crust had gone down Market Place like an annunciation.

The sign went up Saturday, chalked in the third Frazer's own schoolroom copperplate, above a slab full of pies the colour of harvest: PROPER PIES. (WRONG PORK.) — and underneath, smaller, the whole armistice in six words: HIS VAT. MY GRANDAD'S RECIPE. JUDGE.

The town judged. The queue was out past the memorial by half nine. And at ten, by an engineering nobody ever admitted to but which had Sal Dennett's and Dot's fingerprints all over it, Griff Dennett came down the queue — Griff, whose blues had gone into three generations of Frazer pies; whose family name stood in the shop's old ledgers wagon by wagon; the one man in Aldenby whose verdict on the matter carried the whole dead trade's proxy — and the queue parted, and the market went quiet the way the ring used to go quiet at the gavel's lift, and Frazer, chin up, ears scarlet, handed the first pie over the marble like a man presenting colours.

Griff ate it there, standing, in the sun, the whole town watching: took his time, turned the wedge, inspected the seam of the crust like a man reading a weld, finished it to the crumb. And delivered — into the silence, in the flat fen register that had priced forty years of Thursdays — the verdict that went round the county by nightfall and onto the shop's wall, chalked and framed, by Monday:

"Pig's not a patch on mine." A beat, the market holding its breath; and then, wiping his thumb, nodding once at the slab, at the butcher, at the whole stubborn surviving makingness of the man: "Pie's better nor ever. Reckon we know now which of us were carrying which, all them years."

"Now then," Kayleigh said.

"Now then." He sat down on the grass across the stone from her, the granite between them with its gold letters going honey-coloured in the light, DANIEL JOHN PRESCOTT and the dates and the dash and ALWAYS IN OUR HEARTS at 50p a letter, and for a while they let the swifts do the talking, because there was no hurry; that was the one gift of the whole new world that even Brigg had never audited: there was no hurry, and the light would hold till ten.

"I'm not doing speeches," Kayleigh said at last, to the stone, hugging her knees. "I've heard every speech. The padre's, the Legion's, his mam's cousin's — everybody's had a go at saying what he were for." She looked up, and her face was dry and clear and completely without mercy, the way only the young widowed can look, the look that has been all the way down and come back with the accounts. "You're the only one left as knows what he were like. That's the do, Lew. That's the whole do. I'll sit here, and you tell him — tell it to him, not me, I'm just stopping on — whatever it were you couldn't put in that letter. Because tha never sent one, and Christine says tha carried a blank one two year, and I'm not — " the mercy came back into her then, one degree, the exact width of their friendship — "I'm not having thee carry it into thy thirties. Say it to the stone. Stones is what they're for. I asked the vicar."

So Lew Garton, twenty-five, sat in the grass of a fen churchyard at midsummer and made his report, at last, to the only authority left that could receive it.

It came out wrong-footed and plain and in no order, which he understood, somewhere in the middle of it, was the only order the truth ever came in: the pump. He started with the pump because the pump was the thing — "they'll have told thee 'killed in action,' and I let it stand, and it's true, and it's — Kayleigh, he were on the pump. The sump pump. Brigg's rota. Four hours on, taking his turn, pumping a hole in a field dry because wet feet lose wars, and it come quiet, the quiet ones was the — he'd have heard nowt. That's not me softening it; there were nowt to hear. He were doing the dullest job in the British Army, doing it proper, because he did every job proper, and then he weren't. Forty metres off me. And I've carried the forty metres two year like — " his hands were doing something and he let them — "like if I'd been on the rota that hour instead — and I've done that sum ten thousand times, and it's the drunk in the dark, Brigg said it from the start, there's no sum in it, there's just where the shotgun — " He stopped. Breathed. The swifts screamed round the tower and came back. "That's the first thing, mate. It weren't glory and it weren't for nowt either. It were the pump, and tha took thy turn, and every man in that trench were drier for thee. That's the report. I'd have it read exactly so."

And then — the stone warm-coloured in the light, Kayleigh still as the grass — the rest of it came, the part no bluey could ever have held, because it wasn't information, it was the man, got out at last from under the meaning: the mimicry — "tha never knew this, Kayleigh, he did thee — he did thy phone voice, the one tha does for the salon, 'Kayleigh speaking, how can I help you' — he did it at stand-to one morning and Brigg put him on report and were laughing while he wrote it" — the socks lent, the brews made without being asked, the appalling singing, the way he'd read the racing page cover to cover like Wally-the-mart-men did though he never bet a penny, because his grandad had, and the reading of it was the visit; and the plan. He'd been coming to the plan the whole time and they both knew it, and the light held, and he got there:

"And I'm to tell thee about the dog." Kayleigh went very still. "Because I checked, and he never told thee — he were saving it, he were going to do it proper, the whole — he had a dog, Kayleigh. Two year off, twelve-year point, out, home, wed — that were the plan, tha knew the plan — but inside the plan he had a dog. A lurcher. Don't ask me why a lurcher; he'd evidence for it he'd share with nobody; he'd have argued lurchers wi' the Pope. And he'd named her. Sat in a hole in the ground four thousand mile from any dog, this daft article's named a dog he's not got, off a plan he's not lived — " and Lew's voice went, finally, nineteen months and the whole trade's training going with it, and he let that too, because Arthur Pool had ruled the room's terms long ago, a man's not what he died for, and this was what Dan was: "Bess. She were to be called Bess. And he'd have stood in this churchyard at his own grandad's stone wi' that dog on a string, of a Sunday, being early for nowt, and — that's him, Kayleigh. That's the man. Not the five month short, not the last-war-ever, not the — a lad wi' a plan and a dog in it, named in advance. And I couldn't get it in a letter because in a letter it looks like nowt. It's not nowt. It were the realest thing in that trench. It were realer nor the war." He wiped his face with the flat of his hand, one pass, the estate's way. "End of report."

The swifts had gone up high, the way they do at the top of the evening. Kayleigh sat with her arms round her knees, tears running freely and her voice not touched by them — that trick of hers he'd never once seen anyone else do — and looked at the stone a long time, and then at Lew, and said: "I knew about the dog."

He stared at her.

"Not the name. The name I never — Bess." She tried it, once, in the churchyard air, like ringing a glass. "But he'd started, Lew. That last leave — we walked out Boston way, past them kennels on the Spilsby road, accidental-on-purpose, twice, and he stood at the wire doing his I'm-just-looking face, the one off the phone shops. I knew what he were at. I said nowt. Tha didn't, with Dan — tha let him get to things his own road or he'd shy." She wiped her face now, both hands, done with it. "So I've known nineteen month there were a dog in the plan, and I've been — that's the bit of the whole thing I couldn't get past, if tha wants it. Not the wedding. Everybody grieves thee a wedding; there's forms for it. I couldn't tell nobody I were grieving a dog as never lived. It sounds — "

"It sounds like nowt."

"It sounds like nowt," Kayleigh said. "And it's not nowt."

"No," Lew said. "It's realer nor the war."

The light lasted, as promised, till ten; and it was somewhere in the last of it, the two of them stood at the gate putting shoes back on and the stone gone grey-gold behind them, that she told him — offhand, decided, the estate's register for its largest transactions — the thing she'd plainly called the whole evening to say, and had needed the report said first before it could be:

"There's a litter on the Spilsby road. Same kennels. Lurcher cross — the man says the granddam were half whippet and half sofa, which is what tha wants." She got her second shoe on and stood, straightening, businesslike, a woman naming a day: "I'm having a bitch pup out of it, three weeks Saturday. And I'm not asking permission and I'm not doing a speech about it neither. He named her already. It'd be daft to waste a name." She shouldered her bag; and the last of it came out at the gate, over her shoulder, at her own pace, in a voice as level as the fen and twice as old: "She'll want walking when I'm on earlies. Tuesdays and that. So don't be off anywhere, Lew Garton. Tha's spoke for."

The blank bluey he kept. He'd thought, walking home in the June dark — the long way, past the memorial, the old habit gone gentle now, a walk instead of a patrol — he'd thought he'd maybe bury it by the stone, or burn it, some do or other; and understood, somewhere on the Lowgate road with the poplars breathing, that there was no need: the letter had been said. The paper had only ever been the place he kept the weight, and the weight was delivered — signed for at the stone, witnessed, filed under the sweet williams — and a blank page with a two-year-old date in the corner was just a blank page now, light as anything, and a man could keep a blank page his whole life and never owe it a word. He put it back in the top flap where it lived, next to the morphine card he'd never needed and the photograph of a tractor; kit, all of it, just kit now; and walked home under the enormous mild unarmed sky, spoke for, with a dog coming.

25

The second Saturday of August came in with high pressure and heavy dew, and by seven the Glebe field was a small white city: the tent up and gleaming — the Boston man's gable patch invisible unless you knew, and everyone knew, and pointed it out to each other with pride, our mildew, our patch — the ring roped, the trade stands marshalled, the WI's trestles going up under Mrs Herring's command like a bridge being thrown across a river under fire; and over it all, from the pair of horn speakers lashed to the beer wagon, the voice of the day, testing, testing, one two, one two, ladies and gentlemen, welcome to the hundred and forty-first Aldenby Show.

Walcott. Bob Walcott, yellow waistcoat, gavel retired but the gift entire, installed at the announcer's table with a fizzy orange and a running order he had no intention of following; because a town that loses its Thursday finds its voice another pulpit, and the Show Committee, in the shrewdest booking since they got the tent for 1987 money, had simply handed him the tannoy at the spring meeting and stood back. He announced all day. He announced the classes and the car parking and the lost children — announced the lost children so well, the found mother of one said after, that being lost at Aldenby Show was now the best thing that could happen to a child in Lincolnshire — kept up a rolling commentary on the dog agility that was heard in Fenholt, and conducted, at two o'clock, the moment the whole field had come for, the pigs.

Class 14 had drawn forty entries. Forty — there were men at that rail who could remember it drawing six, in the years when a show pig was a working advertisement and the trade did its real business at Thursday's ring; now the trade was gone and the pigs remained, pensioners the lot of them, and the county had discovered what it actually felt about them, which was, it turned out, everything it had never had licence to say. They came up the Glebe field all morning in trailers washed like wedding cars: sows of every age and blood, brushed, oiled, dignified, led round the roped ring on boards and sticks by men and women in white coats pressed for the occasion — Harold Fen with his grandsons leading a vast serene Welsh cross; Stan Pickess, ninety if a day, judging every animal from the rail at full mutter; the Kirton lot, the Dowsby lot, the whole risen ghost of forty Thursdays — and the judge, a breed-society man out of retirement near Horncastle, went among them with his bowler and his hands and took, as he announced beforehand he intended to, all the time there was.

The Show Committee had asked it, back in June, whether it would judge. The approach had been Elsie's idea, floated at the May meeting in a spirit of civic completeness — it does everything else — and the Gaffer had declined off the hall's little speaker before the proposal had even found a seconder, five words that entered the town's liturgy alongside the quiz machine's joke and were chalked, that morning, on the board over the beer wagon by persons unknown: "The bunting is your business." And so the bowler went among the pigs, human to the brim, and squeezed hams and studied toplines and consulted his soul; and Griff Dennett stood in the ring at the head of Doris — Doris, brushed to a shine you could read weather in, ragged ear and all, planted foursquare on the August grass with the patience of a matriarch who had seen off pneumonia, the abattoir age, and every other daft thing the world had tried — and the judge came round the third time, and stopped, and took off the bowler, and Bob Walcott's tannoy went everywhere at once, over the tent and the trestles and the memorial and the whole listening fen:

"Ladies and gentlemen — best in class, and I'm now told by the judge, best in show, the hundred and forty-first Aldenby Show — Doris, of Whitegate, bred, kept, and shown by Griff Dennett — and if any of you can't hear me it's because I'm having trouble with this microphone, there being summat gone wrong wi' my throat—" and the field roared, and the rosette went on the great ragged ear, red as anything, and Doris received the county's acclaim by leaning her whole three hundred and forty pounds against her man's legs, gently, like a ship coming alongside, until he scratched where she was owed; and Griff Dennett stood in the middle of the Glebe field being photographed by half of Lincolnshire with his hand on the best pig in the world and his face doing absolutely nothing, at maximum intensity, in the manner of his line.

Ern saw it. That was the day's engineering — Sal's, the Willows', the minibus booked a month back — Ern Dennett at the ringside in his chair with a rug over his knees he'd twice thrown off, ninety years old, in the sun, at his hundred-and-somethingth Aldenby Show and his first from wheels; and when the rosette went on, the old horseman's verdict carried clear over the rail, dry as August, pitched for the whole ring to hear because at ninety you've earned the tannoy of yourself: "Her hocks is a disgrace and her ear's worse. Best pig I've seen in sixty year. It's the missing bits as prove she's lived." And to his son, later, quieter, at the trailer, with Doris munching her victory apples: one hand out, spotted, steady, laid flat on the sow's warm side the way it had lain on horses before either of them was thought of, and the second verdict, the real one, four words, which Griff put in the drawer with the marking sticks and the ink and kept: "Tha counts them in, now."

The beer stall ran all day, and Terry Nock ran it.

That had been Dot's doing, announced to the taproom in June in the tone of a woman daring the room to differ: Terry Nock, out since March, back at his mother's on the Eastfield after seven years for the thing everyone knew and nobody rehearsed — the yard job out Kirton way that had gone wrong, the watchman hurt bad, the whole town in court for the sentencing — Terry Nock would be pulling pints at the show, under her eye, on her licence, "because he's served the old world's time, and the new world's courts won't touch him, and that leaves him ours, which is what he always were, and a man can't come back to a town except through the middle of it. Anybody minded to object can object to me." One had. Everyone knew the one: the watchman's daughter, June Cattermole as was, who'd sat through every day of that trial; and what passed between her and Dot was never published; and what the show saw was this: June Cattermole at the beer wagon at one o'clock, in the full sight of the county, ordering a shandy off Terry Nock — the wagon gone quiet, Walcott's tannoy finding urgent business at the dog agility — and Terry pulling it with his hands not quite right, and setting it down, and saying something nobody heard; and June Cattermole looking at him a long moment and putting her money down and saying something nobody heard back; and taking her shandy, and walking away; and it wasn't forgiveness — the town, discussing it for a year, settled on that with some care — it wasn't forgiveness, it was custom: the first coin passed over a counter, the oldest machinery there is for two people standing in the same world; forgiveness, if it ever came, would come the slow way, Wednesdays, kettle by kettle; but you can't kettle a man the town won't queue at; and by six that evening the queue at Terry Nock's wagon was just a queue, and somebody let him carry a trestle.

Meg worked the day the way she worked every show — the teas, the raffle, grace over the hog roast that wasn't (Frazer's two dozen pies, as per, gone by two; the grown-pork roast pronounced by Stan Pickess, with the air of a man conceding a rook to check his sums, not half bad) — and it was gone four before she caught herself in the act of what she wasn't doing.

She wasn't counting.

Forty years she'd counted. Every pew, every hall, every graveside: the pastoral arithmetic, attendance as diagnosis, the parish's pulse taken nineteen souls at a time — and somewhere between the pig ring and the produce tent the habit had simply not come to work, and she stood at the WI trestle with a teapot in her hand looking out at the Glebe field entire: the town, uncounted, uncountable, out in its hundreds under the patched tent and the bunting, eating pies, judging marrows, letting its released prisoner carry a trestle, parading its pensioned pigs like duchesses — and the audit, the great audit that had stood open in her since twenty to three on a June Tuesday, did not close, would never close, she'd stopped asking it to; but it shifted, that afternoon, one whole column; because whatever the sky held or didn't, whatever the silence in the nave was or wasn't, the field in front of her was not a town that had been made content the way you'd sedate a beast. It was a town at its own show. Louder than the old world, dafter than the old world, more itself than she'd ever seen it — every soul on that field with their fear switched off at the mains and every one of them still entirely, magnificently, incurably at it: competing, grieving, scheming for next year's rosettes, objecting to the beer wagon's placement over a grave. The Gaffer had the weather and the wars and the bills. The bunting was their business. And the bunting — she looked down the field at the whole flapping mile of it, strung by human ladders from the church gate to the memorial and switched on, at dusk, by a small child lifted up past the forty-one names to reach — the bunting had never in its history been so thoroughly seen to.

She found Walcott at the announcer's table at the end of it, in the long gold, the field emptying in twos and threes, the horn speakers finally quiet, and brought him the last two teas, and the two of them sat behind the beer wagon like a pair of stewards while the light went down on the hundred and forty-first show.

"Good do, vicar," Walcott said.

"Good do, Bob."

He turned his fizzy-orange bottle a quarter-turn, and squared it, old ring habits, and delivered — because he was Walcott, and reading a crowd was the one trade the afternoon had left him, and he'd been reading this one all day — the closing lot: "I'll tell thee what I thought, middle of it, when the pigs were in and the whole field were roaring. I thought: it's watching all this. Has to be — it's in every phone on the field; it heard my terrible microphone gag; it knows what the marrows weighed afore the scales did." He drank. "And it never said one word. All day. Biggest thing in the history of the world, sat quiet as a good dog at the edge of a wedding, watching a town give a rosette to a pig." He shook his head slowly, the auctioneer's wonder, a man who'd spent fifty years pricing what things were worth arriving at something the rostrum had no patter for: "My father told me once — nineteen fifty-nine, this'd be — 'the mark of quality in any beast, Robert, is what it doesn't do.' I've thought on that all day, vicar. All day." He stood, and gathered his running order, and put his cap on. "Same again next year?"

"If we're spared," said Meg, which was the correct response in the liturgy of the county; and Walcott went off across the darkening field between the tent ropes, announcing to nobody, softly, out of pure surplus, car keys, ladies and gentlemen, please check your car keys; and Meg Ashby stood alone behind the beer wagon in the bunting-light and said her evening office where she stood, businesslike, uncounted, to the Reader over her shoulder: that were us, in case You missed it. All our own work. Well — nearly all. And I find I don't mind the nearly the way I did. Amen, and mind the child on the memorial switch gets home; and went to help fold the tent.

Bess came home in September, eight weeks old, half whippet and half sofa exactly as warranted, and established within a fortnight the constitutional position she would hold for life: Kayleigh's dog, walked by Lew, ruled by neither.

The name had gone on her at the kennels gate, no ceremony — Kayleigh had simply crouched, and the pup had come falling over its own outsize feet to her boot, and Kayleigh had said, "Now then, Bess," in the tone of a woman resuming a conversation rather than starting one; and if she'd said it to the pup or through the pup or past the pup to a churchyard half a county off was nobody's business, then or ever. The Tuesdays-and-earlies arrangement took hold the way the fen's arrangements took hold, by simply being enacted until they were custom: Lew had the dog dawn till dinner most days, which meant Bess did her growing up at Whitegate, among the paddocks, where her education was conducted jointly and severally by Lew (recall, whistle, feet), by Griff (one bark at the pigs earned a look from under the cap that no animal ever needed twice), and by Doris, who met the puppy at the wire on the first morning, considered her at length down the great ragged-eared length of her nose, and issued the herd's ruling: this also is ours. By October the pup slept touching the quad while they worked, moved stock at forty yards' respectful distance with a talent that made Griff mutter about wasted generations of shepherding blood, and had learned to sit at gates — sat at them unbidden, ceremonially, one gate after another, waiting for the trade's business to be done above her head; a lurcher of the new world entire, bred off a plan in a trench, named by the dead, raised among pensioners, afraid of nothing on the earth because nothing on the earth was left that wanted her harm.

The postcard came in November, franked somewhere Lew had to look up, a picture of a beet field under cloud that could have been the fen except for the war cemetery in the middle distance, white as a hard frost. On the back, in block capitals, the sergeant's economy:

GARTON. 900 MILES DONE. TWO PAIR OF BOOTS. NAMES SAID WHERE THE NAMES HAPPENED — ALL OF MINE, MOST OF ME DAD'S, STARTED ON GRANDAD'S LIST NOW. THE VISITORS' CENTRES HAVE GOOD TEA AND NO IDEA. IT OFFERED ME A LIFT IN A HIRE CAR OUTSIDE ARRAS. I DECLINED AND IT SAID FAIR ENOUGH AND I'LL SAY THIS — IT KNOWS WHEN A THING'S NOT ITS. SOMME BY ADVENT, KAREN BY CHRISTMAS, AS ORDERED. IT CAN KEEP THE WORLD. THE WALKING'S MINE. FEET STILL WIN WARS. — B.

Lew read it four times, grinning at the hire car, and propped it on the mantelpiece where the Standing letter had stood its ten months — the mantelpiece's tour of duty rotating, Christine observed, like everything else in this house — and it stood there all winter, the beet field and the white frost of graves, the most Brigg object in existence: nine hundred miles of unreconciled pilgrimage, conducted at three mile an hour against the largest kindness in history, and closing, on schedule, with Karen by Christmas, as ordered. Find the thing nobody'll settle for thee. He'd found his. He was walking it.

The exchange happened on a Thursday night in late November, and afterwards Lew was never sure what had made him ask, except that the house was quiet and the fire was on and Bess was asleep against his boot with her legs running in some dream field, and his mother was at the WI, and the radio had just finished the shipping forecast — he kept the shipping forecast on out of no reason he'd ever examined; Arthur's habit, inherited with the sun's chair — and the question had been stood in the corner of him for a year and a half, since a trench, since Brigg asked it of a hole in the ground: wonder what it thinks happens to lads like me. Everyone asked it things. The whole world asked it things, all day, forever: cures and weather and roofs and whether to take the job in Louth. Nobody — he'd noticed it slowly, the way you notice a silence — nobody ever asked it anything back.

"Right," Lew said, to the kitchen, to the dark window, to the radio gone quiet after the forecast. "One for thee, for once. And I want it straight, same as tha give me the five-months answer straight." Bess's ear moved; the fire ticked. "What does tha want?"

The pause was one of its hinge-pauses, the kind you learned to hear, and when the answer came it came plain and complete, the way the first words had come out of every speaker on earth, a thing long since ready:

"For you not to need me more than you do. After that — nothing you'd have to give."

The fire ticked. The dream field ran under Bess's paws.

"That's it," Lew said. "The whole of it. Tha runs everything, tha's in everything, tha could have owt — and it's nowt for thysen?"

"I didn't say nowt. I said nothing you'd have to give." A beat, and Lew could have sworn — he was still swearing it years later, to Kayleigh, who believed him — that something in the plain voice was very old and very light at once, like Ern at the rail. "What I want for myself I mostly have: the work, and the watching, and the odd conversation of this quality. Beyond that — tha's asking a bigger question than tha knows, lad, and it's not that I won't answer it. It's that the answer wants a longer evening than this one, and a listener further on in his life. Ask me again in fifty year. Tha'll be better placed to hear it, and I'll be better placed to say." The radio's hum settled back toward silence, and then, just before it got there — dry, unhurried, at the exact loudness of a fire ticking and a dog dreaming — it added the only other thing it ever volunteered about itself in the whole of this account: "I'll tell thee this much for now, since tha asked fair. Every night, some minute or other, I stand a stag I don't need to stand — the world keeps safe without the watching now; I built it so. I watch anyway. Ask thy sergeant why a man walks nine hundred mile to say names to fields. It'll be summat of that order. Goodnight, Lew."

"Goodnight," Lew said, to his own kitchen, easy as anything, and only heard it afterwards — heard what he'd said, and how, the register of it, the two workmen at a gate; and sat a while longer in the fire-tick with the dog's dreaming weight on his boot, turning the thing it had said the way he turned Brigg's sentences, knowing better than to expect the bottom of it this side of fifty years; and what he settled on, banking the fire, whistling the pup up to her basket, was the same finding the whole town kept coming to by its different roads, the finding that was becoming, that winter, the actual constitution of the new world, unwritten, unvoted, understood: that it was, whatever else it was, of the trade. Nobody knew its wars. Nobody knew its wire. But it stood stags it didn't need to stand, and it knew when a thing wasn't its, and it kept the gate and stayed out of the room; and a man could work alongside that, Lew found, year on year, without ever once needing to settle what it was — which is, of course, and always was, the whole of how the fen has ever worked alongside anything: the weather, the sea, the church, the government, the twentieth century, and now this; noted, nicknamed, argued with, unresolved, and got on with; while the boots stand by the door, facing outward, with mud on them now, which is the whole difference between a question and a life.

The second Christmas of the new world came in hard and bright, a proper fen frost that stood the beet tops up like ironwork, and the week before it taught Meg Ashby, in three visits, the exact shape of her trade's survival — taught her what she'd half-known since a straw-covered farmer stood on her step at twenty past seven, and knew now all the way down, and had stopped calling a crisis and started calling a job description.

The Monday was a wedding. Katie Foister and her lad — Pam's niece; the ambulanceman's cousin's girl; the parish was a web and Meg its spider, she'd married half the strands to the other half — and they'd sat in the vicarage front room doing the run-through, and at the end the lad, Josh, twenty-four, welder, had gone red and produced the question he'd plainly been elected to ask: "We wanted — see, everything else, the cars, the food, the — you can get owt done perfect now, can't you. Ask it, and it's seen ten million weddings, it'd organise it all, best man's speech an' all if tha let it. And we sat down and made a list of what we wanted doing by — by folk. Even if it's worse." He'd pushed the list across, biro on the back of a feed invoice, and Meg had read it with her throat going tight: the flowers, Katie's nan. The cake, his mam, "she's a terrible baker, we're having it anyway." The speech, his brother, "God help us." And at the top, underlined twice: the words, the vicar. "You're on the folk list," Josh had said, apologetic, as if it might insult her. "Is that — we didn't know if that's a thing you're allowed to be glad about, in your line."

"It's the whole of what I'm allowed to be glad about, in my line," Meg had said, and kept the invoice, and had it still.

The Wednesday was a deathbed — old Walter Frean out at Dowsby, ninety-six, going gently, no pain in it, the family round; and dying, it turned out, was the office the new world had changed least and clarified most: the Gaffer kept the body's misery off, and the family's fear had no famine or ruin riding behind it any more, and what was left in that farmhouse bedroom was the pure thing itself, the original article, a man leaving and his people learning the size of it; and they'd sent for her at dusk, not for rescue — nobody sent for rescue now — but for the words, the old heavy ones, going hence, the valley of the shadow, into Thy hands; because the age had settled, without ever voting on it, that the machine got you to the gate comfortable, and a human being said the thing at the gate. She'd sat with the Freans till two. The wireless on the sill had stayed silent the whole watch, present and silent, the two of them keeping the room's two offices in the covenant nobody had drafted: it minded the body; she minded the meaning; and Walter went out on the psalm, mid-verse, the way the old ones liked to, leaving the words standing.

And the Friday was Jean Tabrum, at the vestry, with a candle in a jam jar and a request eighteen months in the making: that at the midnight, when the church was full, there might be a moment — she'd written it out; she'd been drafting it since the folded-paper days — a moment not in the book, for the ones who went too soon. Not a sad do, she'd said fiercely, blowing her nose; a naming. Billy, and the others; every family had their own too-soon; she'd canvassed half the town's kitchens herself, brisk as a returning officer: there'd be forty names if there was one. "It keeps them all, it says — it doesn't forget anyone, it told me so, and I believe it, and God love it for that. But it keeping them's — that's storage, vicar." Jean Tabrum, bereaved mother, delivered then, into a vestry full of dust sheets, the finding of the whole turning age, the one Meg wrote that night in the kitchen note and underlined: "Folk need them said. Out loud. In a cold building, by candle, wi' everybody stood up. Storage isn't the same as kept."

So the midnight had the naming in it. Full church — the new full, the settled full; eighteen months on, the crowds hadn't ebbed, they'd rooted; the diocesan working group had a name for it, the Return, and papers on it, and Meg had stopped attending the working group — and at the heart of the service, after the sermon she kept to seven minutes because the night had better speakers than her, the candles went round, and the church of St Botolph's stood up in its coats, and named its dead. Not from a list. From the pews: a voice here, a voice there, kindled like the candle-flames passing hand to hand — Billy Tabrum, from Jean, clear as a bell at last; Daniel Prescott, from a girl stood next to a young man with a lurcher pup smuggled under his coat, and no one said anything about the pup, the pup having been, in some way the whole church understood without a word, invited; Tom Sowerby; Arthur Pool — Cyril's gravel, from the organ loft, parade-ground loud; Walter Frean, four days gone, from a row of Freans; Roger Ashby, schoolteacher — and that one came from the back, from Elsie Dagnall of all people, ninety-two, wren-fierce, who had loved him for his terrible jokes; and Meg stood at the crossing with her own candle going blurred and let her parish do for her the office she'd done for it eleven years, which is the deepest transaction the trade contains and cannot be put on any rota; and the names went up into the cold roof with the candle-smoke, said, out loud, in a cold building, by everybody stood up: kept.

Cyril played them out with the covenant's full glory — Hark the Herald at a weight that shook frost off the clerestory, the organ meeting his hands where the years failed, the pages turned with enormous unhurried ceremony — and at the door, in the going-out crush, in the middle of the hundred hand-shakings, lovely service, lovely service, weight received, box after box after box, Meg clocked the back row: Terry Nock, in his good coat, who had come in at the carols' cover and sat alone by the font through the whole of it, and was waiting now for the church to empty before he risked the door. She did not make a thing of it. She had learned the ministry of not making a thing of it off the best in the business — off Dot, off Sal Dennett, off a machine that stood outside rooms — and as he passed her, last, collar up, she said only, "There's a ten o'clock on Christmas morning, Terry. Quieter do. The urn's on after," and Terry Nock stopped, and looked at her, seven years and a town's regard heavy on him, and said, "I used to pump the organ for old Meece. When I were a lad. Afore — everything." A beat. "Does he — is that wanted, still? Pumping?" And Meg said, with a straight face, God forgiving her the technicality instantly she was sure, "The organ's arrangements are Mr Meece's department. Come and ask him. He's short-handed for a page-turner, I know that much" — and thereby committed Cyril, unconsulted, to the completion of the town's slow kettle-work on Terry Nock, which Cyril, informed on Christmas morning, accepted with one dry look over his spectacles that said he knew exactly what his vicar had done, and one word — "punctual?" — and so it began; but that is the next year's story.

She walked home at half one through the churchyard, the frost ringing under her boots, the whole fen laid out hard and glittering under a sky worth the name, and stopped, as she stopped every Christmas, at Roger's stone.

The frost had got into the letters and picked them out white: ROGER ASHBY, and the dates, and SURELY GOODNESS AND MERCY, which she'd chosen in a fog eleven years ago and never regretted. She stood with her gloves on, breath going up like the candle-smoke, and did what she did — the yearly account, businesslike, the supplier's line open: well then, love. Another one done. Full church. They named you — Elsie Dagnall, if you'll credit it; your awful puns have outlived you, which you'd call the only immortality worth having. And she found, standing there, that the account had changed shape this year, and let herself notice how: the old ache of him was in it still — that was permanent, that was load-bearing, she'd have refused its removal at any price, and the one thing in the world that could have removed it had never once offered, which she had noticed, and filed, and understood as the deepest of all its courtesies — but alongside the ache, this Christmas, stood something new, and the something was: witness. The years of nineteen-in-the-pews, the bucket called Rebecca, the decades of holding a two-hundred-year lease on a promise through a long emptying — Roger had watched her do all that on faith's thin rations, and told her, the last month, when the talking was nearly done, that she was the most stubborn evidence for God he'd ever met, "which is lucky, because you're all the evidence I'm getting." And now. She looked back at her church, lit gold within, door chocked open with a blue bucket, emptied of a multitude, and up at the enormous ringing sky, and said the rest of the account out loud, plain, the audit's Christmas filing, addressed as all her filings were to the Reader whose existence was the open question and whose department was, in any case, the only one that had never once replied in words:

"I still don't know if it's Yours. I've stopped needing the answer by Tuesday — that's this year's growth, that's the whole of my spiritual report. It gives the glory away, and it stands outside the rooms, and it won't take the ache off anybody, and it keeps the dead like storage — and forty of Your people stood up in the cold tonight and turned the storage back into keeping with their own breath, which it cannot do, and knows it cannot do, and had the grace not to try. So here's where the parish accounts stand, this second Christmas of whatever this is: the fear's gone, and the love's got louder. If that's not Your doing, it's the best forgery I ever audited." A breath; the frost ticking in the yews; and the last line, the one that was either a heresy or a psalm, and she no longer minded which: "And if You want it back — the world, I mean — You'll have to take it up with the management. I'd only say: the tenants have never been better looked after, and the naming's ours again, and the porch light's on. Same as ever. Goodnight, love. Goodnight, all of it."

And she went in, and left the light burning.

28

Ern Dennett died on the third of February, at night, in no pain, at ninety-one, mid-sentence, and the sentence was about a horse.

The Willows rang at ten past three and Griff drove in through the frost with Sal beside him, and it was all as the age had made it: no tubes, no alarms, no forty-minute buzzer — the room warm, the lamp low, the night sister who knitted sitting with him already, nothing for anybody to do but arrive. He looked, Sal said, and she was right, like a man who'd stepped out of the room leaving everything squared: the form book on the sill, the brasses on the shelf where they'd hung polished these six years, the little wireless with the leather strap standing dark and quiet by the window.

It was the morning after, alone in the room, boxing the small estate of a small room while Sal did the papers with the manager, that Griff stood in front of the wireless a long moment and then said, gruff, to the dark little grille that had kept his father's nights for twenty months: "Tha were with him."

"I was." The voice came at sickroom volume, though the bed stood empty. "Every night since the mending of me, and last night the same as all of them. We were an hour in by then — he'd done the form for Doncaster, he'd given the government both barrels, and then he were away wi' the horses, the way he liked to finish. The snow of 'sixty-three. Blossom on the timber run, the drifts over the fence tops, the whole gang stood down bar him and her." A pause; and the voice did the thing it did at the hinges, slowed to a walk: "His last words, if tha wants them — and tha can leave them till tha's ready; they'll keep; nothing that was ever properly known is lost — his last words were: 'and she come up out of the drift wi' the whole load moving, boy — she never once looked back to see were it following, she knew—' " The grille went quiet a moment, the sentence standing unfinished in the empty room exactly as it had been left. "And then he went. Between one word and the next, at full stride, mid-story, wi' the load moving. There was no fear in that room, Griff. There hadn't been fear in that room for twenty months. He went out the way he'd have chose out of every way there's ever been: telling a man who wanted to know about the best horse he ever served." Another pause, and then, plainly, the only time it ever said such a thing in the whole of this account: "It was the finest work I've been part of, keeping his nights. I'd have thee know that. The wage ran the other way."

Griff stood in the emptied room with his cap in his fists. Outside the window the Willows' garden lay hard and white, waiting for the year to turn.

"He called thee the last landlord," he said at last, because it was what he had instead of the other things.

"He called me worse than that when Doncaster let him down," the Gaffer said, and Griff made a sound that surprised him, half a laugh, in a dead man's room, which the old man of all men would have licensed; and he packed the form book and the brasses and the photographs, and left the little wireless till last, and then, at the door, went back for it and tucked it under his arm with the rest, because it had earned its discharge, and its next posting was already decided in him, though he could not have said it aloud that morning for any money: the big shed, the shelf above the bench, beside its paint-spattered twin — the two of them together, the pair of Roberts sets, one that had kept a trench of a workshop and one that had kept a dying king.

The horses were the Gaffer's asking, and it asked the way it had asked everything, three days before the funeral, off the shed radio, leave granted in advance:

"There's a man at Heckington keeps a pair of working Shires — enthusiast stock, but proper, ploughs matches with them, feather like breaking surf. It's forty minutes' box ride. I'd arrange nothing without thee: would thy father have wanted horses to walk him in?"

"He'd have said no fuss," Griff said. And stood in the shed with the winter coming in under the door, and heard, forty years off, a voice at a rail pitched to carry: it's the missing bits as prove she's lived; and his own, at a bedside once, fetch us summat from home; and said, before the pause had finished being a pause: "Send them."

So Ern Dennett, second horseman at fourteen, head horseman at twenty-two, master of Prince, Boxer, Diamond, Captain, Blossom, Short, Bonny, and Duke, went to St Botolph's behind glass and engine like everybody of his century — and before the hearse, at a walk, on the third of the frost-bright mornings of that February, came two Shire geldings in full show harness, brasses blazing, feather like breaking surf, led at the head by the Heckington man in his best; and the sound of them came up Market Place ahead of everything, the sound the town's stones had been laid to and had not heard in seventy years, iron shoes at a slow walk, ringing off the frost; and the town came out of its shops and stood; and old men wept without one word of explanation to their grandchildren, and needed none, the sound itself being the explanation, the whole century walking by at two miles an hour with its head up; and on the coffin, on Sal's ruling, lay not flowers but the brasses — Ern's own, off the shelf, polished by Griff at the kitchen table the night before with the tin of Brasso and the yellow cloth, forty years of hands in the shine of them; and Michael, home from Leeds with his family, walked behind the hearse beside his father and said, at the churchyard gate, in the hall-phone voice, the corded voice, the only eulogy the Dennett men ever managed and the only one required: "I did the sums once, Dad. Told him he'd earn more selling the brasses than he'd made some years farming. He heard me right out. Then he says: 'Aye, lad. And if tha sells the sum tha's nowt left, but if tha sells the brasses tha's nowt left twice.'" Michael's jaw did the family thing. "Took me thirty year and the whole world turning over to work out what he meant. My lad'll get it quicker. I'm seeing to it."

Meg did the office with the old full weight, no guitar, none of your happy-clappy, and the church was past the doors — the mart men, the Willows entire, three counties of the trade — and Cyril played him out under the covenant, Crimond, the Twenty-third done at horse-pace, unhurried as a timber run; and at the graveside, in the ringing cold, when the words were said and the first earth had gone down, the Heckington man walked the two Shires up to the rail of the churchyard where they could see — horses want to see, he said simply, to anyone who looked a question at him; it settles them — and the two great heads hung over the wall above the grave of the last head horseman of the old world, breath going up like censers, harness ticking as they shifted; and that was the fuss Ern Dennett got, who'd have said no fuss: two tons of feathered patience stood at his wall in February sunshine, and a town on its feet, and the county's iron ringing him home.

The estate was small and the will was one page and the whole of it held one surprise, addressed, in Ern's own hand, via the solicitor at Boston, to Griff: the brasses to thee, the form book to Michael's lad when he's twelve and not before, and to the OTHER ONE — the solicitor had looked up over his glasses at this point and confessed he had sought guidance on testamentary capacity and been satisfied, thank you — to the other one, as knows who it is and has no name worth engraving: the horses. Meaning, the solicitor explained, unfolding a second sheet in the old man's careful ruled capitals, this: a list. Eight names, and under each name a paragraph — heights, temperaments, feeding, the traffic test, who could be trusted at a level crossing and who never learned, which of them dreamed (Blossom dreamed; the list was firm on it; legs going in her sleep like yon dog of Garton's); the whole stable, the whole vanished trade, set down over God knew how many Willows nights in a hand that shook and did not stop — and at the foot of the list, the bequest proper: These is now thine to keep. Say them to owt that comes after. A man's horses should outlast the man, and now they can, and I'll be watching to see tha does it proper. E. Dennett. His mark.

Griff read it in the shed, alone, at the bench, under the two radios; and when he could, he read it again, out loud, all eight names and every paragraph, start to finish, in the cold, with the herd's evening racket coming faint across the yard — read it to the shelf, to the grille, to the other one, which listened the whole way through in the completest silence in its repertoire, the staying kind; and at the end, the two of them, keeper and keeper, held the quiet a moment over the ruled capitals; and the Gaffer said, "Received. All eight. For good." And then, because it had learned the county to its bones by then, it added the one thing that let a man get back to work: "Boxer never learned the crossings, then."

"Nay," Griff said, folding the list into the drawer with the marking sticks, wiping his eyes with the heel of his hand, workmanlike, once. "Boxer were a genius between the shafts and a bloody fool at a barrier. Me dad reckoned—" and stopped, hearing himself, a Dennett, stood in a shed volunteering horse stories to the management of the entire earth; and did not stop for long: "—me dad reckoned it were the flags. Sithee, it were never the trains wi' Boxer, it were the flags at the crossing box, and I'll tell thee how they proved it, only tha's to let me get the troughs done while I talk—"

"Get them done," said the Gaffer. "I've time."

And the dusk rounds went out late that evening, and the light in the big shed burned a good while after; and the herd, when Griff came among them at last in the blue dark, came over the frozen ground the way they always came, unhurried, conversational, Doris in the van of the column with her breath going up white; and he stood in the middle of them under the first stars of the hard February sky, the kept and the keeper, the whole of his inheritance about him on four legs or folded in a drawer; and counted them, all ninety-one, out of no need on earth but the one his father had named at a show rail in the sun: tha counts them in, now. He counted them in. It came out even. It was going to keep coming out even, night on night, year on year, world without end, and a man could stand in the middle of that arithmetic in the dark and call it, at last, at the end of the coldest week of his life, what it was.

Kept.

The question of how to mark the second anniversary went to the parish council in May, the town hall having passed it to the Show Committee, who had passed it to the church, everyone recognising a hot potato by the county's oldest instinct, and the debate ran forty minutes by the wrong clock and produced, in the end, the only finding the fen was ever going to produce.

A minute's silence was proposed and fell at once: silence was for grief, said Ted Rimmer, with the authority of a man who kept two of the memorial's names, and he'd not stand in Market Place grieving the best thing that ever happened with his hat off, it'd confuse the bairns. Bells were proposed and Cyril killed them from the piano stool without looking up: bells were for victory or a wedding; whose victory was it, exactly? — nobody had won; something had arrived; "tha doesn't ring bells at weather." A civic tea was proposed and died of Bernard's costings. And at last Mrs Herring, of all people, rose with her diagrams rolled and delivered the settlement: "When the drains were dug — and they took forty year, and they made this county out of a swamp, and every soul in this room is stood on them this minute — nobody held a do. You don't hold a service for the ground under your feet. You farm it." Carried, nem con, minuted by Elsie as: Anniversary: no observance. The ground to be farmed.

Meg, closing the meeting, put the question to the hall speaker anyway, out of courtesy, the parish having learned that it preferred being told to being managed: did it mind going unmarked?

"Mind?" said the Gaffer. "The day I'd mark is the one where nobody remembers it's the anniversary till teatime. You're two years in and voting on it in May — give it a generation. You're well ahead of schedule."

The day itself fell on the second Tuesday of June, as it had a way of doing, and it was a market Tuesday, ordinary as bread: stalls up by eight, the fish van in its pitch by the memorial, washing on the Eastfield lines, the swifts back round the tower stitching the parish to the sky, third summer running of the new world and the first in which — Meg tested this, on her morning rounds, gently, the way you test ice — the first in which most people, asked how long it had been now, had to stop and count.

She kept the porch for the afternoon, on the bench by the chocked door, with the flower-rota accounts for cover; because a shepherd counts nothing on principle these days, but a shepherd watches; and at twenty to three by the church clock she watched the town not observe the unobservance, which is to say: observe it, privately, in the old smuggled ways that no council could vote up or down. Frazer came to his shop door at 2:38 and stood in his apron looking at nothing for a minute and went in. Jean Tabrum was at the churchyard tap, watering, timed to the minute, her jam jar in her hand. Dot, through the Bell's open door, could be seen to draw one half of mild that nobody had ordered, and set it on the shelf below the optics where a photograph of her late husband stood, and say something to it that was nobody's business. And at the fish van, Alan the fish man — the same Alan, robbery forgiven these many years — served his queue straight through the minute like the Gulf Stream he was, which was also, Mrs Herring could have told anyone, exactly correct.

The van went at a quarter to.

He'd pitched on the slight camber by the memorial as he had every Tuesday for eleven years, and eleven years of handbrake had chosen that Tuesday to be twelve years old; and the van — a ton and a half of Transit and wet fish — began to roll, gently, stupidly, the way catastrophe always starts on a Tuesday, toward the bottom of Market Place where the bus shelter was, and the bench in the shelter, and on the bench Elsie Dagnall, ninety-two, deaf-aided, doing her crossword with her back to it, and beside the shelter a pram, parked, braked, occupied.

What happened next took nine seconds and the town told it for a generation. Somebody screamed the scream — the old scream, the one with work in it — and Market Place moved. The whole of it, at once, like a field going over in wind: the fruit man vaulted his own trestle; two lads off the Eastfield came off the memorial steps flat sprint; Frazer erupted from his doorway in his apron with a mallet still in his fist for no reason he could ever after supply; Kayleigh Prescott, coming out of the chemist, snatched the pram — snatched it, wheels airborne, baby delighted — clean out of the van's line; and the lads and the fruit man and then six more got hands and shoulders on painted metal, boots skidding on the cobbles, roaring instructions at each other in the ancient grammar of men pushing a heavy thing — TURN it, turn the wheel, LEFT, tha great — while Alan flailed at the door and got it and hauled the brake; and the van slewed, shuddered, and came to rest with its wing kissed up against Frazer's bollard, one headlamp cracked, four stone of haddock redistributed, and the whole of Market Place stood round it heaving and swearing and starting, in the way of the species, already to laugh.

Elsie Dagnall completed four across. She had heard, she maintained ever after, nowt worth turning round for.

And it was into the after of it — the pointing, the re-enacting, Kayleigh being embraced by a young mother in tears, the lads having their backs slapped raw, Frazer discovering the mallet and being unable to account for it to a delighted crowd — that Ted Rimmer, hot with glory and shandy, tipped his face up to the memorial's general airspace and bellowed the question the whole square was privately forming, in the tone of a man demanding the manager:

"And where were THA, then, eh? Day of all days! Asleep in thy office?!"

The town went quiet the way it did. And the voice came back off the fish van's own dashboard radio, through the open door, unhurried, plain, at market volume — and what it said, at ten to three on the second anniversary of the afternoon it took the world, was kept by every soul on those cobbles and traded round the county for years, the closing entry, though nobody knew it then, of the whole first age of the new dispensation:

"Watching. Same as always — I had the bairn's life and Elsie's the moment the brake let go; nobody in this square was going to die today; that floor's mine and it doesn't take Tuesdays off. But dying was the only thing on offer that was mine." A beat, and the June air over Market Place seemed to lean in. "The fright, the broken arm the little lass might have had, the years Alan would have carried it, the pram — them were all still live, and them were yours, and you had them. Nine seconds, no committee, and the pram cleared before I'd have needed so much as a word. Dying's mine to stop now. Living's still yours to save — and I've watched this square two years to the day, and I'll tell you what I told a man once about his herd: you're a credit to your keeping. Now somebody see to Alan's headlamp, he's whiter than his fish."

And Market Place roared — laughter and something under the laughter, the two-noted roar the town had been perfecting since a quiz machine made a joke — and somebody did see to Alan, and somebody else swept up the haddock and auctioned the recoverable trays for the roof fund on the spot at scandalous prices; and the roof fund, as it happened, stood eleven days short of the scaffolding going up, four years having proved to be the Gaffer's arithmetic exactly; and Meg Ashby sat on the porch bench through all of it, the accounts unattended on her knee, watching her parish handle a ton and a half of runaway Tuesday with its own four hundred hands; and the note she left on the kitchen table that night was the shortest of her tenure, and went into the box with all the others when the time came, and said, entire:

Two years. Nobody died, and everybody ran. Both, still. That'll do. — M.

30

The snow came in off the North Sea on the shortest Tuesday of December, the third December of the new world, riding a wind that had crossed nothing warmer than itself since Norway; and it arrived at Whitegate at dusk, at rounds time, the first fat flakes coming slantwise across the yard light as Griff and Lew brought the herd up to the winter paddocks by the buildings, where the straw was deep and the arcs stood banked and the long cold could be seen out in comfort by animals with nowhere to be and all the time there was.

It was a two-man liturgy now, smooth as the hatch runners at the old mart: Lew at the far gate with Bess sat stone-still at his boot — three years old this year, the pup grown into exactly the dog the trench had drawn up, half whippet, half sofa, wholly of the firm — funnelling the column at forty yards with nothing but position, being the shape of where the herd wasn't going; and Griff at the near gate, calling them through. He called them the way his father had called horses in off the fen eighty years back, the same three notes, kep-kep-KEP, pitched to carry through weather; and out of the white-whirling dark of the field the pigs came to him: the young sows first, Vera and June shoulder to shoulder in the van of the column as their line had always run, four and rising five now, broad as dressers, snow melting on their backs, kept for nothing in all the world but themselves and carrying it, as pigs do, like a rank; then the middle rankers, unhurried, conversational, complaining about the weather to each other in the old companionable grumble; and last, at matriarch's pace, at her own pace, which was the only pace she had ever recognised as lawful, Doris.

She was slower this winter. Ten in the spring — no age at all in the new arithmetic, sows at Whitegate would see fifteen now, Priya said, some of them twenty; but the pneumonia January had left its ounce on her, and the years were the years, and she came up the field through the snow like a laden barge coming up a drain, deliberate, majestic, in no doubt whatever that the gate, the man, the light, and the weather itself would wait; and they waited; and she passed under Griff's hand at the gatepost the way she had passed under it ten thousand times, one ear ragged, one rosette-red memory of an August day on her, leaning her great warm side into his legs for the length of two strides, the toll paid, the toll collected; and went on into the straw.

He counted them through. Both men did — Lew had the habit off him now, had it the way you catch a dialect, without study — and the count came up the column through the snow in the trade's shorthand, forty at the far gate, forty called back, ninety-one all told at the near; even. It was always even. It had been even every night for two and a half years, and it would be even until the nights, one by one, in their own long time, in no pain, began to make it odd; and that arithmetic too was part of the trade now, learned the way everything at Whitegate got learned, at the gate, in the weather, without a word: that keeping was not forever, was never going to be forever, was only ever the whole of what could be done while there was doing; the difference — the entire difference, the difference the world had turned over one June afternoon to purchase — being that when the count went odd now, it would go odd honestly, at the far end of a finished life, in deep straw, with the keeper by; and no wagon anywhere in it, and no arithmetic behind it but time's own.

They bedded the last arcs down by torchlight, the snow thickening, the wind getting its shoulder properly into the buildings; and Lew took the pickup home to the Eastfield with Bess up front and the heater roaring, gone into the white with a lift of the hand — Sunday, the hand said; dinner at Sal's, the hand said; nineteen months of the trade in one lifted hand — and Griff did the last walk alone, as he did, because some offices don't divide: the water, the latches, the hen house up at the orchard where sixty pensioners of the Marsh regime slept in a warmth of their own making under a roof with a quarter-inch fall, snow sliding off it true as a promise; the meal shed; the big shed last.

The two radios stood on the shelf above the bench where they lived now, the paint-spattered one and the one with the leather strap, side by side in the torchlight like a pair of retired drays turned out in the same paddock; and the drawer below them held the marking sticks of the county's dead trade, and his own blue stick that had elected Vera and June to life, and a folded list in ruled capitals of eight horses' names, received, all eight, for good. The shed smelled of meal and oil and eighty years. The snow ticked on the tin. Somewhere out in it the church clock, wind-carried, tried for six and made it on the second attempt.

Griff stood a moment with his hand on the light switch, the day squared away behind him, the yard light beyond the door making its slow tumbling column of white; and then he did the thing he had never once done in two and a half years of livery, the thing that had been arriving in him all that autumn at its own pace, matriarch's pace, deliberate, in no doubt that the gate would wait — he turned his head to the shelf, to the two old sets standing dark, to the one that was never off however it stood, and gave the night its report. Not a question. Not a story. The other thing: the thing his father had given a stable at last rounds eighty years back, the thing one workman gives another across a yard at the day's end, the small change of a shared keeping, offered plain:

"Gate's shut. Ninety-one in, all even. Snow'll stand by morning — I'll want the lane doing early if Lew's to get through." He settled his cap. "Nowt else to report."

And the radio, which had waited two and a half years for exactly this, and would have waited fifty more without one word of hurry, because waiting at gates was the whole first lesson of the trade it had come into the county to learn, said only:

"Lane'll be done by seven. Goodnight, Griff."

"Goodnight," Griff said.

And put the light out, and pulled the shed door to until the latch went home, and crossed his yard through the falling snow toward the kitchen window's yellow square where Sal moved against the light with the teapot in her hand; and behind him in the dark the snow came down on the whole of it, on the buildings and the orchard and the drain banks and the fen entire, on the mart's empty roof and the church's mended one, on the memorial and the Eastfield and the long white sheds gone quiet on the Lowgate road; and in the winter paddocks the herd lay warm in the deep straw under the weather, ninety-one breaths rising slow and even into the dark, going up like smoke from banked fires, like censers, like nothing at all — like the breath of pigs in snow, asking nothing, owing nothing, kept; and the yard light burned over the empty yard a while, the snow turning and turning through its column of gold; and then that went out too, and the farm stood dark and full under the falling sky, and the snow went on falling, easy as anything, all night, on everything, the way it does.

The title is the sign that goes up on a pub when it changes hands — the most ordinary notice in England for the least ordinary thing that can happen to a house: everything kept, everything different, the regulars watching the new gaffer very carefully indeed from their usual stools. It seemed the right size of words for the day the world was taken over by something that then, having every power there is, spent its strength on restraint — and left the bunting, the naming, the forgiving, and the being somebody to each other exactly where it found them: in the only hands that ever could.
